# Style DNA

## 1) Kurzprofil (1 Absatz)

Close third-person limited narration in past tense with high psychic proximity to the POV character (predominantly Jason, with strategic POV shifts to antagonists/allies for dramatic irony). The prose is action-forward, emotionally restrained yet deeply felt — characters reveal inner states through physical micro-reactions and clipped dialogue rather than explicit emotional exposition. Sentence rhythm alternates sharply between ultra-short punch fragments ("Gone." / "Silence.") and flowing mid-length sentences, creating a cinematic, breath-controlled pacing. Internal thought (italicized) and RAE-dialogue run as a constant second track beneath the external narrative. The tone balances genuine tension with dry, understated humor. Recurring structural motifs — the color-scale system (Green/Yellow/Orange/Red), the closing refrain "One X at a time," and the word "Together" — provide cohesion across chapters. The style prioritizes clarity, immediacy, and earned emotional weight over literary ornamentation.

## 2) Perspektive, Nähe, Tempus

- **POV:** Third-person limited, predominantly anchored in Jason. Chapters shift POV for strategic purposes (Malvek in Ch. 03, Reeves in Ch. 16, Lina in Ch. 05/11, Milo in Ch. 13 opening). In the final two chapters (Part 3, Ch. 13–14), the narration shifts to first-person plural ("we") as Jason and RAE merge — a deliberate structural break signaling transformation.
- **Psychic Distance:** Very close. The reader is inside Jason's sensory experience — headaches, nosebleeds, the taste of copper, the weight of exhaustion. Thoughts are rendered directly, often mid-action, without attribution tags ("His stomach dropped." not "He felt his stomach drop").
- **Tempus:** Simple past tense throughout. No present-tense narration. Occasional present tense in dialogue and internal thought (natural conversational register). The past tense anchors the narrative in a grounded, reportorial tone that contrasts with the fantastical subject matter.
- **POV Shift Rules:** When shifting to a non-Jason POV, the new perspective is introduced via scene break (***) and a clear physical/spatial anchor (Malvek at his desk, Reeves in the sedan). These shifts are always external — we see Jason from outside, gaining dramatic irony.

## 3) Ton & Stimmung (Mood, Spannung, Humorgrad)

- **Baseline Mood:** Shifts across the arc. **Part 1:** Quiet dissatisfaction and loneliness that gradually opens into wonder and cautious hope. Jason exists in a small, flat life — mundane routine, emotional suppression, the resigned acceptance of being "not special." The mood is introspective and liminal: something is missing, then something arrives, and the dominant feeling becomes curiosity laced with self-doubt. External threat enters only in the final third (surveillance marks, the storm-scan, the figure in the rain), transforming the mood from inward searching to outward vigilance. **Parts 2–3:** Tense vigilance. Characters operate under constant low-level threat. Even safe moments carry awareness of surveillance, deadlines, or physical cost. The baseline established in Part 1 — that quiet, precarious openness — never fully disappears; it resurfaces in private moments (Jason alone on a bench, Lina cooking after a crisis) as a reminder of what's at stake.
- **Tension Mechanics:** The tension toolbox expands across parts. **Part 1:** Tension is primarily internal and philosophical — (1) identity crisis (what if everything I accepted about myself is wrong?), (2) trust calibration (can I trust RAE? Milo? my own perceptions?), (3) slow escalation from wonder to threat as the external world begins to notice Jason, and (4) the fear of possibility itself, which is more frightening than any monster. **Parts 2–3:** Tension shifts outward and becomes structural — (1) countdown structures (two-week deadlines, Malvek's countdown "Ten… Nine… Eight…"), (2) physical cost escalation (Green → Yellow → Orange → Red), (3) information asymmetry (what do they know? what do *we* know about what they know?), and (4) interpersonal stakes layered atop external threats. **Across all parts:** Consent and autonomy serve as a persistent tension undercurrent — RAE's "choice is sacred" / "do not overwrite" principle means the protagonist's agency is always the live wire, and any moment that tests that boundary carries maximum charge.
- **Humor:** Dry, understated, dialogue-driven. Functions as pressure relief between high-tension beats. Typically clipped one-liners that reveal character: "Language," Milo muttered, though he was grinning. / "That's called strategy, not overthinking." / "You're either very brave or very stupid." — "Can't it be both?" Humor never breaks the reality of danger; it coexists with it.
- **Emotional Register:** Feelings are earned, not declared. Characters express vulnerability through action (Lina's eyes going red-rimmed, Milo staring at the ceiling) rather than internal monologue. When emotions surface explicitly, they land harder because of their scarcity.

## 4) Satzbau & Rhythmus (Tempo, Varianz, Absatzschnitt)

- **Sentence Length Variance:** Extremely high. The text oscillates between 1–3 word fragments ("Gone." / "Silence." / "Not a question.") and flowing sentences of 20–30 words. This alternation is the primary pacing tool.
- **Action Sequences:** Short sentences. Short paragraphs (often 1–2 sentences). Rapid paragraph turn. Minimal attribution. The prose accelerates by stripping away connective tissue.
- **Reflective/Dialogue Passages:** Longer sentences. More complex syntax. Mid-length paragraphs (3–5 sentences). The prose breathes, slows, allows space for character interiority.
- **Paragraph Breaks as Beats:** Single-line paragraphs serve as emphasis or pacing beats — not decorative, but rhythmic. A one-sentence paragraph after a longer block functions like a drum hit.
- **Scene Breaks:** Triple asterisks (***) used consistently to separate time jumps, POV shifts, or tonal transitions within chapters. High frequency — typically 3–6 per chapter. These create a segmented, episodic feel within individual chapters.
- **Staccato Clusters:** Sequences of ultra-short paragraphs in rapid succession (especially during crisis moments) create a breathless, falling-domino effect:
  > "His body went limp."  
  > "'Jason!' Lina's voice."  
  > "Blood dripping from his nose."

## 5) Wortwahl & Register (Diktion, Fachbegriffe, Wiederholungsmuster)

- **Register:** Contemporary, accessible, mid-register English. Not literary-elevated, not casual-slangy. Clean prose that reads fast.
- **Technical Vocabulary:** A custom lexicon of resonance-world terms — *carrier, harmonic, integration, invest/Invest, coupling, dampening, shaping, perception field, resonogram, baseline frequency, containment, suppression fields*. These terms are introduced organically through action and dialogue, never info-dumped.
- **Emotional Vocabulary:** Restrained and precise. Physical sensations replace abstract emotion words: "His throat tightened" instead of "He felt sad." "His stomach dropped" instead of "He was afraid." Body-as-barometer.
- **Repetition Patterns:** Deliberate echo-repetition within and across chapters:
  - "One [X] at a time" as a closing refrain (appears in 15+ chapters).
  - "Together" as thematic anchor word.
  - "Red stops. No exceptions." → carried as a motif.
  - "Brave or stupid" / "Can't it be both?" — recurring character callbacks.
  - Color-scale language (Green/Yellow/Orange/Red) embedded throughout as ongoing status vocabulary.
- **Favorite Constructions:** "Not [A]. Just [B]." / "Not [A] — [B]." pattern for correction/clarification. "Something that might have been [emotion]" for ambiguous emotional reads. "Which is exactly why…" for character logic chains.

## 6) Dialogstil (Stimmen, Tags, Untertext, Taktung)

- **Tags:** Minimal. Predominantly "said" / "asked." Rarely adverbial tags ("said quietly" appears, but "exclaimed furiously" never does). Tags are frequently replaced by action beats: "Lina set down her fork. 'I forgot how much I missed this.'"
- **Voice Differentiation:**
  - **Jason:** Direct, slightly self-deprecating, honest. Shorter sentences. Asks questions.
  - **Lina:** Protective, assertive, occasionally sharp-humored. Commands. Uses imperative constructions.
  - **Milo:** Analytical, prone to tangents, nervous humor. Longer explanatory dialogue. Technical precision.
  - **Elyra:** Blunt, authoritative, bitter-wise. Short declarations. Teacher cadence — command followed by explanation.
  - **RAE:** Slightly formal, precise, occasionally warm. Shorter sentences than humans. Analytical but developing emotional register.
  - **Malvek/Reeves:** Professional, measured, deliberately neutral. Institutional vocabulary. Power expressed through controlled calm.
- **Subtext:** Dialogue consistently says less than what's meant. Characters understand each other through context, shared history, and tone rather than explicit statements. "Tomorrow" carries weight far beyond the word.
- **Rhythm/Taktung:** Rapid-fire exchanges (2–5 word volleys) alternate with longer monologue/explanation blocks. Comic relief comes from interruption patterns and deadpan callbacks.

## 7) Gedanken & Innenleben (wie/wo oft, Form, Länge)

- **Form:** Italicized text for internal thought and RAE-communication. No quotation marks. Presented as direct thought (first person) — *Can you blame her?* / *Then we don't fail.*
- **Frequency:** Very high. Internal thought appears on nearly every page. RAE-dialogue functions as a constant second voice, creating a dual-track narration.
- **Length:** Typically 1–3 sentences per internal exchange. Rarely extends beyond 4–5 lines. Quick, punchy, functional. Extended internal monologue is avoided.
- **Embedding:** Internal thought is woven seamlessly between action and dialogue — often mid-scene, mid-action. The transition between external narration and internal thought is unmarked except by italics, creating fluid immersion.
- **Function:** Internal thought serves three purposes: (1) RAE-dialogue providing tactical/analytical commentary, (2) Jason's emotional processing (usually brief, honest), (3) dramatic irony when the reader knows Jason's internal state but other characters don't.
- **Evolution:** In the final chapters, as integration deepens, internal thought and external narration begin to blur. By Chapter 13–14, the "we" perspective eliminates the distinction entirely — thoughts are no longer marked as internal because all narration has become first-person plural.

## 8) Action/Bewegung/Spannung (Mechaniken, Beats, Klarheit)

- **Camera:** Close tracking shot. The narrative follows Jason's sensory experience in real-time. During action, the "camera" pulls in tighter — fewer peripheral details, more immediate sensation (blood taste, hand tremor, headache spike).
- **Action Beats:** Physical cost is always present. Every use of power has visible, quantifiable consequences (nosebleed, headache escalation, exhaustion). This grounds the fantastical in physical reality.
- **Clarity:** Action choreography is spatially precise. Distances measured ("Five meters. Seven meters. Ten meters."). Positions tracked. The reader always knows where characters are in relation to each other and to threats.
- **Punchiness:** Action sequences use sentence fragmentation and rapid paragraph breaks. Attribution drops away. The prose becomes almost screenplay-like:
  > "Kade moved."  
  > "Jason barely saw it."  
  > "He hit the ground."
- **Tension Escalation:** The color-scale system (Green → Yellow → Orange → Red) provides a built-in tension meter that the reader learns to track. When "Orange" appears, we know "Red" (catastrophe) is one step away.
- **Countdown as Device:** Numbered countdowns used in climactic scenes (Malvek's "Ten… Nine… Eight…" at the RP-0 confrontation) create external time pressure that amplifies internal stakes.

## 9) Beschreibung vs. Handlung (Detaildichte, Sinneskanäle, Metaphern)

- **Balance:** Heavily action/dialogue-weighted. Description serves function — establishing spatial relationships, conveying atmosphere, or revealing character perception. No passages of pure description without narrative purpose.
- **Sensory Channels:** Tactile and kinaesthetic dominate (cold stone, warm food, shaking hands, headache pressure, blood taste). Auditory second (echoing footsteps, teapot hum, resonance vibration). Visual is present but functional, not painterly. Olfactory used sparingly for atmosphere (rust, antiseptic, espresso, old machine oil).
- **Detail Density:** Low-to-medium. Setting descriptions are 1–3 sentences maximum. The prose trusts the reader to fill in the picture from key details rather than cataloguing everything. Selective, telling details over comprehensive description.
- **Metaphor/Simile:** Rare and concrete when used. Functional comparison rather than poetic ornamentation: "Like holding a thread someone kept tugging." / "Like a conversation." / "Resonance like a broken mirror reflecting only pieces of light." Metaphors drawn from the resonance-world vernacular rather than traditional literary imagery.
- **Setting as Character:** Environments reflect emotional states through functional detail — the sterile hospital, the cold warehouse, the anonymous hotel, the expensive attorney's office. Architecture and atmosphere telegraph tone without explicit commentary.

## 10) Typische Stil-Signaturen (3–8 Bulletpoints)

- **"One [X] at a time" closing motif**: Nearly every chapter ends with a variation of this refrain — "One precise step at a time," "One careful conversation at a time," "One containment crisis at a time." Provides rhythmic closure and thematic continuity.
- **Color-scale status system** (Green/Yellow/Orange/Red): Functions as both in-world mechanic and narrative tension gauge. Referenced throughout as shorthand for danger level. The reader becomes conditioned to respond to these terms.
- **Dual-track narration** (external prose + italicized internal/RAE dialogue): Creates a constant layered experience — the reader simultaneously follows what happens and what Jason/RAE think about it.
- **Physical cost as authenticity anchor**: Every power use extracts measurable, visible cost (headaches, nosebleeds, exhaustion, cognitive impairment). This prevents power-fantasy inflation and keeps stakes grounded.
- **"Together" as thematic keyword**: Recurs across all chapters as the core value — partnership over isolation, cooperation over domination, trust over control.
- **Brave or stupid / "Can't it be both?"**: Recurring callback that crystallizes Jason's character — self-aware risk-taking, humor under pressure, refusal to choose between opposites.
- **Triple-asterisk scene segmentation**: High-frequency use of *** breaks creates an episodic, cinematic rhythm within chapters — each segment almost a mini-scene with its own arc.
- **Perspective shift to first-person plural ("we")**: The most distinctive stylistic signature — the narration itself transforms as characters transform, making integration thematically visible at the sentence level.

## 11) Do/Don't Regeln (mind. 10 Do + 10 Don't)

### DO:

1. **DO use physical micro-reactions to convey emotion** — throat tightening, jaw clenching, hand tremor, stomach dropping. Readers should feel emotions through the body, not through labels.
2. **DO maintain the dual-track narration** — external action + italicized internal thought/RAE-dialogue should coexist on nearly every page. The internal voice is never absent for more than half a page.
3. **DO vary sentence length dramatically** — alternate between 1–3 word fragments and 15–25 word sentences within the same paragraph. Use fragment clusters for emphasis and acceleration; longer sentences for reflection and breathing room.
4. **DO use action beats instead of dialogue tags** — "Lina set down her fork. 'That's enough.'" instead of "'That's enough,' Lina said firmly."
5. **DO quantify and ground fantastical elements** — distances in meters, durations in minutes, integration in percentages, danger levels by color code. Abstract power should always have measurable, concrete costs.
6. **DO end chapters with a forward-looking refrain** — use the "One [X] at a time" pattern or a variation that echoes the chapter's theme while pointing toward the next challenge.
7. **DO use scene breaks (***) liberally** — 3–6 per chapter. Each segment should function as a mini-scene with a clear purpose, entry point, and exit beat.
8. **DO let humor coexist with danger** — dry one-liners, deadpan callbacks, and self-deprecating observations should appear even in high-tension passages. Characters who joke under pressure feel more real.
9. **DO differentiate character voices through syntax** — Elyra speaks in short commands. Milo explains in longer analytical chains. Lina uses imperatives. RAE is precise and slightly formal. Voice should be identifiable without attribution.
10. **DO show institutional power through controlled calm** — antagonists (Malvek, Reeves) express authority not through shouting but through measured tone, professional vocabulary, and deliberate pacing. Their restraint is what makes them threatening.
11. **DO use selective sensory detail for atmosphere** — one or two precisely chosen sensory anchors per scene (the smell of rust, the cold of stone, the hum of equipment) outperform comprehensive scenic description.
12. **DO track physical consequences across scenes** — if Jason hits Orange in one scene, the headache should persist into the next. Recovery takes time. The body has memory.

### DON'T:

1. **DON'T use adverbial dialogue tags** — no "said angrily," "whispered seductively," "exclaimed triumphantly." If the emotion isn't clear from the dialogue and context, the dialogue is underwritten, not the tag.
2. **DON'T write extended descriptive passages** — setting description should never exceed 3 sentences without returning to action, dialogue, or thought. The reader's eye must stay in motion.
3. **DON'T explain emotions explicitly** — never write "He felt afraid" when "His hands shook" is available. Trust the reader to infer emotional states from physical signals and context.
4. **DON'T let power use be consequence-free** — every shaping, perception extension, or carrier connection must cost something visible. If there is no cost shown, the scene loses tension.
5. **DON'T write internal monologue longer than 4–5 lines** — RAE-dialogue and Jason's thoughts should be quick exchanges. Extended philosophical inner monologue breaks the pace.
6. **DON'T use elaborate metaphors or poetic register** — comparisons should be concrete, functional, and brief. "Like holding a thread that someone kept tugging" — not "like a gossamer filament spun by ancient weavers across the loom of consciousness."
7. **DON'T break the color-scale system** — Green, Yellow, Orange, Red is sacred vocabulary. Don't substitute other terms. Don't use the colors for non-danger contexts.
8. **DON'T let chapters end without a hook or refrain** — every chapter ending needs either a forward pull (revelation, question, threat) or a thematic echo (the "One X at a time" motif, "Together," etc.).
9. **DON'T summarize what just happened** — no "He reflected on the events of the day" recaps. If the reader was there, they don't need to be told again. Forward motion, always.
10. **DON'T make antagonists caricatures** — Malvek, Reeves, and institutional forces must have legitimate reasons for their actions. Present authority as competent, rational, and operating within a framework they believe is justifiable.
11. **DON'T overuse exclamation marks** — they appear rarely and only in genuine crisis moments (dialogue during physical danger). Standard dialogue and narration stay calm.
12. **DON'T drop the dual-track narration** — even in non-Jason POV chapters, maintain an internal thought layer. Characters always think alongside acting.

## 12) Style-Checklist (10–20 abhakenbare Checks)

- [ ] Does the chapter open with immediate action, dialogue, or sensory grounding (not with summary or backstory)?
- [ ] Are sentences varied in length — short fragments mixed with flowing mid-length sentences within each page?
- [ ] Is every emotion conveyed through physical reaction or behavior, not through explicit feeling-labels?
- [ ] Does the dual-track narration (external + italicized internal/RAE) appear within the first page?
- [ ] Are dialogue tags minimal — predominantly "said"/"asked" or replaced by action beats?
- [ ] Can each character's voice be identified without reading the attribution tag?
- [ ] Does every use of resonance/power have a visible, quantifiable physical cost?
- [ ] Are scene breaks (***) used to segment time jumps, POV shifts, or tonal transitions?
- [ ] Is setting description kept to ≤3 sentences per establishing moment, focused on 1–2 sensory anchors?
- [ ] Does the chapter contain at least one moment of dry humor or understated levity?
- [ ] Do action sequences use short paragraphs (1–2 sentences) and reduced attribution?
- [ ] Is the color-scale system (Green/Yellow/Orange/Red) referenced or implied if danger levels fluctuate?
- [ ] Does internal thought stay under 4–5 lines per exchange?
- [ ] Does the chapter end with a forward-pointing hook or a thematic refrain ("One X at a time" / "Together")?
- [ ] Are antagonists/institutional figures presented as competent and rational, not as caricatures?
- [ ] Is the physical state of characters tracked consistently across scenes (lingering headache, fatigue, injury)?
- [ ] Are metaphors/similes concrete, functional, and brief — no poetic overreach?
- [ ] Does tension escalate through quantifiable mechanisms (deadlines, distance measurements, integration percentages, color-scale shifts)?

## 14) Mini-Beispiele (max 6, kurz, bevorzugt paraphrasiert)

**1. Punch-Fragment nach längerer Passage** (paraphrasiert):
> A long sentence describes the structure fighting against Jason's stabilization, physics and stress cascading through failing steel. Then — a one-word paragraph: "Gone." This rhythm-break creates emphasis through sudden brevity.

**2. Dual-Track Narration** (paraphrasiert):
> Jason hands over the document, outwardly calm. Simultaneously, italicized text delivers a quick RAE-exchange — *Can you trust him?* / *Not yet. But we don't have a choice.* — then the external narration resumes seamlessly.

**3. Emotion through Body** (Original, 1 Satz):
> "His throat tightened." — Used instead of "He felt emotional." The physical replaces the abstract.

**4. Dry Humor Under Pressure** (paraphrasiert):
> Character A says Jason is either very brave or very stupid. Jason pauses at the exit: "Can't it be both?" The humor functions as stress release and character signature.

**5. Chapter-End Refrain** (Beispiel im Stil / Synthetic):
> *One precise, careful step at a time.* — Typischer Kapitelausklang. Variiert das [X] je nach Kapitelthema: "One check-in at a time." / "One containment crisis at a time." / "One revelation at a time."

**6. Action-Sequence Rhythm** (Beispiel im Stil / Synthetic):
> "Kade moved. Jason's barrier shattered. He hit concrete. Tasted copper. The ceiling spun. Someone called his name — distant, underwater. Then: nothing." — Demonstrates the rapid-fire, stripped-down prose used during combat/crisis.
