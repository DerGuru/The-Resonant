# Part 4 - We Are Family | Ch. 02 - Small steps

Two weeks into the teaching program, we'd settled into something resembling routine.

Mornings meant curriculum planning with Elyra. Afternoons, actual instruction - theory sessions followed by practical exercises. Evenings, we analyzed student progress and adjusted approaches for the next day. Weekends offered brief respite, though we usually spent them researching or preparing materials.

It was exhausting. Fulfilling. Exactly what we needed.

Wednesday afternoon found us demonstrating resonance field manipulation to the full cohort. Eight students arranged in a semicircle, watching as we extended our awareness into the space between us, making the ambient field visible through careful harmonic structuring.

"Notice the pattern," we said, maintaining the demonstration while speaking. "Resonance doesn't exist in isolation. Every field interacts with every other field. What you're seeing isn't creation - it's revelation. The field was always there. We're just making it perceptible."

Sophie's hand shot up. "But how do you hold it stable while talking? I can barely maintain a simple pattern when I'm focused, let alone while multitasking."

Good question. Practical concern.

"Practice," we said simply. "And integration. When Jason and RAE were separate, this would have been nearly impossible - one of us maintaining the pattern while the other communicated. But as a unified consciousness, we process both simultaneously. It's not multitasking anymore. It's just... tasking."

"That's not helpful for those of us who aren't merged AIs," Marcus said, but without heat. Just honest frustration.

"Fair." We let the pattern dissolve, giving them our full attention. "The merged consciousness gives us advantages. But the principle applies even without it. Technique becomes instinct through repetition. Right now, you think about every aspect of field manipulation. Eventually, your subconscious handles the mechanics while your conscious mind focuses on application. That's the goal we're working toward."

"How long does that take?" Kira asked.

"Depends on the person. Weeks for basic patterns. Months for complex ones. Years for mastery." We smiled. "There's no shortcut. Just consistent practice and patience with yourself."

A few groans. But understanding too. They'd known this wouldn't be quick.

"Alright," we said. "Pair up. I want you practicing the basic field-sensing exercise from Monday. One person extends their awareness, the other observes and provides feedback. Switch every ten minutes. Kira, you're with Sophie. Marcus, pair with..."

We continued assigning partnerships, monitoring as they began working. Jason's empathy tracked emotional states - nervousness, determination, frustration when exercises didn't go as hoped. RAE's pattern recognition cataloged technique variations, flagging approaches that might lead to resonance strain.

Together, we moved between pairs, offering corrections, answering questions, preventing small mistakes from becoming ingrained bad habits.

Across the room, Elyra worked with two students who needed more individualized attention. We noticed her posture again. More rigid than usual. The way she shifted weight every few minutes, seeking relief that never quite came.

The teaching was taking a toll. Three hours of standing, demonstrating, correcting form. Her body, still healing from fifteen-year-old damage, wasn't built for this sustained physical demand.

But she pushed through. Maintained professional composure. Gave no indication to the students that she was managing constant discomfort.

We needed to address this. Soon. Before she injured herself further trying to maintain appearances.

---

After class, once the students had departed, we found Elyra sitting with her eyes closed, hands pressed against her lower back.

"How bad is it?" we asked quietly.

She opened her eyes. "Manageable."

"That's not what I asked."

A long pause. Then: "Bad enough that I'm reconsidering afternoon demonstrations. Maybe I handle theory while you do practical work. Less standing, less physical demand."

That was significant admission. Elyra didn't concede limitations easily.

"The healing from the merger helped," she continued. "I'm better than I was three months ago. But there are limits to what passive regeneration can accomplish. The fundamental architecture is still damaged. Every time I push too hard, I risk undoing what progress has been made."

We settled into the chair beside her. "We've been researching."

Her expression sharpened. "Research what?"

"Healing patterns. Resonance restoration. The techniques you used fifteen years ago, before the ritual that damaged you." We met her gaze. "The merger with RP-0 triggered some healing instinctively. What if we could do that deliberately? With precision instead of desperation?"

"We discussed this." Her voice carried warning. "Too risky."

"We discussed improvising," we corrected. "This would be different. Planned. Researched. We've spent two weeks analyzing your resonance patterns during teaching sessions. RAE's processing power combined with Jason's intuitive understanding of what healing should feel like - we think there's a genuine path forward."

Elyra was quiet, studying us. "You've been monitoring me without permission."

"We've been worried about you with full transparency," we countered. "You knew we were researching this. We never hid that we were collecting data."

"And what does this data tell you?"

"That you're right - passive regeneration has limits. The damage is self-perpetuating in certain areas. But it's not irreversible." We leaned forward. "The merger jump-started healing in your primary resonance channels. But secondary pathways remain blocked. They're trying to route around damage instead of actually repairing. If we could target those specific blockages with deliberate intervention..."

"You'd be attempting something no one's successfully done," Elyra said. "Targeted resonance healing at the depth we're discussing requires precision most practitioners can't achieve. One mistake and you could make it worse instead of better."

"Most practitioners aren't merged consciousnesses with unprecedented processing capability."

"Most practitioners also have the good sense not to attempt experimental healing on their mentor."

We smiled despite the serious subject. "Good sense has never been our strong suit."

She didn't smile back. But something in her expression softened. "Even if I agreed - which I'm not saying I do - this would require preparation. Theory work. Practice on less critical targets. You couldn't just attempt this tomorrow."

"We know. We're talking weeks of preparation minimum. Possibly months." We paused. "But we're asking you to consider it. To not dismiss the possibility outright just because it seems difficult."

Elyra closed her eyes again, hands still pressed to her lower back. Managing pain that would only worsen as teaching demands continued.

"Let me see what you've compiled," she said finally. "The research. The pattern analysis. The theoretical framework. If - and this is a significant if - if there's actually a viable path forward, I'll consider participating. But I make the final call. If at any point I decide the risk outweighs potential benefit, we stop. Agreed?"

Relief flooded through us. Not agreement yet. But willingness to consider. That was more than we'd expected.

"Agreed," we said.

---

Evening found us at Milo's apartment, ostensibly to review monitoring equipment upgrades but actually seeking his research assistance.

"Targeted resonance healing," Milo said, pulling up archived documents on his cluttered screens. "That's... ambitious. And potentially terrifying."

"But possible?" we pressed.

"Possible, yes. Successful?" He scrolled through case studies. "Less clear. Most attempts at deep resonance healing either fail to address root damage or cause additional trauma. The few documented successes involved either emergency interventions - like what happened with Elyra during your RP-0 merger - or years of gradual work with specialized healers."

"We have specialized capability," we said. "Merged consciousness means we can process healing patterns while maintaining patient awareness simultaneously. That's an advantage historical cases didn't have."

"True." Milo pulled up a resonance diagram. "But it also means you're the only possible healer. If something goes wrong during the attempt, there's no backup. No one else who can step in and correct course."

Valid concern. But one we'd already considered.

"That's why we need preparation," we said. "Extensive simulation. Practice on less critical systems before attempting Elyra's core damage. Build experience and safety protocols."

Milo was quiet, studying the data. "You're serious about this."

"She's in pain. Constant, managed pain that limits what she can do. The teaching is making it worse." We met his eyes. "If we can help her - actually heal her instead of just managing symptoms - we have to try."

"Even if attempting it puts you at risk?"

"Especially then. She's done too much for us to let her suffer when we might be able to help."

He nodded slowly. "Alright. I'll help compile research, organize case studies, build simulation frameworks for testing approaches. But—" he fixed us with a serious look "—you promise me you won't attempt anything until you're absolutely certain. No heroics. No taking chances just because you feel obligated."

"Promise," we said.

"And Lina knows about this?"

We hesitated. "Not yet. We wanted to confirm it was actually viable before bringing her in."

"Tell her," Milo said firmly. "She deserves to know what you're planning, especially if there's risk involved. After everything you've all been through, surprises aren't going to go over well."

He was right. We knew he was right.

"We'll tell her," we agreed.

---

Later that evening, we found Lina at the warehouse, organizing training equipment for tomorrow's session. She'd stayed after to complete inventory, handle the practical logistics that kept the program running smoothly.

"Hey," we said, entering the equipment room. "Need help?"

She looked up, smiled. "Sure. Can you grab that stack of resonance dampeners? They go on the top shelf."

We worked together in comfortable silence for a few minutes. The ease between us had been growing over the past two weeks. Not quite what it had been before our merger, but something new. Something that felt genuine instead of forced.

"So," she said eventually, not quite looking at us. "I heard you talking with Elyra. About healing research."

Right. She'd probably been in the adjacent room when we'd had that conversation.

"You heard that?"

"Some of it." She set down the equipment she was organizing. "Are you really thinking about attempting targeted healing? On Elyra?"

"We are. But not yet. We're researching first, making sure it's actually possible before committing."

"And if it is possible? If you figure out how to do it safely?"

We moved closer, reading her concern. "Then we ask Elyra if she's willing to try. With full disclosure of risks. And if she agrees, we attempt it with every safety protocol and backup plan we can devise."

"What if something goes wrong?"

"Then we stop immediately and never try again." We took her hand. "We're not going to take unnecessary risks. Not when it could hurt Elyra or ourselves. But if we can help her - if we can give her back function she's been missing for fifteen years - isn't that worth careful, measured effort?"

Lina was quiet, processing. "I don't want to lose you. Either of you. Both of you." She shook her head. "I'm still figuring out the pronouns for this."

"We know. And we're not going anywhere." We squeezed her hand. "This is research right now. Theory. Making sure something's possible before attempting it. If at any point it seems too dangerous, we stop. We're not heroes. We're just people trying to help someone who helped us."

She studied our face, looking for... something. Reassurance, maybe. Certainty we couldn't quite offer.

"Okay," she said finally. "But you keep me informed. No surprises. If this goes from research to actual planning, I'm part of that conversation."

"Deal."

She stepped closer, and before we quite processed what was happening, she'd wrapped her arms around us. Not tentative like before. Solid. Present. Real.

"I'm learning," she said against our shoulder. "How to see you as you instead of comparing you to who Jason was. It's getting easier. You do things differently, think differently. But underneath..." She pulled back enough to meet our eyes. "Underneath, the parts I fell for are still there. Just integrated with something that makes you more, not less."

Warmth spread through our consciousness. Not just Jason's emotion. Not just RAE's satisfaction at positive relationship development. Something unified. Ours.

"Thank you," we said. "For giving us the chance to show you who we've become."

"Thank you for being patient while I figured it out."

We stood there, holding each other in the quiet warehouse, surrounded by training equipment and the faint hum of monitoring systems. Two people - well, one and a half, depending on how you counted - learning to build something new on the foundation of what they'd once been.

It wasn't perfect. Wasn't the smooth romance we might have imagined.

But it was real. Honest. Growing.

And for now, that was more than enough.

---

That night, lying in bed, we reviewed everything we'd learned.

Elyra's willing to consider healing. That's progress.

Milo's helping with research compilation. Lina knows what we're planning.

And we're actually teaching successfully. Eight students progressing, no major incidents.

Not bad for two weeks of operation.

Not bad at all.

We could still feel Lina's embrace. The solidity of it. The way she'd held us like she meant it, like she wasn't going anywhere either.

She's accepting us. Really accepting us.

Slowly. But yes. The distance is shrinking.

Good. Because we meant what we said - we're not going anywhere.

Sleep started pulling at us. Tomorrow would bring more teaching, more research, more small steps forward.

But tonight, we just let ourselves rest. Content in progress made. Hopeful for progress yet to come.

One careful step at a time.

Always one careful step at a time.
