## Industrial Yard

*Observe only,* Arendt had said. The cups still held the echo of his tea when they stepped out into the cold, each choosing a different moment so the door didn’t confess to a group.

Jason adjusted his scarf, let the street air sand down the shape of Arendt’s offer, and set their pace to municipal: not hurried, not slow. Lina picked up the rhythm beside him. Milo fell in a half‑step behind, reading a flyer like it was fascinating.

The city did what cities do—blinked, hummed, forgot. A tram sighed past and sketched a flicker of their reflections along its windows. Far down the block, a car idled with the patience of a cat pretending not to stalk.

“Perimeter?” Lina’s lips barely moved.

Jason’s fingers found the transparent curve of the **whisper‑clip** behind his ear. “Low tide,” he said, the join phrase soft enough to vanish into the street noise.

RAE joined through the clip—a dry clarity at Jason’s mastoid, consonants shaped in bone. “Mesh up. Floor to Lina.”

Lina’s voice warmed the same invisible path. “Floor accepted. Eyes right: storefront camera two, intermittent blink. Not ours.”

“Copy,” Milo said, the word placed like a thumb tack.

*Jason: head status?*

**Fine. Slow channel.**

*Slow confirmed. I’ll speak in short edges.*

They crossed with the light. Behind them the café windows put their shadows back where they belonged. Jason kept his face as boring as bureaucracy and let his breath count to three and start again.

---

They didn’t go home. They went where paper said the city kept its old bones: a shuttered **industrial yard** two tram stops east, fence stenciled with numbers that pretended to be rules. The air smelled of dust that had learned patience. Freight hum ghosted from the tracks beyond.

“Cover?” Milo asked, already peeling a sticker from his clipboard.

“Meter survey,” Lina said. “Contractor class. You carry the forms. Jason carries the keys to nothing.”

Lina’s hands stayed in her jacket. “Gate latch is aluminum—clean. Camera on the corner box is cosmetic; the cable feeds nothing. We’re walking.”

They walked. The chain‑link gave under Jason’s palm like an instrument deciding whether to be tuned. Inside, angle‑iron racks held crates that remembered weight. Faded **safety posters** explained things everyone once knew.

*Jason: head status?*

**Clear. Slow channel.**

*Good. I’ll keep to the clip unless you call for room.*

A yard cat considered them, then decided bureaucracy was not prey.

---

Near the maintenance shed, a set of copper loops rose from a concrete pad like ribs that had forgotten a chest. The **two‑point overlay** from the dealer  had marked this exact spot as “should feel wrong.” Milo unfolded a public register copy and slid their thin film over it. The lines kissed and then disagreed.

“Here and here,” Milo said blandly, pen tapping the two anchors. “If it drops, we document. No heroics.”

Jason knelt, palms hovering above the loops without gifting them contact. He listened with his hands: not for sound, but for **shape**. The pad’s **firmness** was good; the quality of whatever ran through it was not. It stuttered like a heart remembering a different song.

*Tiny nudge on the half. Quarter‑second.*

He timed his breath. On the off‑beat, he placed a **micro‑deflection**, a clean **phase nudge** no louder than a decision. The loop’s return wobbled, then steadied at a better wrong.

“Reading,” Milo said, already logging. “Before, after. Overlay aligns to the uglier version.”

“Again,” Lina said softly. “Then we leave it the way we found it, which is to say slightly less terrible.”

Jason set one more nudge, smaller, cleaner. The ache he’d been ignoring pressed a thumb between his eyes, then relented.

*Stop there. You’re buying signal at the price of you.*

**Stopping.** He rocked back on his heels and let the concrete cool his knees through denim. The **faded posters** on the shed wall told him to report hazards to a supervisor from a decade ago.

---

Headlights brushed the far fence: **a guard vehicle** doing its patient loop. Lina didn’t look. Her jaw did that tiny thing again which meant she could run quietly in boots.

“Floor to Lina,” Jason said, out loud and ordinary, as if discussing bolt sizes.

“Route **B**,” Lina returned. She didn’t move yet. She let the truck take its time, let its idle write a line across the crates, let boredom do its work. When the beam passed, she breathed once. “Now.”

They became **boring** with intent: Milo folded paper; Jason held a blank form like it mattered; Lina checked an imaginary meter, counted to six, and wrote a number that would only convince someone who wanted to be convinced.

*External field: low. Someone tried a **beat‑lock** on the yard cameras but the mounts are tired and the tone is wrong. Amateur pamphlet energy again.*

**We don’t answer.**

*We don’t answer.*

The truck completed its **sweep** and parked at the office prefab. A man inside scrolled his phone with the intensity of a monk at prayer. He did not look up.

---

“Two more points for the log,” Milo murmured. “Then we take our dignity and go.”

They crossed to a second pad, this one under a **faded gantry** that remembered cranes. Jason touched the rail with two fingers—**contact beats distance**—and felt the yard’s old metal introduce him to itself. No nudge this time. Only listening.

“Document,” Lina said. She wrote the time, the weather, and three words: **‘no intervention performed.’**

A gust carried the **freight hum** from the tracks stronger. Somewhere out there, a train composed itself.

*Jason, watch the onset. If your chest tugs, we’re done.*

**Copy.** The tug stayed theoretical.

They exited through the same gap they had “used” to enter, because the fence liked stories with loops. Jason set the latch back down with the weight he’d found it at.

On the sidewalk, they didn’t speak. Citizens with **jobs and receipts** rarely narrate.

---

The tram home was half‑full of coats pretending to be people. Jason stood by the accordion joint. The **rail‑joint rhythm** found him. It synced, too neatly, with the muted **tugs** he’d been keeping off his sternum since the café.

*Breathe on the half.*

He did. The rhythm slid past instead of through. Lina’s reflection in the window looked like a stranger having the same thought. Milo texted a note to their future selves. No one chased them.

The city held them like cargo that had paid its fare.
