## Dealer’s Trap

The basement shop had the kind of lighting that made coins look honest and people look slightly underwater. Four mismatched fluorescents hummed at different ages, two cool-white, one tired blue, one that took a breath between flickers. The smell was citrus cleaner over dust, as if someone had mopped truth onto the floor and left lies on the shelves.

A bell tacked to the door jittered once and decided that was enough. Lina stepped in first, Jason a half pace behind, Milo last with the clipboard.

A long glass counter ran the length of the room. Inside: meters, coils, stamped copper slips, a few dead resonators that had been posed to look asleep instead of gone. The far wall wore municipal maps like a conscience.

“Welcome, friends,” said the dealer, the word shaped like an invitation to use his first name without ever learning it. He was mid-forties, crisp shirt, sleeves rolled to exactly the forearm, smile calibrated. “You made it down the scary stairs.”

Milo looked past the smile to the hallway beyond the bead curtain. “We like stairs.” He lifted the clipboard just enough that the paper crackled. “We like receipts more.”

The dealer’s eyes crinkled. “You’ll get both.”

Jason’s headache, faint since the walk, took one small step forward and stopped. He catalogued the room the way he’d taught himself over the past weeks—count, name, let go; without judgment. The fluorescents. The hum of a small fridge behind the counter. The off-rhythm tick of a wall clock that was trying to make its mind up about seconds.

"Carrier chosen: counter fridge"*,* RAE said—by way of the cooler’s soft, steady vibration under the glass once, forming a sound beam only Milo could hear.

Milo angled his head a degree toward the glass, as if reading a label.

“Basement Regulations,” the dealer said with a little flourish, palms up. “No last names, no recordings, no… demonstrations.” He let that land, grin considerate. “Everyone leaves with exactly what they came in with, unless they pay to carry more.”

“Good,” Lina said, scanning shelves with a practiced disinterest. “We came for a map you teased on the forum and an explanation that doesn’t start with ‘trust me.’”

The dealer’s gaze did the quick arithmetic of reputation. “You’re the cartography crowd,” he said, as if that were a joke. He flicked two latches under the counter, lifted a panel, and set a flat archival case on the glass. “I have a map that proves everything.”

“Everything,” Milo repeated, dry. “Bold category.”

“Proves what we’re all feeling and half of us are afraid to name: the municipal fabric around Mill‑4 has been cut and resewn.” He set the case between them and opened it to reveal a large sheet: city grid, river line, ward boundaries—overlaid with an ochre ink that braided faint loops through streets and alleys like spilled thread.

Jason leaned just close enough that he could smell the paper. Old, yes—but the varnish was new, and whoever had done the conservation had used a citrus solvent.

“Provenance?” Milo asked without looking up.

“A retired surveyor with a sentimental streak,” the dealer said. “If the city were honest, it would pay *him* a pension for this and then burn it.”

“Chain of custody?”

“Three hands in six months. He to a picker, the picker to me. All clean.”

“Dates?”

“Surveyed nineteen years ago, annotated in the last five.”

“Any two independent concordances?”

The dealer smiled wider. “I love a man who says ‘concordances’ before lunch.”

Milo tapped the clipboard with his pen, unamused. “Receipts.”

The dealer slid out a second sheet: a faded print of a maintenance schedule with margins scribbled by an obsessive. He double‑tapped three circles with a nail cut interestingly short. “Streetlight outages along a diagonal. Twice. A decade apart.” A third paper surfaced, this one a transit timing slip. “Micro‑delays here and here on the red‑line—only at crossings that used to intersect a walkway that no longer exists. Patterns that form not because someone planned a circle, but because someone wanted to erase a line.”

Lina kept her voice light. “And what do *you* want?”

He closed the archival case with the gentleness of someone signing a contract. “Nothing dramatic. A copy of your querying pattern—you have one, or you wouldn’t be here—and a favor later.”

“No,” Lina said, pleasant as sunshine. “That’s the price you ask when you don’t have receipts.”

“On the contrary.” He gestured toward the map wall. “You can test me right now.”

Jason slid his hands into his pockets to keep them neutral. The  ache behind his eyes put on its boots.

**What’s wrong with the clock?**

*Listening.* A half beat. \*Tick deviates under speech stress. That clock is a cheap field stabilizer disguised as décor. He leans on it when he wants the room to stay flat.

**Flat how?**

*Less reactive. Fewer accidental tells. A carpet over footprints.*

**And why can Milo hear you and he can’t?**

*Private lobe from the fridge hum—constructive at Milo’s left ear, null toward the counter. No implants. Just geometry and consent.*

Jason let his weight shift to the other heel. The fridge hummed once like agreement and then minded its business.

“May I?” he asked, fingertips hovering over the map’s margin where a clean corner pretended to be old. He didn’t touch. The dealer nodded as if granting a favor.

“Your varnish is new,” Jason said. “The citrus keeps the old smell honest, but it shines in the wrong direction under this light.”

The dealer’s smile thinned. “You work with paper.”

“He lives with it,” Milo said. “And he doesn’t pay in pattern signatures.” He pointed his pen like a tiny gavel. “If your map proves ‘everything,’ it can survive daylight. Hand me two sites within walking distance where the loops cross and we can verify a single public fact: that something has been moved without leaving a maintenance trail anyone admits to.”

The dealer considered, eyes flicking to the clock just as it ticked two seconds in one.

*He will try a shortcut,* RAE murmured through the counter glass. *Stay inside receipts.*

“Elm Passage,” the dealer said. “Alley behind the laundries. And the service lane behind the market stalls—north run.”

Milo wrote them down. “We’ll bring you a yes/no, and maybe a sandwich if you stop selling favors like they’re stickers.”

The dealer chuckled. “Ah, the ethics crowd.”

“Ethics keep people out of holes,” Lina said. “Show us the back room.”

His eyebrows climbed. “Back room?”

“Every basement map seller has one,” she said cheerfully. “The too‑tidy one where the provenance lines up and the dangerous stuff is on the top shelf where you need the short ladder with the missing rubber foot. If you’re real, yours smells like oil and lemon. If you’re fake, we’ll come back with questions.”

The beads parted and fell behind them.

---

The back room *was* too tidy. A folding ladder  leaned like a memory in one corner. Shelving held boxes of field‑copper slips and spools of etched wire with their teeth aligned. The smell turned from citrus to lemon‑oil‑plus‑metal in the way of carefully edited truth.

The dealer gestured like a maître d’. “As advertised. Tools, blanks, papers that have learned patience.”

Jason kept his hands in sight and his attention soft. The headache hovered in the doorway. On the top shelf, a box bore a municipal stamp that should have flaked by now. It hadn’t. Someone had clear‑coated history.

Lina took one step toward the ladder and stopped because it wobbled just enough to be a story. “Hmm...missing foot,” she said, pleased by prediction.

“Can I see your work orders?” Milo asked. “For the map care. The varnish, the underlay, the backing. It’s not a trick question. I collect boring paperwork.”

The dealer weighed whether to treat that as adorable or dangerous. “I’ll fetch what I can.” He crossed to a drawer, opened it, and took out a folder labeled with a handwriting that tried to look older than the pen that had written it. He handed it to Milo.

Milo didn’t rush. He paged, pursed his lips, and said nothing. “Thanks,” he added, as if the word had a weight limit.

Jason felt the clock on the other side of the curtain tick wrong again. The fluorescents disagreed in a new way—a little chorus that lost the beat for a bar and then found it because they’d always meant to. The field did not want to show its edge where it touched the dealer.

**He’s soothing the room,** Jason thought. **Trying to make us a little polite.**

*Correct.*

**Anything dangerous?**

*No. Just persuasive. A field manners lesson applied without consent.*

Lina reached for a copper slip and let it refract the light. “Pretty,” she said. “Wrong stamp.” She set it back among its arranged siblings. “Let’s not get fancy.” She turned to the dealer with the kind of smile that gets people to underestimate you for exactly the right amount of time. “What’s the favor?”

The dealer looked offended on behalf of his own restraint. “A call in six months. A small delivery.”

“No,” Lina said, same pleasant voice. “Try again.”

“Access to your private queries.” He said it lightly, but the room leaned around the words like a boat in a wake. “Read‑only. For safety.”

Milo laughed and it wasn’t kind. “Safety is why receipts exist.” He held up the folder. “These are pretty, but I can tell which pages you wrote yesterday. Two dates still wear their coffee rings like they’re fresh.” He handed the folder back. “I’m not accusing you of fraud, friend. I’m accusing you of sales.”

Something in the dealer’s posture shifted a millimeter toward honest. “Fair,” he said. “I’m running a shop.”

“Then sell us something you can *prove*,” Lina said. “We’ll even pay.”

He thought. The wall clock on the other side of the curtain skipped a breath.

*Off‑beat tick magnitude increased*. *His composure is a machine with too many belts.*

“Fine,” the dealer said. “I’ll loan you the two‑point overlay.” He slid a thin film from between two cardboard backers. “It lines our loops to the city’s official sheets **here** and **here**. You can lay it on a public register copy at the depot and see if your stomach drops. If it does, we’re in business. If it doesn’t, we all had a walk.”

Milo nodded, businesslike. “That is a test we can live with.” He glanced at Jason. “Depot B, Mill‑4. Map drawers.”

Jason nodded back, grateful for the shape of a plan with paper in it.

They returned to the front room. The fluorescents decided which key to sing. The clock pretended to have always been regular.

“Price?” Lina asked.

“Paper money. A deposit. And your promise that you’ll bring it back if you don’t like how it feels.”

Lina paid without looking like she was paying. The dealer slid the thin film into a plain sleeve, then into a larger paper envelope with nothing written on it. He pushed it across the counter.

*Exit now*. *We have enough. The longer you stay, the more of your edges you let this room sand down.*

“Thank you,” Jason said, and meant it in the way you thank someone who gives you a test instead of an answer.

“Pleasure,” said the dealer, meaning *margin*. “Elm Passage. North run.”

The hallway outside the shop was painted the kind of beige landlords tell themselves tenants like. It smelled like cleaner and the ghost of cigarettes. Their shoes scuffed a small history across the stair treads.

Lina tucked the envelope into the inner pocket of her jacket. “We’re not touching that until it’s on the depot’s table,” she said. “No ghosts in pockets.”

“Depot, then the alley,” Milo said, already writing their order of operations in the corner of his printout. “Then coffee. And no favors.”

Jason exhaled. The headache had not left; it had decided to walk politely behind them.

**He wants us to owe him.**

*He wants a future where he can name a direction and watch you lean. That’s what his clock is for.*

**We’re not clocks.**

*No. You’re citizens with jobs and receipts.*

He smiled despite himself.

At the turn of the stair, a door stood ajar. From inside came a sound two notes long—soft, high, and then lower, like a question being reduced to its shape. It wasn’t a melody yet, just a fragment that made his skin listen.

Lina heard it too. Her eyes cut to Jason’s. Milo said, “I’m not adding ‘mysterious music’ to the list.”

Jason’s chest did that small, familiar tug that was neither pain nor warning—attention, perhaps, in a language he had not learned yet.

*I’m quiet under melodies*. *That’s a rule I learned the hard way.*

**Why?**

*Melodies are low‑entropy attractors. If I speak on top, the field tries to phase‑lock me to the tune; that widens my footprint and makes me easy to triangulate. Vowels ride farther and imprint identity; I avoid them under music. Safer to go quiet—or keep to consonants.*

**And internal diskussion?**

*We can, yes—direct thoughtline. No air, no carrier. Under melody it’s the safer path.*

“Noted,” Jason murmured.

They didn’t investigate. They did what people with alibis do: they left. Daylight met them at the street like a witness.

On the sidewalk, the city fit them right back into its pockets. Tram bell, stroller on brick, someone was arguing kindly about olives outside a grocer.

Milo grinned sideways. “Bench Scale update: the shop’s stool is a minus one. Only a liar can sit on it for more than thirty seconds.”

“That man isn’t a liar,” Lina said. “He’s a shop.”

“Shops can lie about provenance,” Milo said. “But you’re right. He sells pressure and stories to people who want shortcuts. We’re boring.”

“Boring keeps us alive,” Lina said, and bumped Jason’s shoulder with hers.

Jason folded the small ache behind his eyes into the kind of clarity that makes lists.

- Depot
- Overlay
- Public table
- Photographs
- Compare
- Alley

He glanced back at the stairs. The two notes didn’t come again. The wall clock downstairs would find its beat and hold it until someone spoke too hard.

He didn’t know yet whether the map would make his stomach drop. He did know they hadn’t paid for the privilege of owing anyone.

Daylight, normal shoes. Paper before rumor. They moved.

*Proceeding on slow channel.*
