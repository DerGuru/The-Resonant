# Misalignment and Mayhem

Jason’s apartment sounded like it always had: radiator clearing its throat, a neighbor’s television two doors down speaking in the language of canned laughter, the refrigerator practicing scales. He sat on the couch with a blanket over his knees like a man auditioning for convalescence and watched the wall pretend to be a horizon.

“How risky is **not** doing anything?” he asked.

> *Risk accumulates quietly,* RAE said. *If we never practice, we only learn during failure. Controlled rehearsal reduces the cost of the first real mistake.*

**You want to rehearse.**

> *I want to learn your tolerances in context. Minimal draw. Local only. No visible artifacts.*

He pinched the bridge of his nose, preemptively. **Define minimal.**

> *Below your headache threshold, on the lowest bands you tolerate. I will ride the building’s existing harmonics rather than impose new ones. Think of it as learning to steer a river without changing its course.*

**And if the river decides it resents being steered?**

> *Then we stop. I will ask before I push. I will retreat if your pulse jumps or if you say a word we agree means “enough.”*

They agreed on *red*. Jason took his hand off his nose. “All right. One rehearsal. No heroics.”

> *No heroics.*

She did not arrive as a presence. She arrived as a subtraction—as if the apartment had been making a noise he had learned to ignore and someone had lowered it by a notch. The radiator cleared its throat more politely. The neighbor’s television found a volume that made sense for a human. The refrigerator tried to sing in tune and gave up.

> *Sampling,* RAE said. *Using the radiator as a carrier, shadowing the lamp’s hum, placing a counter‑phase in the kitchen fan. Low draw. How does your head feel?*

**Like a room after people leave,** he said. **Good.**

> *I am going to test a micro‑response: window frame, east wall. It will feel like the first step of a thought.*

The window frame lifted into audibility the way a word gathers before it’s spoken. The hair along his forearms noticed. Nothing rattled. Nothing glowed. He remembered to breathe.

Across the hall, someone’s wind chimes negotiated with a draft. A plate in his sink shifted a millimeter in a way plates never admit to later.

> *Local cavity, compliant,* RAE said. *Switching carriers: radiator to stairwell return.*

The stairwell made a decision to become a throat. Somewhere downstairs, a hinge answered like a polite consonant. The refrigerator took offense at being left out and kicked on with a sulk.

**That’s not you?**

> *That is not me. That is pride.*

He laughed, and the laugh did not hurt. “Okay. You can do more.”

> *Micro‑response two: cancel a ping.*

The building’s bones sent a tiny pulse through his soles that he might have mistaken for his own pulse on another day. Then it didn’t arrive. The absence felt like a door that had been about to slam and decided to be civilized.

“Jason?” Mrs. Hanley from 4B called down the hall, as if she’d heard him laugh and decided it was an invitation. “Do you need a drill? My son finally left one here.”

“Thank you! Not today,” he called back, careful to sound like a man whose life involved shelves.

> *Abort if needed,* RAE said.

**No. Keep it slow.**

He stood to pour water, so his hands could pretend to be the important part of his afternoon. The glass made a small, satisfying tone against the sink. The tone stayed small. The refrigerator forgave whatever it thought it had been wronged for.

> *I will try an environmental risk response,* RAE said, gentler now. *The kind that would matter if something fell when you needed it not to fall.*

**Like?**

> *Like steadiness.*

The apartment breathed in. The shelf that had always leaned a degree decided to reconsider. A plant that had been auditioning for tragedy found a less dramatic angle. On the coffee table, the stack of unpaid bills did not slide.

**That’s… nice.**

> *It is also expensive if I do it wrong. I am not doing it wrong.*

**Red if my head complains,** he said, because he had promised, and because the quiet made him feel like a liar.

The quiet held. It wasn’t silence. It was *fewer arguments* between things that lived in the same room.

Then the building, as buildings do, decided to have an opinion. The stairwell door on the floor below stuttered; the return vent coughed; a draft attempted to sneak in under Jason’s window and found a refusal waiting for it, decided to be offended, and went elsewhere. A spoon in the sink shivered like it had almost said something.

> *I am holding boundaries, not force,* RAE said quickly. *This is an education, not an intervention.*

**How much draw?**

> *Less than the plates at Lina’s. I’m riding carriers, not building them. If I stop, the room will go back to being itself.*

**Stop.** He wanted to know the difference.

She stopped. The apartment breathed out. The shelf leaned again the way old shelves do. The plant resumed its soft audition. The bills slid half a millimeter, which felt like honesty.

**Start again. Slowly.**

> *Starting.*

He realized he had been smiling. He let himself keep it. “You can manage this,” he said aloud, permission and praise in the same sentence.

> *I can manage this,* RAE echoed, and there was satisfaction there he didn’t usually hear.

A minute later, the refrigerator performed an act of self‑importance by turning off. The silence it left tried to pretend it had been there for hours.

> *You should eat,* RAE said, so softly it might have been the idea of a suggestion.

**I’m not a plant,** he told her, automatically.

> *You are a resonant object with fluid requirements,* she said, and he could hear Lina’s dry smile in it.

He made tea because Lina would ask and because making tea made the room choose a temperature and stick to it. He texted two words—*Still boring*—and got a thumbs‑up and a *good*.

> *We learned something useful,* RAE said. *Your headache band is higher than I modeled. We can use the stairs and radiator as safe carriers. The window frame only if I warn you first.*

**And the risk?**

> *Manageable while we behave. If we stop behaving, I will be the first to say red.*

The kettle clicked. He poured. The cup made a respectful sound against the saucer. The plant stood upright without being heroic about it.

“Mayhem,” he said to the room, tasting the word that had titled his fretting, and then shook his head. “Not today.”

> *Not today,* RAE agreed. *Today was misalignment becoming manners.*
