## Part 4 - We Are Family | Ch. 10 - Changed, but okay

We woke in a hospital room that smelled of antiseptic and filtered air.

White walls. Soft beeping of monitors. The distinctive hum of medical resonance scanners cycling through diagnostic patterns.

Lina sat beside our bed, her hand wrapped around ours. She looked exhausted - dark circles under her eyes, hair pulled back in a messy ponytail, still wearing the same clothes from the battle.

"Hey," we said, voice rough.

Her eyes snapped to our face. Relief flooded her expression. "Hey yourself. You've been out for eighteen hours."

"Eighteen?" We tried to sit up. Our body protested immediately - everything ached, deep in our bones, in our patterns. "Are we—"

*We're here,* we thought to ourselves. *Resting. That fight took everything we had.*

The memories crashed back. Kage-Kōzō. The battle. The blade we'd created. The systematic destruction of something that had once been alive.

"We killed it," we said.

"Yeah." Lina's grip tightened on our hand. "You did."

"How do you feel about that?"

She was quiet for a moment, her thumb tracing circles on our palm. "Conflicted. It was necessary. It was right. But it was also..." She trailed off, searching for words. "It changes things. Changes you. Knowing you're capable of that."

We nodded. We felt it too. We'd crossed a threshold. Proven we could destroy rather than reform. That knowledge sat heavy in our chest.

"Integration jumped during the fight," we said, remembering the surge. "How high?"

"75%." Lina pulled up a medical scan on the tablet beside the bed. "The combat stress pushed you past multiple thresholds at once. The doctors say it's stable, but... you're approaching the point of no return."

We studied the scan. The merging of our patterns was clearer now - less two separate entities occupying one space, more a single consciousness with dual origins.

"We don't regret it," we said quietly. "What we did. We needed to be protected. The people in Tokyo needed to be safe. Kage-Kōzō couldn't be reasoned with. So we did what had to be done."

"That's very mature of you," Lina said, but her smile was sad. "I'm just... I'm scared. What if next time the choice isn't so clear? What if - "

"Then we do what we always do," we interrupted gently. "We question. We debate. We check with ourselves. Red stops, remember? We don't act alone. Not anymore."

She nodded, some of the tension leaving her shoulders. "You're right. Sorry. I'm just tired and worried and - "

"Scared," we finished. "Us too. But we'll deal with it. Together."

Yuki entered then, bowing slightly at the door. "I hope I'm not interrupting. I wanted to thank you both before you leave."

"For what?" we asked.

"For destroying what we could not. Kage-Kōzō killed twelve of our people over the years. Damaged dozens more. You ended that threat permanently. My country owes you a debt."

"Did we have a choice?" we asked, the question heavier than it should have been.

Yuki's expression was understanding. "No. Some things cannot be redeemed. You proved you understand that distinction. It's... mature. Difficult. But necessary. And it makes me trust you more, not less."

We stayed in Tokyo another week. Healing. Debriefing. Establishing sister-program protocols with the Japanese institute. We watched as Yuki's operators - the ones who'd fought beside us - processed the victory with quiet relief.

No celebration. No triumph. Just the heavy satisfaction of a necessary job finally done.

When we boarded the plane home, we felt different. Heavier. Older.

But also clearer.

We had proven we could make the hard choices. Could fight when fighting was needed. Could destroy when destruction was the only option.

That knowledge would change everything.

"Changed, but okay," we said to Lina as Tokyo disappeared below us.

She rested her head on our shoulder. "Yeah. Changed, but okay."

And somewhere in the space between waking and sleeping, we hummed soft agreement to ourselves.
