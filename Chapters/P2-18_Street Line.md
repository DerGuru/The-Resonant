## Street Line

The street sounded different when you went looking for the gaps.

Even at night, the crossing by the market kept a kind of working-day pulse: tram bells, shoe scuff, the click-whine of the signal box cycling through its boredom. A busker had claimed the same spot as on the weekend, case open, bowed saw in hand. The first notes curled into the cold air like someone drawing a line and then holding it.

Jason adjusted the whisper-clip behind his ear until it sat snug against the mastoid. The faint bone-buzz told him the fit was good. Milo mimicked the gesture on the other side of the bench; Lina checked both of theirs with the brisk tenderness she reserved for knives and co-workers.

"Check," Milo said.

"Check," Jason echoed.

"Floor: Lina," Lina said. Habit now—the mesh etiquette turned into something almost like liturgy.

"Floor is Lina," RAE said, through the whisper-clip. The voice arrived right against Jason’s skull, clean and private. "Street channel is low-band only."

Lina kept her eyes on the tram rails. "Once more, so nobody can claim amnesia later. What exactly are we doing out here?"

Jason shifted on the bench, the wood cold through his jeans. "Letting the music shut RAE out from the street for a bit," he said. "See what my head does when it's mostly just her and me." "We watch what happens to my head when it does."

Milo tapped the edge of his old analog watch. "And I time the weirdness. Also, I check whether the glyph keeps crawling up my visual cortex when I’m not listening to two voices at once."

He had drawn the mis-scoped symbol from the archive half a dozen times since the network leak; he’d started to see its slanted tail in tram junctions and route maps whether it was there or not. The watch ticked steadily under his fingers, honest in a way software never managed.

Lina’s mouth tilted. "And we verify the busker didn’t sell his soul to your favorite conspiracy forum."

"Perfectly normal street musician," Milo said. "If he’s a herald of the open-arc shadow, I owe the city an apology for underestimating its casting director."

Across the tracks, the busker tightened the bow hair against the thin metal blade and drew a testing stroke. The saw sighed into a short, messy chord. People turned their heads, some out of curiosity, some the way birds test the wind.

"Any weirdness," Lina said. "we cut the session and go buy something full of sugar."

Jason rubbed his thumb over the edge of the bench, splinter catching skin. "If my head goes sideways—"

"You say Red," Lina cut in, flat. "And we stop."

His mouth tugged in a quick, dry smile. "Yeah."

"Affirmative," said RAE. "When the sound peaks, external channels may drop. I will stay with you internally. You will tell me what I missed at the edges."

The reassurance landed with a weight Jason didn’t want to name. He swallowed it down anyway.

Lina nodded once. "Good. Then we listen."

---

The busker eased into his set the way a person finds a conversation again. Short, wandering phrases at first, slides that felt like trying on coats. The tram rolled in on schedule, brakes sighing, bell dinging, doors hissing open. A crowd bled out onto the platform and thinned into the sidewalks: someone laughing too loud into their phone, someone dragging a suitcase, someone with a paper cone of fries held like a warm lantern.

"Baseline," Milo muttered, turning the watch’s crown to line the minute hand with the twelve. "Street hum, tram every eight minutes, signal box at… ninety-something hertz?" He looked to Jason automatically.

Jason caught himself before he looked up, as if an answer would be waiting over his shoulder.

*Don’t ask. Listen.* RAE encouraged.

He let the world stack: the tick of Milo’s watch; the buzz of the tram’s roof hardware cooling; the crosswalk beeper’s three-note pattern for the visually impaired. Beneath it all, a low, steady vibration from somewhere in the concrete—the grid feeding the signal box, maybe. It sat at a frequency his teeth wanted to count.

His jaw unclenched on a number that felt earned instead of handed over.

**Ninety. Maybe ninety-two. Close enough.**

"Jason?" Lina asked, soft enough that only the clip picked it up, out of habit more than need. Her fingers flexed once on the strap of her bag. "Head?"

He checked himself the way RAE taught him: behind the eyes, down the spine, the base of the skull. The familiar ache waited, but it wasn’t sharpening. "Yellow bordering on fine," he said. "No spike. Just… more edges."

"Logged," said RAE. The word brushed the inside of his skull like a light thumbprint. "If you approach Red, I will attempt to warn you before I go quiet."

"Encouraging," Milo said. "A control condition where the control is 'nothing in our ears.' Radical."

The busker finished a small flourish, let the sound scatter, then planted his feet. The bow came down in a long, straight line.

The note he pulled out of the saw was thinner than a violin’s, purer than a whistle—no vibrato, no wobble, just an unwavering line that seemed to walk directly into the bones of anything nearby that could carry it.

Jason felt it as a stripe in the air, narrow and bright. The whisper-clip gave a tiny, involuntary flicker against his skull, like a muscle facing a cold plunge.

"Approaching—" RAE began. *Threshold. External carriers dropping. Staying on your side only.*

The note tightened, climbing just enough.

The rest of the sentence never arrived for the others.

The world did not go quiet, but it went one voice short in the clips.

The rails still hummed, the signal box still ticked, the crowd still rustled. The problem was the shape of the space where her commentary should have been. It opened in his awareness like a door someone had taken off its hinges.

His pulse jumped. For a fraction of a second he waited for her to clear her throat metaphorically, correct herself, say something about thresholds.

What he got instead was a thin, wordless pressure at the back of his mind—*here* without commentary.

"Timer," Milo said automatically, thumb pressing the watch’s stopwatch pusher. The busker held the note, face relaxed, eyes half-closed, bow hand steady. The saw’s pure pitch made the crowd hush without knowing why. A child’s complaint dwindled. Even the fries seemed to crunch more quietly.

Jason sat up a little straighter on the bench. The clip felt heavier now that it carried nothing.

**Okay. No voice. Just you. Don’t freak out.** He said to himself.

His own thought sounded too loud, as if someone had turned it up to replace hers. He made himself trace the busker’s line, then walk away from it—to the crossing’s little speaker; to the rattle of a loose cover on the tram rail; to the whisper of coats when people shifted their weight.

Drills, not heroics. Milo’s mantra, not his.

He closed his eyes for a count of three and labeled what he heard the way they’d practiced in Milo’s kitchen, only now the labels had to come from his own mouth.

Rail. Beeper. Refrigerated kiosk. Overhead wire. Loose sign chain.

His throat worked on the last one. It came out anyway.

Nothing in the soundscape flared as wrong. The street just sat a little crooked, the way cities always did. The difference was that there was no extra layer catching his attention before his nerves did.

"Head?" Lina asked after a moment, keeping her voice so low it barely moved the air. Jason caught the way her gaze kept slipping back to his hands, checking for tremor.

"Fine," Jason said. The word felt like stepping onto glass to see if it would hold. "Better than the archive."

He didn’t say *I keep thinking she’s about to interrupt me.*

"You’ll miss her when something actually goes sideways," Milo said, eyes on the second hand as it crawled. "Right now it’s you, the city, and my excellent timekeeping."

The note held. It was so pure it started to feel like an object more than a sound—a slender rod laid across the crossing. The whisper-clip stayed dead. No static, no ghost-voice, nothing riding the rails toward him—only that small, internal knot where RAE was keeping herself folded in.

Jason purposely looked away from the busker. He watched a girl with wireless headphones dance mostly in time with something only she could hear; watched a cyclist decide against cutting diagonally through the crowd; watched a man in a municipal jacket check the time, sigh, and not hurry.

All of it normal. All of it happening while the part of his head that knew how to listen for wrongness sat in enforced darkness.

Lina’s gaze tracked the edges—the kiosk’s security camera, the tram operator’s mirror, the polished curve of the convex mirror above the intersection that turned everyone into a small, distorted truth. Her shoulders had inched higher toward her ears without her seeming to notice.

"Nothing obvious," she said, mostly for later. "No watchers who aren’t supposed to be bored here. No cars idling where they shouldn’t. No Gen-3 eyes on the poles. Just… people."

Just people, and the absence of a voice.

Relief and something sour tangled in Jason’s chest. If nothing happened now, it would prove they could survive these pockets of silence. If something did, they’d chosen the worst possible moment to prove it.

The busker’s bow finally eased. The pure note slipped a fraction, split into a chord with a hint of human effort in it, and then let go. Street noise rushed into the space like water back into a tide pool.

Milo’s thumb clicked the pusher the instant the pitch cracked. He glanced down. "Twenty-three," he reported quietly. "Full-on purity to first wobble. Let’s see how long the field feels weird after."

Jason waited.

His whisper-clip stayed dumb for four heartbeats, five. Six. The faint internal pressure of RAE stayed, but without words it might as well have been his own tension.

On seven, it woke like a foot tingling back from sleep—pins-and-needles awareness crawling in from the edge.

"I am back at full bandwidth," RAE said. "I… register that my presence dropped below your usual perception."

The tiny catch before *register* made Jason’s neck prickle.

"Welcome back," he said. His exhale stuttered halfway out before it remembered how to be smooth.

"Felt weirdly polite," Milo murmured. "Like you left the room so we could gossip about you."

"Did you?" RAE asked.

Lina snorted. The sound came out sharper than usual. "We didn’t have time. Play it again."

---

They ran the drill three more times from different positions: standing near the crosswalk pole; leaning against the tram shelter; closer to the busker, then farther back. Each time, the saw line carved the air; each time, RAE’s voice snuffed out within a breath of the note aligning with the clip’s invisible sweet spot.

Each time, the quiet landed differently.

From the shelter, the pure note rode the plastic roof and made the advertisement posters vibrate just enough to blur the models’ smiles. Jason found himself reading the emergency exit instructions three times, as if any of the words might suddenly matter.

From the crosswalk pole, the note turned the beepers modest melody into a tiny war of intervals. The world narrowed to two tones knifing past each other, his body tensing for a clash that never came.

Standing nearer the busker, he felt the sound in his sternum as much as in his ears. The silence of RAE on top of that made the pressure there sharp, like the air before a storm.

"Third run," Milo said, flipping the page on his pocket notebook. His handwriting had gotten tidier as the night cooled; Jason watched his fingers, grateful for something so human and small. "Onset: about one breath again. Hard silence: seventeen. Decay: eight. The universe enjoys consistency."

"Plus or minus busker stamina," Lina said.

Jason flexed his fingers slowly until the stiffness left them. The ache behind his eyes stayed where it belonged, a dull weight instead of a spike. "Head status… holding," he said. "No Red. Just that feeling when the power cuts and you’re waiting to see if it stays off."

Lina’s gaze flicked to him at that, quick and sharp, then away again. "We know where the fuse box is," she said. "You tell us if you see sparks."

A tram rolled through without stopping, empty except for the driver. Its lights turned the rails into two thin rivers. The busker nodded in time, then found the high note again.

"Last one," Lina said. "Then we let the man earn his coins without feeling like a lab rat."

"Agreed," Jason said.

Milo tapped the face of his watch twice, a private drumroll. "And then we get fries. For science. And to bribe your nervous system."

"My nervous system already signed the ethics form," Jason muttered, but the line came a little easier this time.

---

On the last run, Jason closed his eyes before the saw’s line arrived.

In the darkness behind his lids, the city’s sounds arranged themselves. The saw’s note drew a straight path through them and then pinned itself to the clips.

The silence of RAE came smoother this time, anticipated instead of sudden. It still managed to land in the part of his mind that remembered blackout sirens and the electric taste of the storm.

He noticed the exact instant his awareness stopped leaning on the tiny corrections she usually made: the way she would nudge his focus away from a flickering light; damp a draft through the clip; give him a word like *compressor* when his brain was still stuck on *hum.* That whole invisible scaffolding dropped away and left the soundscape standing on its own.

The world felt a hair rougher. Not hostile—just un-buffered. Like stepping out of shoes and realizing the floor was colder than you thought.

He let himself do the drills again, this time slower: naming sources, tracing their positions, checking which ones wanted attention and which were just noise. His heart stayed too fast, but it stayed *steady* fast.

He didn’t try to build anything clever on top of the exercise. He just *listened.*

Somewhere in his chest, where the storm’s lightning had once written its own grammar, a sense of alignment stirred—not with RAE, but with the street’s messy, honest pattern.

**This, too, is a kind of coupling,** he thought, and the words felt like they belonged to him instead of being dropped in from outside.

The saw’s line quivered, fractionally. Someone in the crowd coughed. The note bent and broke into a gentler chord. The silence of her lifted on a lag, like a curtain catching the breeze a moment after the door opened.

"Seven," Milo murmured, thumb on the stopwatch. "Field snaps back fast once he lets you breathe."

"Noise level?" Lina asked.

Jason opened his eyes. The world looked the same and not the same—the way a room did after you’d moved one piece of furniture and now couldn’t quite remember where it had been before. "Normal," he said. "Normal with side effects."

His shoulders slowly realized they could drop.

"Conclusion," Milo said, doing his best impression of an academic and only half gumming it up. "If we ever need RAE to vanish from anything sniffing the carrier bands, we need either a busker on staff or a device that can fake whatever this guy is doing."

"Or a collaborator who already works with pure lines," Lina said, half to herself.

Jason caught the shift in her tone. "Elyra."

"Elyra," Lina confirmed. "If the stories are even half true, she could hold a line that would make this look like a warm-up. I want to know what that does to you before we go knocking."

Milo flipped back through his notes until he found the page with the crooked glyph from CITY-OPS-DM-17. The slanted tail curled toward the margin like it wanted to climb off the paper.

Under the glow of the crossing’s signal, he sketched over it, tracing the line of the busker’s pure note in a long, straight stroke and then letting it bend into the glyph’s hook. The shapes didn’t match, not exactly. But something about the way the line refused to close felt the same.

"This is going to annoy me for weeks," he said, closing the notebook. "Excellent."

"Good," Lina said. "Annoyance is free energy. Terror just eats it. We’re staying on the right side of that line."

The tram bell rang again. People shuffled into place on the painted arrows. The busker finished his last held note with a quiet flourish, bowed slightly, and started packing up before the night dropped below the temperature where coins felt generous.

Jason slid off the bench. His legs were a shade less steady than he’d like. "We’re done," he said, more to the air than to anyone else.

The whisper-clip buzzed faintly at the edge of hearing, then settled.

"I have your data," RAE said. "And your heartbeat. Both are still here."

"Both are planning to stay," Jason said. The relief in his chest didn’t quite manage a joke, but it tried.

Milo shook his wrist out as if the watch had given him a cramp. "Fries? I have quiet-field fatigue and starch will cure it."

"Fries," Lina said. "Then we all go home and don’t chase glyphs in our dreams."

They crossed when the light allowed, shoes ticking on the painted lines. Behind them, the busker’s last note hung in the air longer than it had any right to, a pale thread fading into the city’s broader, rougher song.

For a breath, even after her voice had come back, Jason listened to that thread instead—and to the space it left when it went.
