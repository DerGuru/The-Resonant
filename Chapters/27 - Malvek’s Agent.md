## Malvek’s Agent

They chose the market café because it sounded like safety. Tile echo, milk steam, a fridge with a moral hum. The corner table caught a draft and enough noise to make secrets pass as weather. They looked like three citizens with jobs and receipts.

Jason ate salt first, then sugar, then the apology of a croissant flake off his shirt. Lina kept half an eye on the line, the other on the glazed door that reflected the street. Milo annotated a route printout that already had more doodles than map.

*Low band. Words only.*

**Copy. Keep it boring.**

“Why here?” Milo asked, then answered himself, tapping points on paper. “Bench‑spacing discipline. Flow study. And the pastries qualify as civic duty.”

“Also the lattice over the bridge,” Lina added. “If we have to move fast, we won’t look like a crew abandoning a stakeout. We’ll look like people with opinions about lamination.”

Jason’s phone sat face down between the cups: the prepaid they’d christened **figleaf**. Cash‑bought, no contacts, sticker residue like a disguise that forgot to try.

He felt the city nudging at the edge of his skull—less a pain than a pressure asking to be named. He sipped water until it forgot its question.

Lina’s gaze flicked to the door. “Okay. Once more. We’re not poking Mill‑4 in public. We are…”

“Documenting detours,” Milo said. “Normal shoes. Clipboard alibi.”

The bell over the door rang the way bells ring when new weather walks in wearing a suit.

A man paused just inside as if calibrating to the room. Not tall, not short. The kind of face that fit files: precise, meant to be photographed and stapled to an assessment. He wasn’t looking for them. He looked past them, then through them, then not at them at all. He ordered tea he would not finish and turned, like gravity had a plan.

Lina didn’t move. Her jaw did a tiny thing that meant she would move very quickly if she had to. Milo’s pen stopped. Jason set his cup down because hands are louder than eyes.

“Excuse me,” the man said. His voice had the courtesy of a warning sign. “Jason Fischer?”

**Red?**

*No. Observe. Keep your head narrow.*

Jason did not stand. Standing concedes ground. “That depends who’s asking.”

“Arendt,” the man said. No badge flash, just a name offered like a card you shouldn’t copy. “I work with Mr. Malvek’s office.”

The corner table learned how to be very still.

Arendt set his untouched saucer on their table uninvited and then, very deliberately, moved it half a centimeter—testing for reaction, seeing who flinched when the world changed by less than a thumb’s width. No one did.

“I’m on a break,” Jason said. “If this is official, we can schedule something through my manager.”

“That would be slower than is useful,” Arendt replied mildly. He took the chair opposite without asking, angling his body to keep the door and their reflections in the glass inside the same field. Polite. Tactical. “I won’t take long.”

Lina’s smile had edges. “We prefer short conversations in public.”

“Good policy,” Arendt said. “Mine as well.”

He placed a folded paper on the table—a printout of a municipal incident report. Three blanked rectangles, one time stamp. The paper smelled like a copier that had not forgiven its job.

“Yesterday,” Arendt said. “Intersection near Mill‑4. Several witnesses described a… disturbance. Nothing dramatic. I even dare it to say, it has been clean.” His eyes held on *clean* like it was both: a compliment and a problem. “A compression shell, if you speak that language.”

Milo made his face into a shrug. “We don’t speak that language.”

“Then I’ll keep it in citizen tounge,” Arendt said. “Someone did something precise and safe in a place where imprecision is common. I prefer that sort of person to keep doing what they’re doing. Preferably with predictability.”

Jason let a beat pass. “Is there a question in there?”

“A proposal,” Arendt said. “Protection in exchange for… visibility. No signatures. An understanding.”

Lina’s laugh was soft and shaped like a warning. “Protection from whom?”

“From those who confuse curiosity with consent,” Arendt said. “And from our own over‑eager colleagues.” He did not look at the fridge when it clicked; he did not need to. “Observe only. That’s all we want. For now.”

*Your pulse is high. Drink. Do not mirror his cadence.*

Jason drank. The water behaved.

“‘Observe only’ has a way of becoming ‘contain,’” he said. “Then ‘comply.’ Then ‘belong.’”

Arendt inclined his head. “Sometimes. Not always. You have my word.”

“The words of a stranger are heavy,” Lina said. “We don’t carry them for free.”

The man acknowledged the blow with a micro‑smile. “Then take this for free.” He tapped the report once. “There is attention on Mill‑4. Some of it is ours. Not all of it is careful.”

Milo slid the paper back with a fingertip. “Kind of you.”

“Practical of me,” Arendt corrected. “You are not on any list. I would prefer you to stay that way.”

*He is telling the truth about the list. He isn’t telling it all about preference.*

**Noted.**

Jason looked past Arendt, not at the door but at the plant in the corner that had learned to tolerate shade. Its leaves trembled once without draft. His chest did the small, old pull it did near the river.

“Suppose I say no,” he said.

Arendt folded his hands. “Then I finish my tea, we step outside at different times, and nothing changes today. I still watch. You still work on whatever you are working on.” He paused just long enough to measure Jason’s blink. “You look like someone who prefers to name his variables.”

Milo’s pen began moving again, as if possessed by bureaucracy. Lina did not blink.

“Variables,” Jason said, stalling because the word tasted like both a job and a trap. “Alright. You first.”

“I’m here alone. No tail,” Arendt said. “I approached here because cafés are courteous to small talks. Your friend on the left tracks exits and reflections. The prepaid between the cups is a burner. Your tolerances are higher in public noise; you train there. And no—” his mouth tilted “—I don’t know your teacher. I can only guess that you have one?”

*He is guessing well. Keep your face boring.*

Jason kept his face boring. “And your variables?”

“Only one that matters,” Arendt said. “Do you prefer to be surprised by institutional attention or consulted?”

Lina’s jaw moved again: the muscle that meant she was ready to stop being polite. Jason touched his cup.

**Lina: Don't.**

“We don’t sign understandings,” he said. “We sign receipts. If you have concerns, send them like any citizen. If you want to keep watching, do it like the weather and the traffic cameras: boring and predictable. We do not perform.”

Arendt nodded as if he had expected the shape of the refusal and was relieved to fit inside it. “Fair.” He stood, leaving the tea untouched and the heat paid. “If you notice anything that looks like an experiment you didn’t choose—” he slid a small, unbranded card across, embossed with a number that would only ever ring during daylight— “call. Or don’t. I will not call you.”

“Good,” Lina said.

He took a step, then another. At the door he paused, eyes flicking to the plant in the corner again. “Nice stability in here,” he said to no one. “Whatever you’re doing, keep choosing quality over force.”

He let himself out. The bell made the same sound it had made when he arrived, as if symmetry were part of his job.

For a few breaths, they all did their best impressions of furniture.

Milo drew a tiny smiley face next to **variables**. “He clocked figleaf. I hate him a little.”

"He read sticker glue and the way you parked it as a prop.", RAE added.

“Get in line,” Lina said, still watching the reflection in the glass even though it held only street.

“Bench scale?” Milo asked out of habit, because normal was a ritual you could choose.

“Zero‑point‑five,” Jason said. “Nobody lingers.”

---

They waited for a minute. That was part of the choreography: let the watchers watch you be uninteresting.

Milo blew out a breath. “On a scale of one to being buried in paperwork, where are we?”

“Hovering,” Lina said. “We were polite. He was a blade wrapped in napkins.”

Jason took the card, not to keep it, but to feel the symbol with his thumb. It was a simple notch and arc, designed to mean nothing until you’d seen it twice.

**Do we keep it?**

*Photograph; then drop it in a public recycler. The symbol is an internal assurance: ‘You are talking to us, not a copy of us.’ It is safer for you to remember the curve than to carry the paper.*

"RAE wants us to take a picture and get rid of the card".

Milo snapped a photo—on the shared junk handset, not on any phone that knew their names. He held up the card. “Farewell forever, item of plausible deniability.”

Jason looked through the window. Across the street, a woman laughed at nothing, then adjusted an earpiece that wasn’t there. A city worker moved cones with the slow authority of someone who had paperwork to bless the cones. The tram camera angled itself two degrees and decided to hold that angle, which was not how trams made decisions.

"Tail present; pressure low. They want to see if you bolt." RAE said.

“We won’t,” Lina said, standing. She left coins on the saucer with the neatness of a person making an offering to an ordinary god. “We finish our errand. Then we go two blocks the wrong way.”

“Three,” Milo said. “Let’s give the city a little dance.”

Jason slid the figleaf into his pocket, and with it, the promise they’d written on a napkin: ninety seconds, no recordings, not a syllable more than necessary.

At the door, he paused. The frame was old wood painted the respectable brown of institutions and cafés. On its inner edge, just above eye level, someone—or something—had left a faint, fine mark: a curve that wanted to be a spiral but stopped short, as if it believed in restraint, too.

“Marker,” Lina said softly.

Milo leaned in. “Not a blessing, not a curse. A ‘you were here, we were here, no one has to shout.’”

Jason didn’t touch it.

*A resonance marker for surveillance boundaries: ‘this doorway is seen.’ It will recur.*

**Should we erase it?**

*No. That would escalate. Document it and walk.*

He stepped through. The sunlight read him as a person with a job. Behind them, the door closed with the small civic sigh of hinges that had been oiled on schedule.

---

They turned right when the obvious route was left, let their reflections pass in three shop windows, and paused to argue—briefly and convincingly—about the difference between good and bad lamination.

“Good lamination doesn’t try so hard,” Milo declaimed. “It trusts the butter to do the work.”

“Bad lamination peacocks,” Lina said. “Shattered layers that taste like intention.”

“Intention is a flavor?” Jason asked.

“It is when someone wants you to notice their technique.”

The watchers, who were paid in patience, logged that and did not move the cones.

Halfway down the block, Jason felt a head‑tighten that wasn’t pain, just pressure. He swallowed, salt taste from memory.

*Slow channel until it eases. Less load on your skull.*

A ding from nowhere confirmed the carrier change; then the city made its own noises again.

They crossed at a light that had decided to serve pedestrians for once. A child dropped a pastry and watched gravity work; a dog believed a pigeon might be a good idea. The world held.

“Arendt was right about one thing,” Jason said when they were far enough away to be part of crowd noise. “Restraint is unusual.”

“Restraint is how you live to try again,” Lina said. “And how bystanders keep not dying.”

Milo adjusted his glasses. “Also, restraint annoys the kind of people who think all gifts should be loud.”

**We’ll keep it quiet,** Jason thought, and the thought hummed back to him like agreement from a room that liked its furniture where it was.
