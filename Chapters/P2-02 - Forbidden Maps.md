## Forbidden Maps

Something was going on in Old Sector Mill‑4. It didn’t add up and there was a whole list of reasons why, taped to Milo’s fridge - crooked and insistent as a toothache. Someone had printed a transit map and highlighted a bend that shouldn’t be there; someone else had scrawled **streetlights: out in a neat diagonal, three blocks long**. A third sticky note read: *bench clusters don’t match footfall*. That one was Milo’s, underlined twice and accompanied by a tiny doodle of a pigeon with an MBA.

“We’re not breaking in,” Lina said, pacing a thin groove between the counter and the window. “We’re walking like people who have errands and opinions about pastry.”

“Daylight. Normal shoes. A bag that crinkles,” Milo recited, tapping the paper. “And at least one believable argument about croissant lamination in case we need to stall.”

Jason leaned against the doorframe, letting their rhythm wash over him. The morning had the clean, brittle feel of a page torn from a notebook. Somewhere below the apartment, a construction crew hit rebar and swore; the sound rang in his ribs like a glass tapped by a spoon. He tried not to flinch.

*Your sensitivity to harmonics is trending upward. Good, but be careful with spikes.*

**I’m fine. Just noticing more.**

*Noticing is good. Pain is not. I will pace the overlay to your tolerance.*

Jason exhaled, nodding to no one in particular. Lina noticed anyway. She had the kind of attention that made you feel seen even when you weren’t sure you wanted to be.

“Routes,” she said, dropping a folded page on the table. “Primary: along Welter Street, turn at the bakery, loop past the park benches, down Klee. Secondary if something feels off: cut through the market lane and come at Mill‑4 from the tram stop.”

Milo bent over the page. “Park benches first?”

“Because the city put five where two would do,” Lina said. “Look at the spacing. That row was designed for people who don’t stay. It’s a corridor disguised as a rest.”

Milo whistled. “You’re going to make me afraid of furniture.”

Jason traced the route with his finger. The map’s crease split the corner of Mill‑4 like a healed scar. “We’re still guessing,” he said. “Could all be mundane - the lights, the benches, the detours. Budget, or laziness.”

“Sure,” Lina said. “And I’ll believe in coincidence after my third coffee.”

Jason’s phone lay face‑up on the table, screen dark. He thumbed the side button once. A small waveform glowed in the corner - no icon, no app name. He turned the volume down to a polite murmur and set the device between the three of them.

“RAE?” he said, as if calling into another room.

“Here,” said a voice from the phone’s tired speaker, clear and slightly clipped, like the world’s calmest newsreader. “I’m limited to low‑band output in your hardware. Assume people will hear only a buzz unless they stand very close.”

Milo brightened. “Morning, RAE.”

“Good morning, Milo.” A dry beat. “Concerning croissants: lamination arguments tend to escalate. Consider quiche.”

Lina’s mouth twitched. “Focus, please.”

Jason cleared his throat. “We’re not using any… advanced tools today. Just eyes, ears, and a bag of bread.”

“Affirmed,” RAE said. “From public records and surface observations, there are four anomalies near Mill‑4: lighting failures in a diagonal alignment; municipal work orders closed without corresponding field logs; transit signal timing that inserts micro‑delays at two crossings; and the bench placement Lina identified. None of these confirm anything. Combined, they warrant a walk.”

“See?” Lina said, lifting her chin. “A walk.”

Milo grabbed the reusable sack. “Okay,” he said, clapping once. “The public map says ‘blocked since X,’ the maintenance logs say ‘diverted to Y,’ and the citizen reports say, in summary, ‘the pavement is lying.’”

“We are not accusing pavement,” Lina said. “We are checking paperwork.”

“Right, right. We love paperwork.” Milo rapped the mirror’s frame. “This was a salvage from a studio move. The backing had a shallow cache glued in - some old tech’s idea of a portable stash. I found a drift of printed overlays and a hand‑label: ‘MASK.’ Not a mask like costume, a mask like - ”

“ - like hiding a seam,” Jason finished, leaning closer. The overlays were crisp where they should have been grubby, as if they had only ever been handled by neat ghosts. He held one to the light. A pedestrian symbol wore a discreet bandage where a walkway used to be.

"Not a hack," RAE said from the radiator with an apologetic hiss. *A hole. A policy taught to be blind. Someone trained the map not to see a class of changes.*

“Why?” Lina asked the radiator as if she had always asked questions of radiators. “Hide damage? Hide a route?”

"Both are plausible. My confidence is low without field observation. Your heads are valuable; I propose we do not argue with guesses. Daylight first," RAE said from the radiator.

“Daylight, normal shoes,” Jason said, and the phrase felt like tying a knot he could trust.

Milo gestured to the overlays. “We make prints, we take photos, we carry clipboards. We do not look like people solving a mystery. We look like citizens who like clipboards.”

Lina’s mouth softened. “I can do clipboards.”

They packed boring things: pens that clicked, snacks that crinkled, exactly one high‑visibility vest because three would look like a plan. When Milo reached for a second vest on instinct, Lina pushed his hand back with two fingers and a dry look. “We are not a crew. We are a coincidence.”

Before they left, Jason repeated the parameters as if the apartment were a witness. "Low bands only. If I need more, I’ll ask first."

"As agreed: if I say ‘Red,’ we stop."

"Red stops," the radiator said.

---

The street thinned into the quiet you only notice when it breaks. Pigeons negotiated their disagreements around park trash cans. A cyclist coasted through, making them take flight. The fifth bench on the west side faced nothing at all - no fountain, no view - just a fence with a poster for a jazz night that had been rescheduled three times. Milo sat, then stood, then sat again, counting under his breath.

“One‑one‑thousand, two‑one‑thousand,” he said. “Nobody lingers. People check phones, take a sip, bounce. Benches as throughput.”

Milo squinted at the empty slats. “On my Bench Scale, this is a zero‑point‑seven,” he said. “Zero is sip‑and‑split; five is a full‑on nap with pigeons standing guard.”

“Is that peer‑reviewed?” Jason asked.

“It has pigeons,” Milo said. “That’s peer enough.”

Lina didn’t slow. “Document it.”

“Already doing it.” He scribbled a tiny diploma on a pigeon in the margin of his route printout.

Old Sector Mill‑4 looked exactly like it should have: practical, unphotogenic, partly gentrified and partly still itself. A bakery made small gold miracles in a window; a shuttered ironworks refused to remember it had ever been anything but shut. They moved with the posture of people who had a reason to be wherever they were and also the moral immunity of clipboards.

“Bench scale?” Milo asked as they crossed a little square with the kind of public seating that forgave nothing.

“Seven out of ten,” Jason said. “Honest municipal. No back support. Moral clarity.”

Lina snorted. “Moral clarity?”

“Benches that try to be parks are liars,” Jason said, and found he was smiling for real.

Somewhere under the market awning, a busker found a high, held note; for that beat, RAE didn’t speak at all. The quiet sat clean in the sound.

They traced the line where the walkway should have tied the square to a service lane and then on to the mill’s riverside path. A new placard said the old route was closed for safety. A handwritten note taped under the placard added, in a believer’s script: **Use the street, not the gap.**

"I am staying on carriers only," RAE said through Milo’s travel mug when he tilted it as if to check the lid. "Say if my presence leans on your head.*"*

Jason scanned the curb seam, the way the paving stones near old conduits always had a different grain, the extra bolts where a barrier had been installed in a hurry by someone who loved bolts more than they loved measuring. His eyes wanted to trace the old line like a melody you only remember when you hum the wrong tune first.

They walked the block twice, slowly. On the second pass, Jason’s breathing shortened - not panic, not pain, just the sense that his head wanted a broader road.

He paused by a corner kiosk and bought water and a small paper twist of salted nuts. He sat on the low concrete step, breathing until the throb behind his eyes leveled out. The tin awning above them ticked with thermal stress like a patient metronome.

*Slow channel until your head says otherwise.*

**Slow channel,** Jason agreed, chewing salt like medicine.

“Anything?” Lina asked, soft enough not to draw the square’s attention.

“Something’s wrong in the way the signs talk to each other,” he said. “Not the words - the forbidding is legit. But the arrow logic is off. The detour sends you two blocks farther than necessary. It avoids the river edge as if the river were contagious.”

They followed the detour anyway, because rules make good alibis. A tram dinged somewhere ahead - bright, municipal, unbothered. Past the square, the path narrowed to a footbridge where a lattice of rusted iron carried its own tired geometry across a thin cut of water. Wind threaded the grid and made the rails hum.

"Hello through the lattice", said RAE, her voice a dry shimmer in the vibrating steel. "I’ll keep to consonants. Vowels carry farther."

“Carrier discipline, noted,” Milo murmured, scribbling. “Mug, bench, lattice.”

Jason set his palm near the handrail without touching. The hum tugged at his focus, inviting him to match it. His headache took a deliberate half‑step toward the door.

He shook his head once and moved on. Lina caught the gesture and reset their pace to something even a suspicious camera would find boring.

On the far side, the river should have rejoined the walkway. Instead, the paving stones dead‑ended into a barrier with fresh bolts and older stains. Beyond the waist‑high fence, the ground dipped into a shallow, unsanctioned desire path: the **gap** the note had warned against - a narrow ribbon of trampled earth that shaved minutes off a route if you were the sort of person who trusted your ankles.

Jason’s chest pulled inward like a hand had pressed there. Not pain. A pressure gradient.

"Jason," the lattice whispered, just once.

**Red,** he thought first; then he said it aloud, plain and human. “Red.”

They stopped as if they’d come to the edge of a crosswalk. Lina’s hand found his shoulder; Milo pivoted to face outward like he was admiring an unremarkable wall.

Salt, water, breath. The world steadied in increments the size of chewed almonds.

"Thank you for stopping," RAE said, barely a breath in the rail. "External field near the river has a shallow, irregular drift. It is not aiming for you. It is indifferent. But your internal tolerance is lower at rivers for now. We avoid sharp corners and watch your head."

“Civic intent read?” Jason asked under his breath.

"Protective, not malicious," RAE said. "The detour logic over‑avoids the river to keep pedestrians off the desire path. Whoever closed the route didn’t want to invite litigation or curiosity. The map’s blindness is policy, not trap. The field is simply… wrong nearby."

“Indifferent wrong,” Milo said. “My favorite kind.”

They backtracked to the safe sidewalk like the sort of people who obeyed signs even when no one was watching. Near the bakery, they took the long way around a delivery truck that had opinions about curbs. Jason’s pulse stopped counting itself quite so loudly in his ears.

---

The café at the market edge was all tile and steam and a refrigerator that hummed like it had decided to live a long, useful life. They took the corner table that always caught a draft. Nobody looked at them because everyone had real problems, like pastry choices.

"I can keep riding the fridge," RAE said, turning the hum into a whisper that only touched their table. "Debrief proposal: codify an open‑speech alibi. You will need it if I must speak in public for more than a sentence."

Lina nodded. “The mug works, the bench works, the lattice works - but if we need actual words out loud, we need a reason for a voice that isn’t ours.”

Jason tapped the face of his old phone. “Not this. Too many ties to me. We get a prepaid handset, cash, swap the SIM monthly.”

Milo’s pen hovered. “Rules?”

Jason counted on his fingers, the habit of a clerk making policy into steps. “Ninety‑second bursts max, with cooldown. Never record. Avoid echo chambers and glass; soft surfaces are our friends. I keep contact with the phone or with the carrier object it’s sitting on so RAE doesn’t have to push to reach me. If anyone asks, it’s a work phone and I hate it.”

"Affirmed. I will refuse to route through PA systems, transit cars, or anything with logging unless you say so out loud."

“Name,” Milo said, already grinning.

“Name?” Jason asked.

“For the phone,” Milo said. “So we can say, ‘hand me - ’”

“ - **figleaf,**” Lina said instantly, wicked and fond. “It hides our shame.”

Milo choked on a laugh. “Sold.”

Jason couldn’t help it; he laughed too, the kind that let air out of the day. “Figleaf it is.”

"Carrier phone alibi v0.1 established," the fridge hummed, pleased with itself.

They wrote the rules on a napkin and then rewrote them on a second napkin because Milo had smudged the first with butter. Jason underlined **Red stops** and **salt + water** because rituals make sense twice.

Outside, a tram chimed, right on schedule. The market door sighed. The line moved. The day kept pretending to be ordinary and mostly succeeded.

They paid in coins and left in the sequence of people who had somewhere boring to be. On the platform, the a tram eased to a halt. The security camera at it's entry blinked once - no recording light, just a single red eye that opened and closed like a thought. A servo whispered inside the shell and the lens drifted a few millimeters, tracking nothing. Milo still giggled. Coins chimed. A child tore a receipt into confetti. The camera gave a last, tidy click and settled.

**That’s Gen-3 temperament. Curious and too polite.**

*Agreed. Field signature matches the vendor family. Quality: thin. It asked “Is there order here?” and then measured its own noise.*

Jason put a palm on the metal rail as they stepped out, more habit than plan. The rail’s hum was ordinary - city power with a winter flatness.

**Do we answer?**

*No. We pass as weather. If it looks again, we still pass as weather.*

The front glass threw back their shapes and the street’s; nothing else joined. The red eye stayed shut.

The trams's doors sighed. Its speaker crackled with an apologetic *Stand clear*. RAE stepped into the tiniest gap in the announcement.

"Bye," she said.

No one turned.

Jason felt the word nest under his ribs like a kept promise. They stepped on, three citizens with jobs and receipts, and the city accepted their ticket without remark.
