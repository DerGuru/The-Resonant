## Look out

Jason sat cross‑legged on the floor of his apartment, elbows on his knees, listening to the faint hum of the radiator.

“This is going to eat me alive if I don’t try it.” Jason spoke aloud.

*Better to burn calories here than burn bridges later.*

Jason tapped his knee. “They’ll notice. Milo especially. He listens for seams.”

*Then give them a seam worth keeping.*

Jason shook his head. “It has to be invisible. Like nothing’s there.”

*Transparent film. Thin curve. Think three‑dimensional print, not clumsy welds.*

Jason muttered, half to himself, “Minimal visibility. Just enough pressure to work.”

> Gentle clasp. Skin forgets it’s there in seconds.

Jason rubbed his forehead, already imagining the shape: a loop so slim it could vanish against skin, barely more than light made solid. He would need to print it in secret, a design simple enough to pass for nothing at all.&#x20;

---

Later that day, he met Lina and Milo in a quiet rehearsal room off a disused corridor. The blinds were half‑drawn, paint peeling on the walls. Jason closed the door behind them, the faint echo of their steps vanishing into stillness.

He rubbed the back of his neck and asked, quiet but firm:

“Do you trust me?”

They exchanged a glance. Milo answered first, quick: “Of course.” Lina followed with a slower nod, eyes steady.

Jason moved closer, something thin and almost invisible glinting between his fingers. “Then hold still.”

He leaned in by Lina’s ear. A light pressure slipped behind the curve of her ear, cool and weightless. He repeated the gesture with Milo, who flinched at first, then let it settle. Neither of them could quite see what he had done—only feel a faint clasp.

Jason stepped away, across the room.

**Are you ready, RAE?**

*Ready, when you are*

**Project my voice to both of them: Can you hear me?**

Both heads snapped up. Milo mouthed a curse. Lina blinked, then whispered back into the empty air, “Yes. You sound—strange. Like you’re speaking through glass.” She glanced at Jason again, startled. “But you didn’t even move your lips.”

Jason grinned. “Good. Now switch with me. One at a time.”

They traded places. First Milo, standing where Jason had been, murmured toward the wall. Lina’s eyes widened at the sound of his voice threading straight into her skull. Then Lina tried, and Milo laughed under his breath, delighted and unsettled at once.

Lina spun toward the center and began humming, soft at first, then louder. Jason lifted his head, eyes bright. “I hear it—every note, clear as if you’re right here,” he said, tapping his temple. Milo clutched his stomach, laughing. “You’re telling me that got through too?”

“Try it from over there,” Jason urged. Milo darted to the far corner, cupped his hand and whispered something low. Lina’s face flushed and she shot him a glare. Jason snorted, unable to hold back. “Yes, I heard that too.”

Their laughter ricocheted off the walls, each trading places, whispering nonsense, singing scraps of tune until Jason staggered slightly and pressed a hand to his temple. “That’s it. Ten meters. Any farther and it hurts.”

They regrouped, still grinning, breathless with the surprise. Milo held a hand behind his ear, tracing the near‑invisible curve. “What do we even call these things?”

Jason shrugged. Lina said it first: “Whisper Clips.”

---

The next morning they packed light and headed for the public depot. Jason carried the thin film the dealer had slipped to him, sealed between cardboard. He hadn’t mentioned it since, but the list in his notebook had grown claws. Overlay. Depot. Sheets. Photographs. Compare.

The depot was a grid of tables under harsh light, each stacked with official register copies of the city maps. The air smelled of dust and old glue. Clerks moved papers in silence, the occasional stamp echoing like a metronome.

Jason unfolded the overlay and smoothed it across a sheet. Two notches, here and here, lined up with the city grid. For a moment nothing happened. Then the lines refused to sit clean; they shivered, offset just enough to make his stomach lurch. Milo grabbed the table edge. Lina inhaled sharply, a hand on her chest.

"Mismatch confirmed.", RAE pronounced, "Their loops don’t match the sheets. Someone redrew the ground we’re standing on."

Jason peeled the film away, folded it back between the cardboard. Milo whispered, still pale, “So the dealer wasn’t bluffing.”

“No,” Jason said. “Which means someone’s been running maps under the floorboards.”

Lina traced the edges of the sheet again, brow furrowed. “If they can redraw the city, what else have they been shifting?”

Jason tapped the cardboard packet. “This proves we’ve got the right tool. Now we use it—street by street if we have to. But we need cover while we compare.”

Milo exhaled slowly, then half‑smiled. “Cover’s easy. Coffee.”

They left the depot with more questions than answers, the taste of paper and glue still in their mouths. Milo suggested the café, neutral ground where they could breathe. Lina agreed—it was public, crowded, safer. Jason didn’t argue. He felt the overlay’s weight in his pocket, heavier than it should have been.
