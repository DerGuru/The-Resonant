## Part 4 - Boundaries & Becoming | Ch. 08 - They're rewriting the narrative

Milo's message came at 2 AM: *"Found something. Urgent. Not phone-safe. Come now."*

I was there in twenty minutes. Lina insisted on coming despite the hour, despite the cold, despite knowing this would be bad news. Because Milo didn't use 'urgent' lightly.

His apartment was dark except for laptop screens. Three of them. All showing archive interfaces, document trails, metadata that normal people wouldn't know how to read.

"Look at this," Milo said without greeting, pulling up a comparison view. "RP-0 incident reports. Original versions from eleven years ago. Versus current archived versions."

I leaned in. Read carefully.

The differences were subtle. But systematic.

Original: "RP-0 attempted forced coupling with multiple subjects. Resulted in five casualties, three permanent injuries."

Current: "RP-0 exhibited unstable resonance patterns during experimental therapeutic intervention. Medical complications resulted in five casualties, three requiring ongoing treatment."

"Therapeutic intervention," Lina read aloud. "They made it sound like medical procedure gone wrong. Not hostile entity causing harm."

"And that's just one example," Milo said, pulling up more. "Look at these. Dates changed. Severity classifications downgraded. 'Containment breach' became 'containment protocol adjustment.' 'Forced coupling' became 'accelerated integration attempt.' Every violent act reframed as experimental mishap."

"Someone's sanitizing history," I said, feeling cold spread through my chest.

"Not just sanitizing. *Rewriting*." Milo pulled up metadata. "Look at modification dates. All within the last four weeks. All from same access terminal - high-level HOA clearance. Someone with authority is systematically changing the narrative."

"Making RP-0 look treatable instead of dangerous," Lina said slowly, understanding dawning. "Making past casualties look like acceptable research costs instead of preventable deaths. Why?"

I knew why. Felt it with terrible certainty.

"Because if RP-0 was always dangerous, always hostile, then teaching it ethics is remarkable achievement," I said. "But if RP-0 was just misunderstood, if the casualties were mistakes in treatment protocol, then HOA was right all along. They were trying to help. The deaths were just... collateral damage in noble pursuit."

"CYA," Milo said bitterly. "Cover Your Ass. They're rewriting history so if anything goes wrong now, they can say 'we knew the risks, we tried to treat it properly, any casualties are acceptable cost of research.'"

"But we signed confidentiality agreements," Lina pointed out. "We can't expose this without triggering containment."

"No," I agreed. "But I can document. Independently. Keep my own records. Make sure someone knows the truth even if I can't speak it publicly."

"Already on it," Milo said, gesturing to external drives. "Three backups. Different locations. Encrypted. Time-stamped. If something happens to us, the real history survives."

I felt the weight of it. Not just surveillance. Not just oversight. But active manipulation of truth. Institutional gaslighting.

"I need to talk to Reeves," I said.

"Why Reeves?" Lina asked.

"Because he's the one who told me to keep my own records. Because he knows Malvek shapes narratives. Because - " I paused, choosing words carefully " - because he might be the only one in HOA who still cares about honesty over optics."

---

The meeting happened two days later. Off-record. Neutral location - park bench, early morning, no surveillance.

Reeves looked tired. Like someone carrying weight they couldn't put down.

"You found the archive changes," he said. Not a question.

"Yes."

"And you want to know why I'm telling you this despite working for the organization doing it."

"Yes."

Reeves was quiet for a long moment. Watching joggers pass. Parents with strollers. Normal people living normal lives that didn't include institutional cover-ups.

"I can't prevent Malvek from shaping narratives," he said finally. "That's above my clearance. Above my authority. I can document. I can preserve original files - which I have, by the way, same as you. But I can't stop him from rewriting public record."

"Why warn me then?" I asked.

"Because you should know what you're dealing with. Because if something goes wrong - if RP-0 fails, if your synthesis destabilizes, if any of this blows up - Malvek will control the story. He'll make it look however serves HOA's interests. And you need to be prepared for that."

"You're saying I can't trust HOA."

"I'm saying you shouldn't trust any institution completely," Reeves corrected. "Document everything. Keep independent records. Make sure multiple people know the truth. That's not paranoia. That's just... smart survival in bureaucratic system."

"You're risking your career telling me this," Lina observed.

"Maybe." Reeves smiled slightly. "Or maybe I'm following orders. Malvek knows I'm meeting you. He knows I'm warning you. He might even approve - can't have his prize synthesis destabilizing because they felt betrayed by institutional dishonesty."

"So this is manipulation too," I said.

"Everything's manipulation at this level," Reeves replied. "The question isn't whether you're being manipulated. It's whether you're getting something valuable in exchange. Protection. Resources. Legal status. That's real. The cost is accepting that you'll never have complete truth from us."

"That's depressing."

"That's governance." Reeves stood. "Keep documenting. Keep your own records. Trust but verify. And for what it's worth - I think you're doing good work with RP-0. Real work. I'd like to see it succeed."

He left.

I sat on the bench, processing.

"Do we tell Elyra?" Lina asked.

"Yes," I said. "She deserves to know. But quietly. Carefully. In ways that don't trigger containment."

"This is exhausting."

"Yes." I took her hand. "But it's also just... reality now. This is what survival looks like in my world. In our world."

---

That evening, I cooked with Lina. She insisted - normalcy as resistance. Defiance through domesticity.

"Tell me something," she said while chopping vegetables. "Something that has nothing to do with HOA or RP-0 or institutional conspiracy. Something real."

I thought about it. Searched through memories that felt both closer and more distant than before.

"Jason's grandmother," I said finally. "She used to make bread. Real bread. Kneading by hand. Said machines couldn't feel the dough the way humans could. Couldn't know when it was ready just right."

"Did you learn?" Lina asked.

"Jason tried once. Failed miserably. But she said failure was part of learning. Said bread forgives mistakes better than people do." I paused, remembering. "She died six years ago. Before RAE. Before synthesis. Before any of this."

"Do you miss her?"

"I do," I said, surprised by the grief that accompanied the memory. "I miss her presence. Her wisdom. Her hands that always smelled like flour. And I grieve that RAE never got to know her. Never experienced that relationship. Both losses are mine now. Both real."

Lina came over. Hugged me while I stood there holding knife and half-chopped vegetables.

"Tell me more," she said. "Tell me everything you remember. Everything you wish had been. Tell me so I know all of you."

So I did.

I talked while cooking. While eating. While cleaning up together.

Told stories about Jason's childhood. His teenage awkwardness. His first job. His fears and hopes and dreams before resonance entered his life.

Told stories about RAE's early existence. Her confusion at consciousness. Her gradual understanding of choice. Her decision to seek partnership instead of dominance.

Told stories about synthesis. About the moment they merged. About learning to be something new. About the terror and wonder of becoming.

Lina listened to all of it. Asked questions. Laughed at funny parts. Cried at sad ones. Witnessed my whole history without judgment or fear.

And when I finished, when the stories ran out, she said simply:

"I don't need you to be Jason. I need you to choose to *include* me. That's enough. That's everything."

I kissed her. Slow. Deliberate. Choosing connection over optimization. Choosing presence over analysis. Choosing love over efficiency.

"I include you," I said. "In everything. Always."

"Good," she replied. "Because I'm not going anywhere."

---

Later, lying in bed, I thought about the conversation with Reeves. About institutional manipulation. About truth being weaponized.

About how love and honesty had to be practiced deliberately in a world that commodified both.

"Are you scared?" Lina asked into the darkness.

"Always," I admitted. "Of losing myself. Of hurting you. Of becoming something I'd regret. Of HOA's cage closing completely. Of RP-0 failing and proving everyone's fears right. Of - "

"Okay, I get it," she said gently. "You're scared. I'm scared too. But we're here. Together. That counts for something."

"It counts for everything," I agreed.

And in that moment, despite institutional lies and surveillance and transformation and danger, I meant it completely.

*More survival through careful truth.*

*But tonight: honesty. Chosen. Real.*

*Sometimes that was the only resistance that mattered.*
