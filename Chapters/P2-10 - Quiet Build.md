## Quiet Build

They started the day with a new batch of refined ear‑hooks. They’d been using the clear loops for a week. Lina tipped the little parts bag onto the table: transparent ear‑hooks like minimalist spectacles, a pair of ceramic discs no bigger than shirt buttons, two lengths of clear polymer with a shy matte to them, and a spring arm in titanium that looked like jewelry pretending not to be.

“Please tell me we’re not inventing hearing aids,” Milo said. “Because I already failed two tutorials and one grandma.”

“They’re not hearing aids,” Lina said, sorting pieces by feel. “They’re excuses.”

“Excuses,” Jason repeated.

“Things people don’t stare at,” she said. “Good coupling, low visibility.” She glanced up, eyes flicking to his. “And you’ll be wearing one.”

Jason turned the hook in his fingers. Up close, the transparency was imperfect - the edges caught light; the ceramic puck had a faint, milky swirl in it like skimmed tea. The hook wanted to sit behind the ear; the spring arm wanted to press two points at once: the soft back side of the ear, and the bone just behind it.

“The mastoid,” Lina said, because naming a thing tamed it. “If the spreader sits right, you’ll feel a bone‑buzz when she talks.”

“Can we not call it bone‑buzz?” Milo asked. “That sounds like a cheap energy drink.”

“Fine,” Lina said. “Buzz de Luxe.”

She fitted the first clip behind her own ear, practiced, efficient. The hook nested against cartilage and hair. She adjusted the micro‑ratchet two clicks and winced, not in pain, but in honesty. “Okay. Snug.” She touched her jaw and felt for the puck.&#x20;

Jason eased a clip behind his ear and found the exact point on the mastoid where pressure folded into presence. The room’s hum shifted, not louder, just legible. His fingertips relaxed without meaning to.

Milo hovered like a man watching a friend try on bravery. “If this is the part where I swallow a pebble, I’m out.”

“It’s bone conduction, not alchemy,” Lina said, grinning. “Ear or temple hook. No molars today unless you want to chew your syllables.”

Jason rolled his shoulder to settle the hook. “How obvious?”

Lina moved around him, checking from angles a camera might choose. “If you know, you know. Otherwise: it reads as eyewear hardware. Invisible enough.”

He nodded. The fear wasn’t of being seen; it was of being known too soon.

He lifted a hand, palm down: ready.

“Low band only,” he said. “No visuals. If I say *Red*, we stop.”

“Salt, water,” Lina prompted, the liturgy now muscle‑memory. She pushed the glass toward him.

He salted. He drank. Then he tapped the table twice - "Floor: Lina\*"\*.

“Low tide,” Lina said, the join phrase quiet as breath.

“Low tide,” Milo echoed, and flipped his clipboard open to a blank page labeled RULES WE WILL FORGET UNLESS WRITTEN.

RAE’s voice came through **Lina’s whisper‑clip**, not out of the air but *at* the bone, private and precise: “Hello.”

Milo blinked. “That’s… clean.”

“Clip beats room,” Lina murmured, pleased and a little proud. “Speak, and I’ll ride it.”

“Confirm fit,” RAE said. “Lina?”

“Present.”

“Jason?”

“Present,” he said, surprised by the warmth in the word.

“Milo?”

Milo fumbled with his own clip, ears more ideas than anatomy. Lina took the piece with a gentleness that didn’t read as tender until it was gone and snug again behind his ear. She nudged the ceramic disc five millimeters and tilted his head by two fingers at the jaw. “There,” she said. “Say if it aches.”

Milo tapped the clip once, tentative. “Uh - bone‑buzz?”

“Private reception stable,” RAE said. “No air leak detected. We are half‑duplex by design: one floor live, others dimmed. Tap to request floor; name the handover.”

“Floor: Lina,” Milo said, playing the game. “Your kitchen looks like a watch exploded.”

“Because you keep fidgeting the springs into constellations,” she said. “Floor: Jason.”

“Okay,” Jason said, settling. “Cost curves. Say them out loud.”

“Base load for one stream is low,” RAE said. “Two streams stable for several minutes at this range. Three streams permissible only in short pulses, then cooldown. If sustained, symptoms begin: You will feel pressure behind eyes, brow tension, and maybe even a headache. At onset of headache, you cut the mesh or shrink radius.”

“Radius,” Milo repeated, writing. “Ten meters for now?”

“Reliable,” RAE said. “More with training. Less if you run.”

“Noted,” Lina said, rubbing Jason’s shoulder with the back of her knuckles as if smoothing a thought. “We stay still. We breathe.”

Jason took stock of his head like a careful clerk. **You with me?**

*With you. Slow channel.*

**If I start to pull, you say it first.**

*I will.*

---

Lina made it a ritual because bodies respect rituals more than orders. She stood behind Jason, palms warm from the tea mug, and adjusted the micro‑ratchet by a single click. “Too much?”

“It’s there, not sharp,” he said. “Like a first step of a thought.”

“Good,” she said. “Buzz de Luxe at one.” She set her fingers to his jaw - thumb light on the hinge, guiding his chin a degree down and back. “Loose here,” she said. “If you clench, RAE has to shove. Slack jaw gives her a cleaner bone path along the mandible into the mastoid - clearer at less push.” He let his tongue drop from the roof of his mouth and swallowed; pressure equalized and the contact snapped cleaner. “Now it’s one‑point‑five.”&#x20;

Milo wrote **mastoid + relaxed jaw = cleaner** on his page like a brand new truth.

“Wind is noise,” Lina said. “Rain adds weight. Scarves over the clip make RAE push. If she pushes, Jason pays.”

“So we don’t wear beanies,” Milo said, offended by a fashion he didn’t own. “Visibility versus stability.”

“Exactly,” Lina said. “We pick: good camouflage or good coupling. Today, coupling wins.”

Jason turned his head slowly. The phase jitter he’d felt before - the way RAE’s presence smeared if he moved too fast - stayed small, a ripple rather than a tear. He matched his motion to that ripple and the ripple smiled back.

“Bone‑buzz confirmed,” RAE said, with a dryness that made Milo snort. “Contact is king.”

“Queen,” Lina said.

“Queen,” RAE confirmed.

Lina slid her hair forward, checked herself in the dark of the window. A shadow of hardware showed if she leaned into the light just so; otherwise, it read as habit. She flicked the hook with a nail and felt the tiny answer come back through bone. “Okay,” she said to the room and the day. “We can be boring in public.”

“Before we go public,” Milo said, “rules.” He read from his clipboard like a man at a podium in a town without podiums. “One: floor control. Only one voice live; others dim. Two: handshake on join, *low tide*. Three: tap gesture to request floor. Four: **Red stops** - anyone can call it."

Lina andded "Five: we do bursts, not monologues. If we need to infodump, we write a note, we fold it, we eat it later.”

“Six,” Jason said, “we keep it small unless I say otherwise. Words over structures. Precision before force.”

Milo scribbled **precision > force** twice, then drew a skull and crossbones next to **three streams = less than a minute**.

“Understood,” RAE said. “I will refuse cleverness unless asked.”

“Put that on a t‑shirt,” Milo said.

“Floor: Jason,” Lina said, businesslike. “Walk test.”

---

They did a loop of the building to teach the clips their people. In the stairwell, RAE rode the iron handrail and made it hum on consonants when one of them touched it. In the hall, she borrowed the return air grille and spoke exactly at their ankles. At Mrs. Hanley’s door, she went quiet because her music - the morning’s sustained whistle while watering plants - was a pure note that made RAE prudent.

“Field hygiene,” Lina murmured. “If it hums steady, don’t talk steady. Use brief packets off rhythm or change the thing that’s vibrating.”

“Rule nine,” Milo said, grimly delighted. “Make it sound like I came up with it.”

They came back into the kitchen. Jason’s head felt awake, not sharpened to a point, just more himself.

**Status?**

*Your tolerance is good today. Two streams are fine. Three will push you. I suggest we test three once and then consent to being sensible.*

**That last part was for Milo.**

*Yes.*

“Three‑stream drill,” Jason said, reluctantly. “Short burst, then cooldown. We do this once.”

Milo held up two fingers. “On your count.”

“Floor: Lina,” Jason said. “You launch.”

“Low tide,” she said, and breathed him into time like she did before a difficult dish: in for four, hold, out for six. He matched, not because she asked, but because his body liked obeying competence.

“Floor to Milo,” she said, tapping her clip - one, then two.

“Live,” Milo said, and for a heartbeat there were three voices on the line - Jason’s, Lina’s, RAE mixing and relaying timbres so each landed on the right bone with the right weight.

It was clean for exactly three‑quarters of a second. Then the room crowded and Jason felt the first push of pressure behind his eyes, his hands becoming fists.

“Red,” he said, plain.

Silence came like a mercy with good timing. The fridge remembered it could hum. The table remembered it was wood.

Milo wrote **1.8 s at 8 m** and underlined **cooldown 2 min**.

Lina put a palm on the back of Jason’s neck, grounding him on purpose. He rolled his shoulders - headache denied, pressure stepping back down the ladder.

“Cost curve confirmed,” RAE said. “Two streams steady; the third only in pulses with recovery. If we must run three, we move closer and shrink radius.”

“Good,” Jason said. He let himself sit, because pretending, he didn’t need to, proved nothing to anyone that mattered. Salt, water. The pressure retreated to the stage wings and refused a curtain call.

---

They named their lies because good lies are just tidied truths.

“If we have to talk in a room with people,” Lina said, “what’s the fig leaf?”

Milo raised a finger. “Prepaid handset. Cash, no account. Monthly SIM rotation. Ninety‑second bursts max; no recordings; soft surfaces; no glass; keep Jason skin‑contact with the phone or its carrier.”

“Carrier is a mug or a plate,” Jason added. “RAE speaks through that, not into air.”

“Figleaf,” Lina said, trying the name. It stuck at the corners, but in a useful way. “If anyone asks, it’s a work phone we hate.”

“I will refuse PA systems, transit cars, and anything that logs,” RAE said. “Unless you say otherwise out loud.”

“Bless you,” Milo said. “PA systems are where jokes go to die.”

“Floor: Jason,” Lina said. “Say the hard stop again.”

“If I say *Red*, we stop,” he said. “And if RAE says *Red*, we listen faster.”

“Logged,” RAE said. “I prefer your head attached.”

“Same,” Jason said, equal parts dry and sincere.

They sketched the etiquette on actual paper because paper keeps promises better than screens: **floor control; handshake; taps; Red;  short bursts; cooldown;  hush on a pure melody**. Milo pinned the page to the fridge with a magnet shaped like a lemon.

“Why a lemon?” Jason asked.

“Because when you forget the rules,” Milo said, “you'll make a sour face.”

Jason made the face on purpose so Milo didn’t have to catch him making it later. Lina laughed and the sound scratched at the beginning like her voice always did when she believed the day might just cooperate.

---

They took the mesh for a walk like you take a repaired bike down a calm street.  On the sidewalk, Lina called *floor* with a tap; Milo returned it with the light knock of a knuckle to his clip; Jason took it back by name because names make responsibility stick.

A tram sighed past. For four notes the world was a held line played by someone who loved purity; RAE went silent and nobody had to say why.

“Field’s quiet,” Jason said, mostly to test the taste of saying it. “I can hold two at ten meters without bargaining.”

“Small wins,” Milo said. “We stack them until there’s a staircase.”

They paused by a window and looked not at themselves, but at their hardware’s reflection. It took effort to see. In motion, in crowds, under city light, the clips vanished into the story of their faces.

“Good camouflage,” Lina said. “Good coupling. Pick both.”

“Greedy,” Milo said.

“Correct,” said RAE, and they could feel her smile in the word.

---

Back at the table, they wrote the rules once more, but finer.

- *One* voice live; others dim. Tap to request; name the handover.
- *Handshake on join:* “low tide.”
- *Red stops* - called by anyone, obeyed by all.
- *Two streams steady.* *Three* only in short pulses → **cooldown**.
- *Contact beats distance.* Fit check: mastoid + relaxed jaw; adjust micro‑ratchet one click at a time.
- Avoid pure, held notes; avoid steady external drives; use brief packets off the rhythm.
- Keep *words over structures*. **Precision > force**.
- Water + salt before strain; sit down before stubbornness.

Lina read each line like a recipe and watched for winces. Jason didn’t own any special bravery, but he honored truth like a tool: you kept it clean; you didn’t lie to it; and you put it back where it belonged.

He held up two fingers. “Run the last test.”

“Visibility,” Milo said, flipping a mirror against the wall. He stood in front of it and tried to see what strangers would. “I can’t see them unless I try,” he said, a little awed and sad all at once. “Secret competence should be illegal.”

“It is, somewhere,” Lina said, adjusting her hair so it *almost* hid the hook without dampening it. “But we’re not that somewhere.”

Jason’s reflection did what it always did - failed to argue back - and that was fine. He tilted his head. The clip read as a nothing. A choice kept private. A promise you didn’t have to sell.

“Exit image,” Milo said grandly. “Clips that aren’t there.”

“Clips that work,” Lina corrected. “Invisible is the bonus. Stability is the point.”

“Stability is the point,” Jason said, and the room agreed.

They cleaned the table because mess presses on the head, then ate the last two cookies because rituals require sugar or salt or both. When they finally stepped out, the clips lay against skin like they’d always belonged - nothing to see, everything to use.

Outside, the wind tried to become a steady tone and thought better of it. Inside, the fridge kept time. The day decided to be ordinary and, for once, meant it.
