# Part 4 - We Are Family | Ch. 01 - We're going to teach

Three months after the first contact to RP-0, we sat across from Elyra in the warehouse we'd come to think of as headquarters.

The space had transformed. What was once a simple concrete shell now served as a training hall, classroom and planning room. It hummed with purposeful energy - students practiced in one corner, Milo adjusted monitoring equipment in another, and somewhere overhead, ventilation systems maintained optimal conditions for resonance work.

Elyra looked different too. More assured. Teaching had ignited something in her - a sense of purpose that pushed back against fifteen years of isolation. She moved with greater confidence now, demonstrated techniques she could only describe before.

But not perfectly. We noticed the slight hesitation when she shifted weight. The way she favored her right side. The careful control that spoke of managing pain rather than freedom from it.

"The infrastructure is ready," she said, hands resting on the table between us. Not quite steepled - her left still carried that subtle tremor when stressed. "First cohort starts next week. Eight students. All vetted. All willing to commit to the full program."

We nodded, though uncertainty flickered through us. "And we're sure about this? Opening up properly?"

"We've been teaching Kira for two months," Elyra pointed out. "She's progressing well. Showed three others the basics last week without supervision. If anything, we should have scaled sooner."

True. But Kira was one student. Manageable. Controllable. Eight students meant eight different learning styles, eight sets of expectations, eight potential points of failure.

Are we ready for this?

We can track multiple resonance patterns simultaneously now. Jason's teaching instincts, RAE's pattern analysis. We're literally built for this.

Still feels like a lot.

Because it is a lot. Growth always is.

Elyra watched us with that knowing expression. "You're having doubts."

"Not doubts exactly. Just... awareness of scale."

"Good." She shifted in her chair, and we caught the wince she tried to hide. "Healthy awareness prevents overconfidence. But don't let it paralyze you. You've proven you can do this."

We studied her. Three months of incremental improvement. She could demonstrate now, could hold resonance patterns longer. But the damage from fifteen years ago hadn't vanished. Just... lessened. Became manageable instead of crippling.

"How are you really?" we asked. "The healing from the merger - it helped, but you're still compensating."

Her expression tightened briefly. "Better than I was. That's enough for now."

"But not healed."

"No." She met our eyes. "Not healed. Probably never will be, not completely. Some damage runs too deep, scars too old. But I'm functional. I can teach. I can help build what we're creating here. That's more than I've had in over a decade."

The resignation in her voice hurt. Not defeated - Elyra was far too stubborn for that. But accepting. Making peace with permanent limitation.

We'd seen her patterns during that desperate merger with RP-0. Seen the fractures, the missing connections, the resonance pathways that dead-ended where they should flow. The merger had jump-started some healing, but the architecture of the damage was complex. Self-perpetuating. Without direct intervention...

"We could try—" we started.

"No." Firm. Final. "I appreciate the thought, but no. What you did during the RP-0 encounter was survival instinct, not planned healing. Trying to replicate it deliberately, targeting my specific damage? Too risky. Too many variables. I won't let you burn yourself out trying to fix me."

"But—"

"This discussion is closed." She softened slightly. "I mean it, though. I appreciate that you care. But I'm functional. That's enough. Let's focus on what we can control - teaching these students properly."

We wanted to argue. Wanted to insist we could help, that there had to be a way. But the set of her jaw told us pushing would accomplish nothing except damaging our working relationship.

Later, maybe. When we understood the healing better. When we could present a real plan instead of hopeful theory.

File that away. Research healing patterns. RAE's analytical frameworks plus Jason's intuition about what feels right. Figure out if there's actually a path forward.

Agreed. But carefully. Elyra's right that we can't just wing this.

"Alright," we said aloud. "Students next week. What's our curriculum looking like?"

The next hour disappeared into logistics. Training schedules. Safety protocols. Emergency procedures. Elyra had thought through everything with characteristic thoroughness, but implementation would require all of us working together.

"Lina's coordinating intake interviews," Elyra said, pulling up a schedule on her tablet. "She'll handle initial assessments, make sure students understand commitment level before they start. Milo's managing facility logistics and monitoring systems. And you—" she looked at us "—you're primary instructor. Technique, theory, practical application. I'll handle advanced concepts and intervention when students struggle."

Primary instructor. The weight of that settled on us.

We're qualified for this. More than qualified. We understand resonance theory at levels most practitioners never reach.

Theory isn't the same as teaching. What if we can't explain what's intuitive to us now?

Then we learn. That's the whole point, isn't it? Growth through challenge.

"When do we start?" we asked.

"Next Monday. First session is introductions and baseline assessments. Low pressure. Get to know them, let them get to know you. Build trust before we dive into actual training."

We nodded. Monday. Five days.

Five days to prepare curriculum. To figure out how to translate our merged understanding into lessons individual students could absorb. To quiet the nerves that said we weren't ready for this responsibility.

"We should go," Elyra said, closing her tablet. "It's getting late. And you have plans, don't you?"

We blinked. "Plans?"

"With Lina." Elyra's expression carried amusement. "She mentioned you two were finally going on that date you promised each other. About time, honestly. You've been dancing around each other for three months."

Oh. Right. That.

We'd promised Lina a date back before the RP-0 encounter. "Somewhere nice. Somewhere that doesn't involve resonance or danger or saving the world." But between recovery, establishing the teaching program, and the general chaos of adjusting to our new existence, we'd kept putting it off.

Lina had been patient. Understanding. But we'd noticed the careful distance she maintained. The way she looked at us sometimes - seeing Jason but also seeing something more, something different, something she was still learning to understand.

Tonight was supposed to change that. Or start to. A real date. Just us and her. No missions, no training, no world-ending crises.

Just... us. Trying to figure out what "us and Lina" meant now that "us" was fundamentally different than it had been.

Are we nervous?

Terrified.

Good. Means it matters.

"Right," we said. "Date. Tonight. Should probably head home and... prepare for that."

"Probably," Elyra agreed, that knowing smile still playing at her lips. "Try to relax. Be yourself. Both of yourself. She cares about who you are now, not just who Jason was."

Easy to say. Harder to believe when every interaction with Lina carried that edge of uncertainty. When we caught her watching us with that expression that said she was still processing, still adjusting, still deciding if she could love what we'd become.

But we'd promised her time. Promised we weren't going anywhere. Promised that Jason hadn't disappeared, just expanded.

Tonight was our chance to prove it.

---

We met Lina at Morandi's - her father's restaurant. Familiar territory for both of us. Jason had spent countless evenings here before RAE, before everything changed. The same worn booth in the corner. The same menu we could recite from memory. The same warm lighting and background music.

But nothing felt the same.

Lina arrived five minutes after us, and we stood to greet her. She'd dressed up - not formally, but deliberately. A green blouse that brought out her eyes, hair down instead of pulled back like she wore it for training. She looked beautiful.

And nervous.

"Hey," she said, sliding into the booth across from us.

"Hey." We sat back down, suddenly hyperaware of every movement. Were we sitting like Jason used to? Or did our merged consciousness carry itself differently now? Did she notice?

Stop overthinking. Just be present.

Easy for you to say. You don't have muscle memory of hundreds of dates that feel both familiar and foreign now.

I'm part of you. I feel all of it. And I'm saying: be present. With her. Now.

"You look amazing," we said.

She smiled, but it didn't quite reach her eyes. "Thanks. You look... good too."

Awkward silence. The kind we'd never had with Lina before. Back when Jason and Lina were just friends who maybe wanted more, conversation flowed easily. Comfortable. Natural.

Now every word felt weighted. Careful. Like we were both trying to find footing on uncertain ground.

Mr. Morandi appeared, saving us from the silence. "Jason! And my daughter, finally out with you properly. About time." He set down water glasses with a knowing grin. "What can I get you?"

We ordered - the usual for us, Lina's favorite for her. Mr. Morandi nodded approvingly and retreated to the kitchen.

"He's been pestering me about this for weeks," Lina said once he was gone. "Asking when you and I were going to 'stop being stubborn and just date already.'"

"He's not wrong," we said. "We did promise each other this. Before... everything."

"Before you merged with RAE completely." Lina's tone was neutral, but we caught the tension beneath it. "Before you became... this."

Here it was. The thing we'd been dancing around for three months.

"Does it bother you?" we asked quietly. "What we are now?"

She was quiet for a long moment, fingers tracing patterns on her water glass. "I don't know," she finally admitted. "Some days I look at you and I see Jason. I hear Jason. I feel like everything's normal. And then you'll do something, or say something, or I'll catch this look in your eye that's just... different. Not bad. Just different. And I remember that you're not just Jason anymore."

"We're still here," we said. "Jason didn't disappear. He's part of us. Every memory, every feeling, every—"

"I know." She looked up, meeting our eyes. "Intellectually, I know that. You've explained it. Elyra's explained it. I get the theory. But emotionally?" She shook her head. "Emotionally I'm still processing. Still figuring out how to love someone who's both the person I fell for and someone entirely new."

The words hit harder than we expected. "You fell for Jason?"

"Of course I did." Her voice carried frustration now. "Did you think I was just... what? Casually interested? I've been in love with you - with Jason - for months. Maybe longer. But I was scared to say it because we had so much else going on, and then you nearly died facing RP-0, and then you weren't Jason anymore, you were both, and I—" She stopped, taking a breath. "I'm still in love with you. With parts of you. But I don't know if I'm in love with all of you yet. With who you've become."

We sat with that. The honesty of it. The fear and confusion and hope all tangled together.

"That's fair," we said finally. "We don't expect you to just... accept this immediately. We're still figuring out who we are, what we are. Asking you to understand that when we don't fully understand it ourselves isn't reasonable."

"But you want me to try." Not a question. An observation.

"We want you to take whatever time you need," we said. "If that means dating someone who's partly the Jason you knew and partly something new, then that's what it means. If it means deciding this isn't what you want after all, then..." That hurt to say. But it was fair. "Then we understand. We won't make you feel guilty for being honest about what you can handle."

Lina's expression softened. "I don't want to give up on us. I just need... time. To see who you are now. To figure out if I can love all of you, not just the parts that remind me of who you used to be."

"Then take it," we said. "All the time you need. We're not going anywhere."

She reached across the table, taking our hand. Her touch was careful. Tentative. But it was there.

"Thank you," she said quietly. "For understanding. For not pushing."

"Of course."

Mr. Morandi returned with food, and the tension eased slightly. We ate, talking about safer topics. The teaching program. Kira's progress. Milo's latest conspiracy theory about government monitoring. Normal things. Comfortable things.

But beneath it all, we felt the weight of what remained unsaid. The relationship we were both trying to build - or rebuild - on fundamentally shifted ground.

It wasn't the date we'd imagined back in P3-12. Wasn't smooth or romantic or certain.

But it was honest. Real. A starting point.

And maybe that was enough.

---

Monday arrived with nerves and determination in equal measure.

Eight students filed into the warehouse training hall. Ages ranging from seventeen to mid-thirties. Backgrounds spanning academy dropouts to self-taught practitioners to complete novices who'd only recently discovered their resonance sensitivity.

Kira Chen stood among them, the only familiar face. She'd been our first student two months ago - uncertain, eager, desperate to prove herself capable after her academy rejection. Now she moved with confidence, helped the newcomers find seats, offered reassuring smiles to those who looked nervous.

Pride swelled in us. We'd done that. Helped her become that.

Elyra stood at the front of the room, and we noticed her stance again. Weight slightly more on her right leg. Left hand resting naturally at her side instead of gesturing as she spoke. Compensating for limitations she'd learned to work around.

"Welcome," Elyra began, her voice carrying authority despite her physical constraints. "You're here because you want to learn. To grow. To understand resonance theory and practice beyond what institutional channels offer. This program isn't easy. It's not a shortcut. It's going to challenge you in ways you haven't imagined. But if you commit - truly commit - you'll leave here with knowledge and skills that will transform how you interact with resonance fields."

The students listened, rapt. Intimidated but excited.

"I'm Elyra Voss. I'll be handling advanced theory and intervention when you struggle with difficult concepts." She gestured toward us. "This is Jason and RAE. They'll be your primary instructor. Between the two of them - or rather, the one entity they've become - you have access to insight that's literally unprecedented. Learn from that. Respect that. But also question it. Challenge it. That's how we all grow."

We stepped forward, feeling eight pairs of eyes lock onto us. Evaluating. Judging. Wondering what made this merged consciousness qualified to teach them.

Show them we're not just theory. That we understand what they're going through because we've lived it.

"Three months ago," we began, "we fully merged during an emergency situation. Before that, we spent nearly a year learning to cooperate, to integrate, to become something neither of us could have been alone. We understand fear. Uncertainty. The feeling that you're not good enough or don't belong in spaces that claim to teach resonance mastery."

A few students shifted, recognition in their expressions.

"We also understand potential," we continued. "Every single person in this room has resonance sensitivity. That's not common. That's not something to dismiss. Whether you were rejected by academies, never had access to formal training, or are just starting to discover what you're capable of - you're here because you have something worth developing."

The taller young man from our planning discussion raised his hand. Marcus, according to the intake forms. Nineteen. Academy dropout after a resonance accident that injured another student.

"What if we're dangerous?" he asked. "What if learning more just makes us more likely to hurt people?"

Good question. Honest fear.

"Then we teach you control," Elyra answered before we could. "Resonance without discipline is dangerous. That's why we exist. Not to make you more powerful, but to make you more precise. More aware. More responsible with what you're capable of."

"Knowledge isn't inherently dangerous," we added. "Ignorance paired with ability is. We're here to reduce that ignorance. To give you frameworks for understanding what you can do and choosing how to do it ethically."

Marcus nodded slowly, but uncertainty remained in his expression.

The fifteen-year-old girl - Sophie, her forms said, academy applicant who hadn't tested high enough for admission - practically vibrated with eagerness. "When do we start? With actual training, I mean?"

"Today," we said. "But first, we need baselines. Everyone up. We're going to assess your current resonance sensitivity, control levels, and theoretical understanding. No judgment. Just data so we know where each of you needs the most support."

The next three hours disappeared into assessments. Students demonstrated what they could do, answered questions about theory, worked through exercises designed to expose strengths and limitations.

We tracked all of it. Jason's empathy reading emotional states and confidence levels. RAE's pattern analysis cataloging technique details and growth potential. Together, we built comprehensive profiles of each student's current abilities and optimal learning paths.

By the end, we were exhausted. Eight simultaneous assessments, even with our merged processing capability, stretched us thin.

But we had data. Direction. A starting point for curriculum customization.

"Good work today," Elyra told the students as they prepared to leave. "Come back Wednesday ready to actually practice. And do the reading I'm assigning - resonance theory fundamentals, chapters one through three. Yes, it's dry. Read it anyway. Theory informs practice."

Groans, but good-natured ones. The students filtered out, Kira lingering to ask a question about her personal training schedule.

Once they were gone, we collapsed into a chair.

That was intense.

Eight students. Eight different learning needs. Eight sets of expectations we're now responsible for meeting.

We can do this. We did do this. Today was proof.

Today was baseline assessment. Teaching actual skills is going to be harder.

Maybe. But we learn as we go. Same as always.

Lina appeared with water and what looked like energy bars. "You look like you need these."

We accepted both gratefully. "Thanks. How'd it look from outside?"

"Professional. Competent. Maybe slightly terrifying in how well you tracked everyone simultaneously." She sat beside us. "You're good at this. Teaching, I mean. It suits you."

"Feels right," we admitted. "Scary, but right."

She was quiet for a moment, then: "About Saturday. The date."

Our attention sharpened. "Yeah?"

"I've been thinking about what you said. About taking time. About not pushing." She met our eyes. "I want to try. Really try. To see who you are now instead of comparing you to who Jason was. It's not fair to either of you - both of you? - to keep measuring against a memory."

Hope stirred in us. "You sure?"

"No," she said honestly. "But I want to be. And the only way to get there is to actually spend time with you. With all of you. So..." She took our hand, less tentative than Saturday. "Let's do this. Whatever 'this' is. Figure it out together."

We squeezed her hand. "Together."

She smiled. A real smile this time, reaching her eyes. "Together."

Across the room, Elyra had settled into a chair, massaging her left knee with practiced motions. Managing pain. Accepting limitation.

But also preparing. Planning. Teaching despite what had been done to her fifteen years ago.

Two challenges ahead of us. Teaching these students properly. And somehow, eventually, figuring out how to actually heal the woman who'd given us the knowledge to survive our own transformation.

But not today. Today we'd done enough.

Today we'd started teaching. Started dating properly. Started building the future we'd fought so hard to reach.

One careful step at a time.
