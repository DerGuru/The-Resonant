# Part 4 - We Are Family | Ch. 07 - Planning together

The engagement changed nothing and everything simultaneously.

Our daily routine remained the same - teaching in afternoons, planning in mornings, evenings with Lina. But underneath the familiar patterns, awareness hummed: we were planning a wedding. Building toward marriage. Making permanent what had already felt permanent.

"Simple ceremony," Lina said over breakfast two days after the proposal. "Just team and students. Maybe thirty people total. Nothing elaborate."

"Agreed. Though your father will probably try to cater the entire thing himself."

"Definitely." She smiled. "But we let him. It makes him happy, and honestly, his cooking is better than anything we could hire."

We pulled up a calendar, looking at realistic timelines. "Two months? Gives us time to plan properly without rushing."

"And lets the second cohort get settled before we take time off for honeymoon." She paused. "We are taking a honeymoon, right?"

"We should. A week somewhere quiet. No teaching, no healing, no responsibilities. Just us."

"Perfect." She marked the calendar. "So we need venue, officiant, guest list finalized, and... rings. We still need rings."

Right. The detail we'd skipped during the spontaneous proposal.

"This weekend?" we suggested. "Ring shopping. Make it official."

"It's already official," she pointed out. "But yes. Let's make it traditional too."

---

Saturday found us in a small jewelry shop Elyra had recommended. The owner, an older woman with keen eyes and careful hands, listened to our requirements with patient attention.

"Simple," we explained. "Meaningful. Not ostentatious. Something that represents partnership rather than possession."

"And durable," Lina added. "We work with our hands - teaching, demonstrating techniques. Needs to withstand daily wear."

The owner nodded thoughtfully, then disappeared into the back. Returned with a selection of understated bands in various metals.

"These," she said, laying them out. "Platinum and titanium primarily. Simple designs but structurally sound. The kind that last decades with minimal maintenance."

We tried several options, finding the ones that felt right. Not too heavy, not too light. Not flashy but clearly intentional.

"These," Lina said finally, holding up a pair of matched platinum bands with subtle resonance pattern etching along the inner surface. "They're perfect."

We examined them closer. The etching was barely visible - a harmonic wave pattern that someone would only notice if they looked carefully. Elegant. Appropriate for resonance practitioners.

"How did you know we'd want resonance patterns?" we asked the owner.

She smiled. "Elyra mentioned you were practitioners. And I've been making jewelry for people in your field for forty years. You develop instinct for what resonates with different customers."

Pattern recognition. Applied to craft and relationships. We appreciated the parallel.

"We'll take them," we said.

The owner sized them properly, promised they'd be ready in a week. As we left, Lina took our hand.

"One more piece in place," she said. "We're really doing this."

"Having doubts?"

"No." She squeezed our hand. "Just... wonder. At how we got here. Three months ago you were still figuring out what merged consciousness meant. I was still processing whether I could love we instead of just Jason. And now we're planning a wedding."

"Fast progression," we agreed.

"But not rushed," she said firmly. "Fast and rushed are different. This feels like natural development. Like we found the right pace for us instead of following some external timeline."

"Careful steps," we said. "Just executed quickly once we knew the direction."

"Exactly."

We walked in comfortable silence, rings ordered, one more wedding detail complete.

---

Monday brought the second cohort.

Ten new students, ages ranging from nineteen to forty-two. Similar mix to the first group - academy dropouts, self-taught practitioners, people with potential but no formal access to training.

We gathered them in the training hall while first cohort practiced in the adjacent space. Intentional separation initially - let new students acclimate without feeling intimidated by more advanced work.

"Welcome," we said, studying the new faces. Nervous energy. Excitement. Uncertainty. All familiar from first cohort's initial session. "You're here because you want to learn. To grow. To understand resonance practice beyond self-teaching or institutional rejection. This program isn't easy. It will challenge you in ways you haven't imagined. But if you commit - truly commit - you'll leave with skills and understanding that transform how you interact with resonance fields."

Elyra moved forward, demonstrating a basic resonance pattern with the ease that still amazed us three months after her healing. "I'm Elyra Voss. I handle advanced theory and intervention. The instructors you see here - Jason and RAE, unified consciousness - they're your primary teachers. Learn from their unique perspective. Question it. Challenge it. That's how we all grow."

A young woman in the front row raised her hand. "Is it true you healed fifteen-year-old resonance damage? That you can facilitate healing no one else can?"

Elyra smiled. "Yes. They healed damage I'd accepted as permanent. But that's not why they're teaching you. They're here because they understand resonance at levels most practitioners never reach, and they know how to transmit that understanding. The healing is remarkable. The teaching is what matters for you."

"But if we get injured—" the woman pressed.

"Then we address it," we said. "But prevention is better than cure. We teach you control. Precision. Awareness of when you're pushing too hard. The goal is that you never need healing because you learned proper technique from the start."

The students settled, reassured but still slightly awed. We'd deal with that. First cohort had been the same initially - seeing us as extraordinary instead of just skilled. They'd learned to view us as teachers rather than miracles. Second cohort would too.

"Baseline assessments," Elyra announced. "Everyone up. We need to understand your current capabilities before we can design appropriate training."

The next three hours mirrored first cohort's initial session. Students demonstrated what they could do, answered theory questions, worked through exercises that exposed strengths and limitations.

We tracked everything with the same merged processing capability - Jason's empathy reading emotional states, RAE's analysis cataloging technical details. By the end, we had comprehensive profiles for each student.

"Good work today," we told them as they prepared to leave. "Come back Wednesday ready for actual training. And do the reading we're assigning - resonance theory fundamentals, chapters one through four. Yes, it's dry. Read it anyway."

Familiar groans. But good-natured ones.

After they left, Kira approached from where first cohort had been practicing.

"They seem promising," she observed. "Nervous but committed."

"Similar to how you were three months ago," we said.

"Was I that obvious?" She smiled. "I suppose I was. Desperate to prove myself after academy rejection. Terrified I'd fail here too."

"And now?"

"Now I'm confident. Capable. Ready to help teach basics to new students if you need assistance." She paused. "That's why I stayed after. To offer. I know you're planning expansion. Thought I might be able to help shoulder the load."

We studied her. Three months of intensive training had transformed the uncertain teenager into a confident practitioner. Not just skill development - actual growth in judgment and responsibility.

"We'd appreciate that," we said. "Start with demonstration assistance. Show new students what proper technique looks like. Answer basic questions. We supervise, but you handle initial guidance."

"Thank you." She grinned. "For trusting me. For seeing potential when the academy didn't."

"The academy's loss," we said simply. "You proved them wrong. Now help others do the same."

---

That evening, Lina and we sat on our apartment balcony again, watching the city lights.

"Second cohort seems solid," she observed. "And Kira's ready to assist. The expansion is actually working."

"Sustainable growth," we agreed. "Not overwhelming ourselves but not turning away people who need training."

"How does it feel?" she asked. "Building an institution instead of just teaching a few people?"

We considered. "Right. Intimidating, but right. Like we found something we're genuinely good at beyond surviving crises."

"You have." She rested her head on our shoulder. "Built something that matters. Something that will outlast us. That's legacy."

Legacy. Strange concept for a merged consciousness that shouldn't exist according to theory. But she was right. The teaching program would continue after us. Students we'd trained would train others. Knowledge and ethics would propagate.

"Two months until wedding," Lina said. "Then honeymoon. Then back to teaching with both cohorts running. Life is getting complicated."

"Complicated but full," we said. "Better than the alternative."

"Definitely better than the alternative." She turned to kiss us. "I'm glad we're doing this. Building this life together. Teaching, growing, helping people. It's exactly what I want."

"Us too."

We sat in comfortable silence, engaged couple planning a future that included teaching and partnership and legacy. Two months until marriage. Two cohorts to train. A program to build.

Complicated. Fulfilling. Right.

One careful step at a time.

Together.

Always together.
