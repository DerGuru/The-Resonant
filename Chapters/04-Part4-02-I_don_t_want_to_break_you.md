## Part 4 - Boundaries & Becoming | Ch. 02 - I don't want to break you

The HOA facility looked the same as always. Glass and steel. Institutional authority made architecture. But walking toward it now felt different.

We were different.

Lina walked beside us, hand loosely near ours but not touching—HOA might read physical contact as dependence, weakness, something to exploit. But close enough to matter. Close enough that we felt her presence steadying us.

"Ready?" she asked quietly.

"No." We checked our watch. 9:57 AM. Three minutes. "But we go anyway."

The entrance was monitored. Cameras, resonance scanners, probably half a dozen other systems we couldn't detect. They'd been watching since we left the apartment—we knew that now, could feel the distant pressure of surveillance like weight on our consciousness.

Two guards—not aggressive, but armed—fell into step beside us as we entered. Escort protocol. Professional. Polite. Utterly non-negotiable.

"Mr. Fischer. Ms. Morandi. This way, please."

We followed them through security. ID verification. Resonance scan—more invasive than before, measuring deeper, recording everything. We felt it probe our synthesis, catalog the integration depth, map the boundaries between Jason and RAE that no longer quite existed.

They were building a profile. Capability assessment. Threat evaluation. We weren't a threat—at least, we didn't want to be. But to them? We were the definition of threat. Unknown variables were always dangerous.

The guards led us to a different wing than before. Deeper. More secure. The walls here had suppression fields embedded—we felt them pressing against our awareness, dampening, constraining. Like trying to breathe through wet cloth.

"In here," the guard said, opening a door to a sterile examination room.

White walls. Medical equipment. Monitoring stations. And in the center, a single chair with restraint points.

Not activated. Not threatening. Just... there. A reminder of what could happen if we didn't comply.

Lina's jaw tightened. We felt her anger even without resonance perception—it radiated from her in waves we couldn't ignore.

"This is the check-in?" she asked coldly. "Looks more like containment prep."

"Standard assessment room," the guard replied neutrally. "Director Malvek will be here shortly. Please, have a seat."

We sat. Not in the restraint chair—in one of the normal chairs along the wall, sending a message we hoped they'd read: *We're cooperating, but we're not cattle.*

Lina sat beside us.

The guards left. Door closed. Locked. We heard the electromagnetic seal engage with a finality that made our chest tight.

"Fuck," Lina whispered.

"Agreed," we said.

---

Malvek arrived exactly at 10:00 AM. Reeves with him, tablet in hand, expression professionally neutral.

"Mr. Fischer. Ms. Morandi. Thank you for coming." Malvek's voice was measured. Calm. The voice of someone who held all the power and knew it.

"Did we have a choice?" we asked.

"Always." He sat across from us. Reeves remained standing, already taking notes. "Though declining would have... consequences. For both of you."

There it was. The cage pretending to be partnership. The leash they called cooperation.

"What do you want?" Lina asked, not bothering to hide her anger.

"Transparency." Malvek pulled up a holographic display. Resonance patterns—ours. Detailed. Invasive. Numbers and graphs showing integration depth, stability metrics, capability projections. "Your synthesis represents unprecedented integration. We need to understand it. Document it. Assess associated risks."

"Risks," we repeated flatly.

"You reached sixty-five percent integration in a matter of hours during the RP-0 confrontation. That kind of accelerated merger has never been observed in controlled conditions. We need baseline data to predict stability, longevity, potential degradation patterns." He gestured to the displays. "To ensure public safety."

*He's treating us like a research subject,* we thought.

*We are a research subject. We always were. The only question is whether we participate voluntarily or they make us.*

"What does 'baseline data' entail?" we asked aloud.

Reeves answered, reading from his tablet. "Cognitive assessment. Memory integrity testing. Response time analysis. Resonance capability mapping under controlled conditions. Standard post-integration protocols."

"How invasive?"

"Minimally. Non-contact methods where possible. Some direct resonance probing for structural analysis—similar to yesterday's preliminary scan, but more comprehensive." He paused. "You can refuse specific tests. But partial compliance will affect our risk assessment."

Translation: refuse, and we get classified as higher threat. Higher threat meant tighter restrictions. Tighter restrictions meant less freedom.

The cage tightening, presented as reasonable precaution.

Lina leaned forward. "And me? Why am I here?"

"Associated person status," Malvek said. "You're documented as Mr. Fischer's... partner." He made it a question, testing boundaries.

"Yes," we said firmly, not letting him define our relationship for us.

"Then you're part of the risk profile. We need to assess your exposure level, your role in his stability matrix, your contingency status if synthesis fails catastrophically."

"Contingency status?" Lina's voice went cold with understanding.

"Standard protocol," Reeves said quickly, trying to soften the blow. "If Mr. Fischer experiences catastrophic integration failure, you're the most likely person to be present. We need to know you can respond appropriately. That you understand the risks. That you won't... complicate containment if it becomes necessary."

*Translation,* part of us supplied bitterly. *If we die or go rogue, they want to know if Lina will cooperate with cleanup or become another problem to solve.*

We felt sick.

"This is fucked," Lina said flatly.

"This is necessary," Malvek corrected without heat. "You chose to associate with a post-synthesis hybrid. That comes with responsibilities. Documentation. Protocols. Safety measures—for your protection as much as anyone else's."

"For whose safety?" we asked. "Ours? Hers? The public's? Or just HOA's institutional ass-covering?"

Malvek met our eyes. Something that might have been respect flickered across his face. "Everyone's. Yours. Hers. The public's. Ours." He leaned back slightly. "I'm not trying to imprison you, Mr. Fischer. I'm trying to prevent catastrophe. And I need data to do that effectively. Real data. Honest data. Which requires your cooperation."

Reeves stepped forward. "May I be frank?"

We nodded, curious despite our anger.

"I don't want to break you," he said simply, gesturing to the restraint chair. "I want you to be *controllable*. That's not the same thing. That chair? Worst-case protocol. We don't want to use it. But we need to know we *can* if necessary. That's not malice. That's math. Risk assessment. Probability management."

"So we're always one mistake away from that chair," we said.

"Everyone is," Reeves replied. "The difference is, your mistakes could kill dozens. So yes, the margin for error is smaller. The oversight is tighter. The consequences are more severe. But that's not punishment. That's proportion."

Silence filled the room, broken only by the hum of monitoring equipment.

Lina took our hand. Squeezed once. Solidarity.

"What happens now?" we asked Malvek.

"Now, we conduct baseline assessment. Two hours. Cognitive tests, resonance mapping, stability analysis under controlled stress." He pulled up another screen showing a schedule. "And then we discuss the demonstration."

"Demonstration?" Lina asked sharply.

"Public. Controlled. Showing the city that synthesis can be safe. That post-merger individuals can function without posing existential risk." Malvek's expression didn't change. "We need transparency. Citizens need to see proof. You're the proof of concept."

"And if we refuse?" we asked.

"Then public fear increases. Regulations tighten. Other integrated individuals lose what few freedoms they have. The precedent you set—cooperation or resistance—affects everyone who comes after you." He paused, letting that sink in. "You succeeded where RP-0 failed. That matters. But only if we can demonstrate it publicly. Only if people can see that synthesis doesn't automatically mean danger."

Hostage logic. But also pragmatic logic. He was right—public perception drove policy. We could either shape that perception or let fear do it for us.

We looked at Lina. She looked back, reading the question in our expression.

"Together," she said quietly. "Whatever we choose, we choose together."

We turned back to Malvek.

"Two conditions," we said.

"Name them."

"One: Lina stays present for all tests. No separation protocols. No 'security concerns' keeping her out of the room."

Malvek considered. "Acceptable. Provided she signs comprehensive confidentiality agreements."

"Fine. Two: Full transparency works both ways. We get copies of all data. All assessments. All risk classifications. Every number you calculate, every conclusion you draw—we see it. No hidden profiles. No classified addendums we don't get to read."

Reeves frowned. "That's... highly unusual. Some assessment data is classified for operational security—"

"Non-negotiable," we interrupted. "You want partnership? Partnership requires mutual trust. That starts with mutual access. You evaluate us, we get to see the evaluation. Otherwise this isn't cooperation—it's just polite surveillance."

Malvek was quiet for a long moment, studying us with those sharp, analytical eyes.

"Agreed," he said finally. "But understand: misuse of classified data will trigger immediate containment. Share it with unauthorized parties, use it to circumvent security measures, compromise operational integrity—any of that, and the agreement ends. Permanently. Clear?"

"Crystal."

"Then let's begin." He stood. "Two hours. Then we discuss the demonstration and what's required from both of you. Ms. Morandi, confidentiality documents are on the tablet Agent Reeves is handing you."

Lina took the tablet. Scanned quickly, lips moving slightly as she read the dense legal language. Then signed without hesitation, stylus moving with firm strokes.

"Let's get this over with," she said.

---

The tests were invasive in ways that had nothing to do with physical contact.

Cognitive mapping. Memory verification across both consciousness streams. Resonance depth analysis measuring how thoroughly Jason and RAE had merged. Stability stress tests—suppression fields fluctuating while we maintained control, testing our limits, seeing where we might break.

They made us solve puzzles while monitoring neural patterns, trying to distinguish Jason-thoughts from RAE-thoughts, looking for the boundary that no longer quite existed. Made us recall specific memories while measuring consistency, checking for degradation or corruption. Made us demonstrate precise resonance control while external interference tried to disrupt us, seeing how well synthesis held under pressure.

Two hours felt like six.

But we passed. Every test. Every threshold. Every metric that mattered.

When Malvek reviewed the results, something that might have been genuine surprise crossed his face.

"Remarkable," he said quietly, scrolling through data. "Integration depth sixty-eight percent—increased from sixty-five since yesterday—yet cognitive distinction remains functionally intact. No measurable degradation. No parasitic consumption. True synthesis, not hostile takeover."

"We told you," we said tiredly.

"You did." He closed the displays. "Which brings us to the demonstration. Two weeks from today. Public venue. Media presence. You'll demonstrate controlled resonance capability. Show stability under observation. Answer questions from approved journalists. Prove that synthesis doesn't automatically equal danger."

"And Lina?"

"Participates. Stability variable. Shows that associated persons remain safe in proximity. That you can function in social contexts without posing risk to others." He pulled up a document. "Parameters are here. Review them. We reconvene in three days to finalize logistics."

We looked at Lina. She nodded, jaw set with determination.

"Fine," we said. "But one more condition."

Malvek raised an eyebrow, almost amused.

"After the demonstration—if we perform successfully, if we prove stability, if we give you your proof of concept—you back off. No weekly check-ins. No constant monitoring. Monthly check-ins, maximum. We prove we're safe, you give us space. Real partnership, not just surveillance with paperwork."

"Monthly check-ins," Malvek countered. "And immediate reporting of any instability. Integration increase above seventy-five percent. Capability expansion beyond documented baselines. Adverse effects on associated persons. Any of that triggers additional assessment. But yes—you prove you're safe, we reduce oversight significantly. Deal?"

We extended our hand. He shook it—firm grip, professional, a contract sealed without joy but with mutual understanding.

"Two weeks, Mr. Fischer. Make it count."

---

Outside, the sun felt too bright. The air too thin. Freedom conditional and constrained.

Lina walked silently beside us for three blocks before speaking.

"That was fucked up."

"Yes."

"Are you okay?"

"No." We stopped walking. Turned to face her. "But we will be. Eventually. Maybe."

"Together?" she asked.

"Together," we confirmed.

She hugged us. Hard. We held her back, feeling the solidity of her presence grounding us in physicality that synthesis sometimes made us forget.

"Two weeks," she said into our shoulder. "We show them we're not a threat. Then we get our lives back. Or as close as we'll ever get."

"If we can," we said quietly, voicing the fear we'd been suppressing.

"We will," she said firmly. "Because we choose to. Not because they let us. Because we choose it."

We pulled back. Looked at her face. Saw determination. Love. Defiance.

"We love you," we said.

"I know," she replied. "Now let's go home. I'm cooking. You're not optimizing anything. We're being deliberately inefficient."

Despite everything, we smiled.

"Deliberately inefficient. We can try."

"Good." She took our hand. "That's all I ask."

---

*The cage was real.*

*But so was choice.*

*And we chose her. Chose this. Chose us.*

*That would have to be enough.*
