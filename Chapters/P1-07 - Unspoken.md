## Unspoken

The restaurant was nearly empty, the late dinner crowd having long since trickled out. Lina leaned against the counter, fiddling with a half-filled salt shaker. Jason still sat in his usual seat, the warmth of a finished meal lingering on the table.

Outside, the streetlight flickered twice before holding steady.

Jason looked down at his cup. The tea had gone cold, but he wasn’t ready to leave yet. Something sat on his tongue, unspoken.

He turned the cup slowly. “Can I ask you something?”

Lina arched an eyebrow. “You just did.”

Jason huffed a quiet laugh. “I mean... something else. About the academy.”

She stilled.

“I know you don’t like talking about it,” he added quickly. “And I’m not trying to dig. It’s just - ” He paused, choosing his words. “I’ve been thinking. That place is expensive. And your dad’s restaurant... I mean, it’s good, but it’s not exactly silver-plated.”

She didn’t respond right away. The silence stretched just enough to make him consider retracting the question.

Then she said, “You’re not wrong.”

Jason looked up.

“My dad couldn’t afford it. Not by himself.” She paused, running a finger along the edge of the countertop. “I got noticed by someone. A tutor. Used to come in here for lunch - back when I was still a kid. Saw me doing some practice drawings in the ledger, figured I had a spark. Turned out, I did.”

Jason waited, sensing more.

“He sponsored me. Got me a partial scholarship. Enough to get in the door. The rest... my dad took care of. Loans. Deals. Promises I didn’t know about until later.”

Jason sat back slightly, letting the weight of that settle. “That must’ve been a lot.”

Lina gave a small smile. “Yeah. It was. More than I knew at the time.”

She crossed her arms, not defensive - just holding something in. “It wasn’t just about the money. My dad thought it would change everything. That if I did well there, maybe I wouldn’t end up tied to this place forever.”

“You didn’t like it?” Jason asked softly.

She didn’t answer immediately.

“I liked *some* things,” she said finally. “Learning to tune resonance, feeling it answer… that part felt like flying. But the people - ” Her voice tightened. “Most of them had no idea what it meant to *need* something. They just assumed they were born to it. I wasn’t.”

Jason nodded, slowly. “And when you … left”

“He didn’t say anything,” Lina said. “He just… cleared a space for me back here. Like he knew I’d come home.”

The quiet between them wasn’t awkward. Just full.

Jason looked at her carefully. “Thanks for telling me.”

Lina smirked faintly. “Don’t get used to it.”

Jason stood in the dimly lit corridor outside his apartment, mailbox key in hand. The overhead light flickered again - four blinks, then out. He gave it a glance, then shrugged and opened the rust-edged metal door marked 3C.

A handful of junk mail, a bill, and a local newsletter fell into his hands. He shuffled through it without interest and turned toward his door.

“Jason!”

The voice came from further down the hall, warm and familiar. Mrs. Amari stood outside her apartment, coaxing a stubborn potted plant back into its ceramic dish.

She was in her late fifties, with streaks of silver in her dark hair and a voice that always sounded like a smile. She wore house slippers and a faded sweater with a stitched patch of a bird on the shoulder.

Jason smiled, just slightly. “Good evening Mrs. Amari.”

“Don’t let the hallway lights fool you. It’s almost morning,” she teased. “Long shift?”

“Something like that.”

She wiped her hands on a towel and stepped closer. “You’re eating enough, right? You look like the wind could take you.”

He gave the usual polite chuckle. “I’m fine.”

“Mhm.” She gave him the look - half-motherly, half unconvinced - but didn’t press.

“You still working at the archive?”

Jason nodded. “Three days a week. Budget cuts.”

“They always cut the things that actually matter,” she muttered, then brightened. “If you ever need real food, you know I make too much.”

“I appreciate it,” he said, meaning it, though he never took her up on it.

She tilted her head. “You remind me of my youngest. Never stayed in one place. Always running.”

Jason folded the mail under his arm. “Did she ever slow down?”

Mrs. Amari smiled. “She moved to Montreal. Got herself a dog. I think that counts.”

---

Jason leaned against the wall, not quite ready to go inside. He didn’t mind talking to Mrs. Amari. She was one of the few people in the building who treated him like more than just background noise.

Still, there was always a line. A quiet barrier neither of them crossed.

“Anyway,” she said, brushing a bit of soil from her palm, “if the lights don’t get fixed soon, I’ll just start handing out candles.”

“I’ll bring matches,” Jason replied.

She grinned, then paused. “You know, I always thought you’d end up somewhere bigger.”

Jason blinked. “Bigger?”

“More exciting. You’ve got the eyes for it. Always thinking. Always somewhere else.”

Jason glanced down. “I guess I like it quiet.”

“Quiet’s fine,” she said. “But don’t let it keep you still.”

He nodded, then stepped toward his door. “Good night, Mrs. Amari.”

“Good night, Jason. Don’t forget to eat.”

“I won’t.”

He closed the door behind him and locked it with the soft click of practiced habit. Inside, his apartment was clean, functional, and devoid of personality. A shelf with books, most unopened. A desk. A worn armchair. The hum of the fridge.

He dropped the mail on the counter and stared for a moment at nothing in particular.

The hallway outside still echoed faintly with the fading scent of Mrs. Amari’s lavender cleaner.

Nothing was wrong.

Nothing was right, either.
