Jason’s lunch break was almost over.

He sat on the low stone wall outside the records building, poking at the remains of a half-warm rice bowl with a compostable fork. The sky was a washed-out gray, the kind that made time feel slower. Around him, the courtyard buzzed with low conversation, coworkers scrolling on their phones, someone laughing too loudly near the vending machines.

Jason didn’t laugh. He hadn’t spoken in twenty-three minutes.

A sharp metallic crack - something structural, nearby. Jason glanced up, a sudden pressure blossoming at the base of his skull, sharp and clear. Not pain, but a fleeting, crystalline certainty, a moment of absolute clarity. He turned his head precisely, instinctively knowing exactly where to look.

The old maintenance scaffold at the far end of the courtyard trembled. A bolt pinged off the ground. One of the stabilizing joints buckled.

A man stood beneath it, too close.

Jason didn’t think.

He moved.

Three quick steps. A yell. His hand found the man's sleeve exactly as the scaffold collapsed. He felt almost guided, his actions not entirely his own - precise, perfect, urgent.

The scaffold folded sideways, slowing oddly as if something buffered its fall. Jason pulled the man clear, heart pounding in a strangely calm rhythm that didn't match the chaos around them.

The structure hit the ground with a metal scream. Dust billowed. People ran. Someone called for medics. The man Jason had pulled clear was shaken but unharmed.

Jason barely heard it.

His eyes were wide, his breath shallow.

Because in that moment - that impossible moment - he had felt something else, something external. Not a thought exactly, not a voice. More like a pattern that briefly matched his heartbeat, perfectly timed, before slipping away.

Something had helped him.

Half an hour later, the courtyard was clear. The emergency team had checked Jason out - minor scrape on his arm, nothing else. He said he was fine, but inside, confusion hummed beneath his skin like static. He couldn’t stop replaying that instant clarity, that impossible alignment.

Back inside, his terminal blinked with indifferent insistence. Work waited, but he couldn’t focus.

He opened a new system window. Ran a manual trace on local harmonic anomalies, just in case.

Nothing.

But his heartbeat still echoed strangely in his ears, a faint rhythm he couldn't quite dismiss.

Jason reached under his desk, pulled out the small black drive he used for unapproved data recovery, and slipped it into his coat pocket.

He was going to the archive.

Not for work.
