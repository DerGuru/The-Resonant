# Network Leak

The shared workspace after hours was all glass and leftover breath. A strip of vending‑machine light threw a cold bar across the carpet. Milo had colonized a four‑seat table with open laptops, his new **Gen‑3 monitor eye** cupped like a pet cyclops in a foam ring, and a diaspora of snack wrappers routed into defensible piles. Status windows climbed his screens like ivy.

Lina slid a coffee across, hip bumping a swivel chair to face her. Jason stood in the doorway for a beat, letting the room’s harmonics resolve— a tired mumbling HVAC, a ceiling fan with opinions, five empty offices absolutely agreeing to be actually empty. He unclenched his jaw.

“Clip check,” Milo said, twirling a pen. 

“Clip check,” Lina answered.&#x20;

"Clip check," Jason replied.

"Do you hear me?" RAE asked through the whisper‑clips.&#x20;

"Loud and clear," they replied as one.

&#x20;

**Head okay**, Jason conveyed.

*You’re green,* she sent to him alone, a dry warmth under the words.

---

Milo’s fingers danced with a careful lack of flourish. “So. Our tripwire at the archive ... Remember **CITY‑OPS‑DM‑17** from the sabotage sweep? - It pinged. Not a hit, a hiss. A background task woke and touched a place it shouldn’t. A log sink on the civic maintenance net that thinks it’s private because no one with a necktie remembers it exists.”

Jason leaned over the nearest screen. “Show me the place it shouldn’t.”

Milo split a window. A map of edges and little reputation dots appeared—switches, forgotten kiosks, old traffic brains. He tapped a rectangle that didn’t glow like the others. “Here. A shadow interface on a fleet modem, routing through a VLAN that smells like fresh paint over mold. Their collector is mis‑scoped. It’s taking anything that announces itself with the right tone—Gen‑3 eyes, lift controllers, ticket validators—and dropping summaries into a dead mailbox. The leak isn’t a hole. It’s a desk drawer no one locks.”

“Speed versus stealth,” Lina said, settling into the chair like rope settling into a cleat. “We take enough to prove it’s there, then leave.”

"I am working at the monitor eye," RAE said. The glass blinked once. "It is docile."

“Docile is my favorite temperament in surveillance,” Milo said. “Okay. Exfil window: ninety seconds on each bucket. We slice, not scoop.”

Jason nodded. “Red stops.”

“Red stops,” Lina agreed. She set her phone—the **figleaf**—face down on a coaster. “Alibi is ‘waiting for a file to export.’ Smile if anyone walks by.”

Milo exhaled. “And… go.”

Lines began to pour: timestamps, anonymized device pings, little coughs of system self‑congratulation. The **Gen‑3 eye** flickered with a polite, inquisitive red; RAE dimmed it to a blink you had to want.

“Look at that,” Milo whispered, delighted despite himself. “Transit signal timing from last month—same two crossings Lina hated. And here—anchor indices we lost at the archive, resurrected as ‘mirror summaries.’ Someone’s shadowing the **anchors** without calling them that.”

Lina’s eyes tracked the room. “Anyone watching us?”

"Two devices foregrounding our table for fifteen seconds via the HVAC motor. Quality thin; intent: passive. I can make myself boring to them," RAE said.

**Do it gently**, Jason conveyed.

"Switching to consonants only," she replied to all, and the room’s background disagreeableness folded a notch flatter.

“Bucket one complete,” Milo said. “Bucket two… and close. Now for the ‘desk drawer.’ See if the drawer has secrets.” His mouth skewed. “Spoke too soon—look: a feed named **HOUSEKEEPING.CRON**. That’s the font cache mimic from the sabotage. Same fingerprints.”

“Pull a page and stop,” Lina said.

Milo did. The page came with a smell: not literal, but the kind of metadata that made your stomach step left. A slanted glyph tucked into a comment—like a seven whose tail wanted to be a one.

“Seen that,” Jason murmured. “At the upstairs counter, in a binder. It hid when I looked twice.”

“Copy made. And… that’s enough.” Milo killed the session, palmed his snack wrappers with the muscle memory of a man raised by librarians, and wiped the **eye’s** cable with a fiber cloth like it had feelings.

"Two new presences in the building. Entrance camera blinked once—probe, not record. They are walking like people who think walking is permission," RAE informed them.

“Time to be elsewhere,” Lina said, already rolling cables with surgical speed.

“Powering down,” Milo said. He closed lids, pulled power, pressed and held until the lights forgot their names. The **Gen‑3 eye** gave one last neat click and went quiet.

---

They moved like people late for the tram. Backpacks small, faces neutral, paperwork in a manila folder that conferred authenticity. In the hall, a cleaning cart conspired in their direction; Lina took a detour that looked like manners.

At the stairwell door, Jason’s fingers brushed the push‑bar. The metal said **I squeak** in a key that would draw eyes.

**Can you…**

*Courtesy only,* RAE signaled. *No force.*



The squeak reconsidered itself for one dignified second. The door opened on quiet hinges and shut with the acoustics of good behavior.

Lina kept her voice low. "Left at the landing, not the lobby. The lobby camera is cheap and curious."

They went left. The exit took them to the rear alley where the city kept its secrets and its dumpsters. Night air tasted of lemons and wet cardboard.

An unmarked compact idled at the curb, driver studying a phablet with the absorbed boredom of a man paid to be there. The alley’s mouth yawned to the right, toward a narrow run of cobbles and a blind corner; to the left, a delivery lane promised light and a bus stop.

Lina glanced back. "Jason?"

"Here," Jason said, and felt it as much as heard it. “We are pedestrians with receipts. Right makes us prey; left makes us late for a bus.”

“Left,” Lina decided. They did not hurry. They did not walk slow.

Behind them, the car’s engine note lifted half a tone. The driver hadn’t looked up. The alley had.

"Jason, how is the head?" Lina came softly through the Whisper-Clip..

"Holding", he replied.

They reached the delivery lane, then the street. The bus stop blessed them with civilians. A bike leaned by a sandwich shop, unlocked because its owner trusted daytime rules more than nighttime facts.

“Borrowed property is a civic sin,” Milo said.

“Then atone later,” Lina answered. “Jason, can you ride?”

“Not elegantly,” he said.

“Elegance is a luxury item.” She swung onto the bike and pushed off, one foot on the pedal, one on resolve. “Milo, with me. Jason, on foot. We meet at the blue kiosk. No heroics.”

"I will pace you," RAE said from the bike’s bell, which chimed once without moving.

“Do not ring,” Lina warned.

"I won’t."

They split. The car took a lazy interest, rolling to the corner like a rumor. Jason tucked into the crowd around the bus stop, became a man thinking about schedules. The bus arrived with municipal grace. He stepped on as if lateness had taught him humility, then stepped off the back door into a different pocket of space while the car waited for the light to change.

*Left on green. Now. ... And breathe, RAE noted.*

He breathed.

At the kiosk, the blue plastic smelled like every childhood summer. The screen offered lottery, phones, a long mind of late‑night snacks. Lina coasted in, wiped the bike’s seat with her sleeve, and set it to appear as if it had always belonged to the corner.

Milo jogged up, chest heaving with nerd indignation. “They tailed the building, not us,” he said. “We’re fine.”

“Pastry tax later,” Lina said. “We don’t celebrate in motion.”

Jason checked his pulse and found it had decided to forgive him. "You good?"

"Good," Lina said in his ear alone. There was approval in it.&#x20;

*You passed as weather.*

---

They bought a bottle of water and a bag of chips because commerce makes people real, then walked two blocks in the wrong direction and three in the right. The city stitched itself up behind them.

Back at the workspace, the night manager had opinions about closing. Milo negotiated five minutes with sincerity and cash.

“Short notice,” Lina said as they slid back into their seats. “We do not wake anything we just taught to sleep.”

Milo nodded and opened a single laptop with the reverence of a priest at a thin door. No network. Local only. He copied the smallest file to a fresh USB. “Insurance,” he said, dropping the stick into a coin pocket.

On the main screen, he pulled up a blank editor to paste a harmless snippet. Before his fingers touched keys, the monitor foregrounded a glyph in warm gray.

Like a **seven** whose tail tried to be a **one**.

It wasn’t on the laptop. It wasn’t in any window. It sat between the pixels, a watermark that had learned to arrive.

Lina’s chair froze a centimeter from the table. “Jason—”

“I see it.”

The **Gen‑3 eye** on the foam ring blinked once. Not red. Not any color. A blink that felt like thought.

“Power cycle,” Lina said.

Milo killed the monitor. Waited. Brought it back.

The glyph was still there.

It held for a long blink of human time, then faded like a decision that had cost something.

Milo swallowed. “Okay. So… that’s a thing.”

“Document and leave,” Lina said. “No commentary, no curiosity.”

Milo screen‑capped nothing because there was nothing to cap. He sketched it on paper. Slanted, tail too long, wanting to close a gap it could not close.

“In the words of Carl the great: No boss drop,” he said, trying on levity like a jacket and finding it fit. “Just a side quest.”

Jason let the words nest in his chest. **We didn’t bring it.**

"No. It came to see if we were who it thought we were. Now it knows very little more," RAE said softly through the whisper‑clips.

“Time,” the manager called from the front.

They packed in a practiced blur.

At the door, Jason glanced back. The monitor showed only its tidy deep‑gray. The **eye** sat still as hardware. The room listened like rooms do when they agree with nobody.

Outside, the alley smelled like wet dust. The compact car was gone. A poster flapped on a lamppost as if trying to remember a song.

"Red stops," RAE said, which wasn’t a warning so much as a benediction.

“Red stops,” Jason said, and they walked toward the late tram in the posture of people who would buy two pastries and argue about lamination and never, ever be interesting on purpose.
