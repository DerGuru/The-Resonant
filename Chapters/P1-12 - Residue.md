## Residue

Jason waited for Lina at the table, reading on his phone. They walked in silence most of the way back from the restaurant. She hadn’t asked questions, and he hadn’t spoken much more. He was grateful that she accompanied him now and it was enough, somehow, that she was willing to come with him later. That promise settled something in him. Not peace, exactly. But steadiness.

--- 

The next morning, they met again. Lina was quiet, observant. She didn’t ask how he’d slept. He appreciated that.

He went to work for a few hours but couldn’t focus. The world felt off-tempo. Monitors blinked with the wrong rhythm. A coworker asked him a question twice before he even registered it. By lunch, he’d given up and signed out early.

Lina was waiting outside, coat zipped high, her hair pulled back in a practical twist. "You ready?"

He nodded.

"Something else happened since yesterday?"

He shook his head. 

"Not much of a talker today. Are you okay?"

Jason gave a half shrug, then said, "I don’t know. Everything feels... suspended. Like I’m inside a thought that hasn’t finished forming yet."

Lina glanced sideways at him. "That’s poetic... A little bleak, though."

He tried to smile but failed. "I didn’t mean to sound dramatic. Just tired. And weirdly alert at the same time."

She gave a small nod. "That tracks. Welcome to the liminal space club. We meet irregularly and never agree on the minutes."

Jason gave a huff that almost became a laugh, but not quite.

They rode the tram without speaking. The city rolled by in soft grey tones, all static sky and dull metal. It matched his mood too well.

The archive building came into view. From the outside, it looked inert, bureaucratic. But Jason felt the shift the moment they stepped inside. The temperature difference. The pressure. Like something deeper than air had changed.

Lina walked beside him as if it were just another day, but Jason could feel her tension. Her eyes moved differently here. She didn’t ask where they were going - she already knew.

"Terminal 7," he said hoarsely.

They moved past silent corridors and sensor nodes, through an old-coded maintenance door Lina opened without hesitation.

Jason glanced at her. "You’ve done this before?"

She didn’t look at him. "Worked a short contract here. Years ago. Systems auditing. I remember the layout."

It explained her confidence - and the tension in her jaw. Jason noticed her breathing shift. Controlled.

"You never told me you worked here," he said quietly.

Lina gave a faint smirk. "You never asked. Besides, I'm sure you’ve got a few skeletons tucked away too."

Jason looked at her, brow raised. "Do I?"

She held his gaze a second longer, then shrugged. "Probably. You’re just even quieter about yours."

When they reached the terminal room, Jason hesitated just outside the threshold. The lights buzzed faintly. The air held a density he hadn’t imagined until now. Like a memory made physical.

Lina glanced at him. "We go in together. Don’t touch anything yet."

He nodded. "I won’t. Not without you."

Inside, the terminal waited. Exactly as he’d left it - screen blank, chair slightly off-center.

"Stand there," Lina said gently, indicating for him to take the spot where he’d been when it happened.

He stepped into place.

Nothing happened.

He let out a breath he hadn’t realized he was holding.

Lina crouched near the access panel, checking for residual field traces. Jason watched her fingers move like she was playing an invisible instrument. From her coat pocket, she pulled out a slim resonance pouch - folded cloth, sealed with a copper edge. She unfolded it quickly and laid out three small Resonograms on the terminal edge. Compact, circular, etched with tightly wound pattern-lines.

"Still carrying those?" Jason asked softly.

"I’m not great at trusting silence," Lina replied, not looking up. "Especially not in places that remember things."

"Low static,” she murmured. "Some harmonic lift, but nothing active."

Jason turned back to the console. "It was alive. I swear. Just for a moment."

Lina stood again, her face unreadable. "Let’s try something. I’ll stabilize the field manually. You just breathe. Don’t reach yet."

He nodded and closed his eyes.

The hum started again - not from the room, but from inside his body. Not sound. Not thought. Just structure.

Lina whispered, "Jason... I think it’s tuning to you."

He didn’t move. "Then what do we do?"

She didn’t answer immediately. When she did, her voice was quieter than before.

"We listen."

---

"Listen to what?" a voice came from the door.

Jason’s eyes snapped open.

"Who are you?" Lina asked sharply. "And how did you get in here?"

Milo Greaves stood in the doorway, one hand resting against the frame, the other holding a takeout cup. He stepped into the room as if it were the most natural thing in the world. His expression was half amusement, half concern - as if he wasn’t sure whether this counted as a prank or an emergency.

"Milo Greaves," he said, with the tone of someone announcing the weather. "And as for getting in... well, let's say doors have a tendency to open for me. Sometimes literally."

"That’s not an answer," Lina muttered.

Milo raised an eyebrow, unbothered. "No, but it’s a true statement."

Jason pushed off the console edge. The field had thinned, but not vanished. Milo’s presence disrupted something - not violently, but like a third tone entering a chord.

"How long have you been standing there?" Lina asked again, more sharply.

"Long enough to hear you whisper something ominous," Milo replied. "And to realize this place smells like old power lines and guilt."

"You followed us?" Jason said.

"I followed the data trail," Milo corrected. "And the building logs. And the ambient field bleed. Not very subtle, you two."

Lina crossed her arms. "You shouldn't be here."

"No one should be here," Milo said. "Which is why I *am.*"

Jason frowned. "That doesn't even - "

"Look," Milo cut in, dropping the grin. "Something’s happening. I’ve been watching internal systems for two weeks - field tremors, low-grade anomalies, dead packets blinking alive. Nothing actionable. But it all started clustering around your work ID."

He sipped from the cup and resumed the grin. "You see ... that got my attention."

"You think I did this?" Jason asked.

"I think you're part of it," Milo said. "Whether you meant to be or not."

He walked toward the terminal. His tone sobered. "Something’s under the surface. Not awake. But aware. And now that all three of us are in the room... it’s listening harder."

Jason looked at Lina, then back at Milo. "And you're not with Security?"

"Not exactly," Milo said, almost cheerfully. "I used to be. Internal Field Stability. Then I got tired of writing reports no one read and started solving problems before they made it into the system. Think of me as... preventive maintenance."

He crouched beside the console and tapped the panel lightly. "And this," he muttered, "is a problem. A very old, very quiet problem that doesn’t want to stay quiet anymore."

Lina reached for one of the Resonograms she had placed earlier,adjusting its place. Jason noticed the faint shimmer along its etched surface intensify.

"It’s drawing on harmonic feedback," she murmured. "Whatever’s down there - beneath the terminal layer - it’s attuned. Not invasive. But present."

"And old," Milo added. "Very old. I've seen harmonic dust signatures like this before - deep core patterns, the kind you only find in collapsed systems or under sealed structures. This one’s dormant, but intact. And that means it's been held together a long time, maybe longer than this facility has existed."

Jason swallowed. "So now what?"

Milo looked at him. For once, no smirk. Just clarity. "Now we decide what we are to it. Interference? Or invitation?"

Jason glanced at Lina. Her expression was unreadable, but her hand hovered protectively over the second Resonogram.

"I don’t think it wants to hurt anyone," Jason said. "It doesn’t feel hostile."

"Doesn’t mean it’s safe," Lina replied.

Jason nodded. "No. And it’s not a static pattern either. It’s responding."

"Exactly," Milo said. "That’s the part that scares me. Usually, dead or current systems don’t change when you touch them. This one shifts. It adapts."

Jason breathed in slow. The pulse in his chest had steadied, but hadn’t left. It wasn’t threatening. Just... waiting.

"Then we listen," he said, his voice low but steady. It wasn’t just a suggestion - it was a decision. A surrender, maybe. Or a first step.

Milo raised his cup again, flashing a lopsided grin. "I brought coffee. That counts as an offering, right?"

Lina didn’t laugh. But she didn’t tell him to leave either.

"Oh, don’t give me the stink-eyes," Milo added lightly. "I’m very useful in situations involving field drift, sealed protocols, and accidental resonance events."

Lina arched a brow. "You rehearsed that line."

"Only a little."

"Any idea what it is?" Jason asked.

Milo stood again, slower this time. "Not yet. But it’s not just reacting to stimuli in a predefined way. At least it looks that way. It’s composing a deliberate reaction. That implies intention. Maybe memory."

"Or programming," Lina said. "Which could be worse."

They stood for a long moment, three people in a quiet room that didn’t feel quite empty.
