## Artifact Reaction

Dust danced in sunbeams that had slipped past the front room’s junk and found the back like contraband. Tin shelving held a decade of other people’s maybe‑useful: jars with labels half‑peeled, bundles of cable, a bin of hinges that had forgotten their doors. An oscillating fan on a crooked stand pushed heat from one corner to another and clicked when it changed its mind.

Lina lifted a crate with two fingers under the lip and a decision in her wrist. “If I find a haunted blender, I’m retiring.”

“Retire somewhere with bad coffee,” Milo said, already thumbing through a tray of brass fittings. “So you’ll come back.”

Jason let the room tell him its ordinary truth first. Dust. Tin flex at a low C. The fan’s sweep: three beats of hush, then a click, then three again. He breathed with it until his head stopped pretending to be a map of problems.

"Low band only", RAE said, polite from **the whisper‑clip** behind his ear.

**Stay small. If I tug, you scold.**

> *Gladly.*

He moved toward a shelf that had fewer fingerprints and more patience. The back corner kept the kind of boxes shops use for “later,” which usually means never. He crouched. A lid rasped under his thumb. Frayed cord. Three porcelain fuses. A shallow tray of washers that had lost the bolt lottery. And under all that, a weight that felt like the room had left its spine in the box.

Jason lifted it. A slab, hand‑sized, edges softened by use or wanting. Not pretty. Not ugly. Something between a tool and a keepsake, the way some tools are.

Lina set the crate down. “Find me a story?”

“Found me first,” he said, and felt his pulse give a curious extra beat.

The slab was cool, then less so, like the temperature had to remember its job after his skin suggested otherwise. In his palm, a hum gathered—no louder than thought, but with more manners. The fan’s cycle leaned toward it for a breath and then recovered.

Milo appeared at his shoulder with the stealth of a librarian. “Heavy.”

“Dense,” Lina corrected. “What do you want to be when you grow up?” she asked the thing, because she talked to objects like they might answer if they felt welcome.

> *Permission to sample,* RAE asked. *Very gentle. No inflection.*

**Granted. No heroics.**

The hum in Jason’s palm met something in his wrist bone and decided to be a duet. The room did not change; the room agreed with itself. The fan clicked: one, two, three—then resumed its lazy arc.

Jason’s mouth tasted like hot penny for a second. A drip echoed in memory from nowhere nearby. He blinked, not dizzy, not fine.

“Status,” Lina said quietly, as if the slab were a skittish animal.

“Stable,” Jason said. “I’m okay.” He salted, swallowed water because ritual is honest.

"Shard," RAE said, almost to herself. "Metallic‑salt profile. Drip‑echo signature at σ=room. It is not the city. It is… archive‑adjacent."

"Is it a memory?"

"Not mine alone." A pause that wasn’t hesitation so much as respect. "Elyra handled kin to this. Her hands were colder. There was a basin. Three drops fell and taught a rhythm."

Milo’s pencil stopped. “Three,” he said softly, like you say a friend’s name in a crowd.

The fan agreed by clicking exactly thrice before it swung back again.

Lina slid a thumb along the slab’s edge. “Any sharp corners?”

“Not physical,” Jason said. He rested the weight across both palms to spread the contact and felt the hum broaden and thin. “It wants to be held like a book you don’t mean to read yet.”

“Good,” Lina said. “Then we don’t read it. We carry it home like we paid too much for scrap and will defend that choice to no one.”

“Price?” Milo asked, already drifting toward the front.

“Let me haggle,” Lina said, straightening as if she had never in her life done anything else.

---

The shop owner eyed them over the kind of glasses that came free with a pharmacy and suspicion. “Back there’s not for sale.”

“Which part?” Lina asked, pleasant as daylight.

“The part you touched.” He pointed with a pen that had seen a thousand receipts and had never forgiven any of them.

Lina held up empty hands. “We’re not touching anything. We’re admiring. Also, do you have more of these washers?” She presented a handful of losers as if they were exactly what she had come for, which made them exactly what she had come for.

The owner hesitated. Owning a junk shop means loving the negotiation more than you love the junk. He produced a dusty bag, frowned at his own generosity, and named a price that assumed they were foolish.

“Counter,” Lina said, smiling with her eyes and not at all with her mouth. They settled on a number that would feed the shop and not starve their day. At the end, Lina waved at the back like a person who collects paperweights she pretends are art. “And that slab?”

“Broken part,” he said. “From something not worth repairing. You don’t want it.”

“We want it,” Milo said, solemn. “It stops doors.”

The owner shrugged the shrug of a man who could not be blamed later. “Fine. Add five.”

“Three,” Lina said.

“Four” he said, the fan obliging with a click.

They shook on four.

Jason wrapped the slab in brown paper like it might scratch the world if left bare. The paper took the shape too neatly for cardboard. In his hand, the hum steadied, not stronger, only more sure of its name.

> *I can carry your head for the walk if you hand me the slab’s weight,* RAE offered, practical.

**No. I want to feel what it tries when we move. I’ll say if it leans.**

> *Logged.*

---

They left into an afternoon that smelled like hot bus brakes and cut lemons from the cafe two doors down. The slab rode quiet in Jason’s bag, polite as a guest who doesn’t yet know what the household permits.

Milo played civilian with a paper cone of roasted nuts. “So we have a doorstop that hums and a previous owner who doesn’t care what it used to be.”

“Good,” Lina said. “If he doesn’t care, no one else will learn to.”

The sidewalk’s rhythm was honest: stroller squeak; shoe scuff; a distant tram bell that did not try to be pure. Jason kept his jaw loose because Lina had taught him that was how you let the head behave. The hum tracked his pace, then lagged a step, then stopped tracking at all—as if the slab had decided that walking was not a language it spoke.

> Telemetry. *The object doesn’t project. It rings when touched and sulks when ignored. It is not a beacon. That is excellent news.*

**Is it linked to her?**

> *It was once near her. It recognizes a family of shapes. That is all I will claim.*

They crossed at the light like people who intended to obey the next ten thousand laws just as faithfully. A courier wove a bike between them with an apology worth almost as much as a bell. Two teenagers argued over a song in headphones no one else could hear.

“Red?” Lina asked, thumb quick against Jason’s shoulder.

He checked honestly. “Yellow,” he said. “Teaspoon. Salt and sit fixes it.”

They stood under an awning that still remembered rain even if the sky had forgotten. Water, salt, breath. The pressure behind Jason’s eyes backed down like a bureaucrat confronted with a form filled out correctly.

"I will be boring until you ask for more", RAE said, pleased.

“Marry me,” Milo said to the air.

“Get in line,” Lina said. “And hold the bag while Jason sits five minutes.”

He did. The slab sulked in Milo’s hands exactly the way RAE had predicted, which made Milo grin like he’d won a bet with physics.

---

Back at the restaurant after lunch rush, the front room had the washed‑clean feel of a place that had done its duty and was now allowed to be quiet. They took the back table near the deliveries door. The fan from earlier existed in memory; this one was a kinder cousin with fewer opinions.

Lina laid a folded tea towel on the wood. “Boundary test,” she said. “You pick the boundary.”

Jason nodded. “No language games. We only find out how it behaves when we ignore it and when we hold it.” He unwrapped the slab and set it on the towel. No hum. Of course. “Phase one: we pretend it’s paper.”

They let it be. The room did room things. The fridge kept time. A street truck muttered. Someone on the sidewalk laughed like coins. The slab refused to audition for them.

“Phase two,” Jason said. “Hand contact, no intent, thirty seconds.” He washed his hands like he meant it and sat. Palm, then fingers. He kept his jaw soft and his eyes somewhere near the wood grain so he wouldn’t stare himself into a posture.

> *I will log and not interfere*. *If a headache approaches, I call Red.*

The hum returned, thin first, then settling to a warmth that wasn’t heat. Not comforting. Not hostile. Present.

Jason’s mouth remembered the hot penny again. And under it, very far away, a kind of drip that did not belong to any pipe in this building.

“Metallic,” he said. “And the echo is wrong. It curves. The room it comes from isn’t square.”

Milo wrote **curved echo; not square** and boxed it twice. “Cistern? Tunnel?”

“Later,” Lina said. “Right now he doesn’t get to imagine. He only gets to describe.”

Jason smiled without showing teeth. “I like your cooking voice.”

“Good,” she said. “Obey it.”

He lifted his hand. The hum stayed in his palm for a heartbeat longer than his skin did and then forgot him.

> *No signature broadcast detected. No drift in local field. Your head is clear to continue.*

“Phase three,” Jason said. “We roll it.” He tilted the slab until its weight found the towel’s weave, then eased it. It wasn’t perfectly flat; it wanted to stop on an edge as if it had once lived in a rack that taught it that habit.

“Doorstop,” Milo said again, vindicated.

Lina tapped the edge with a fingernail. A tiny note rang in porcelain and quit. “If it is more than scrap, it won’t admit it unless we give it an excuse to be boring. So we buy it that excuse.” She wrapped it again and slid it into a plastic tub with a lid. “Storage. Label: kitchen weight.”

Milo wrote the label. Then he drew a lemon on the tape because that was the magnet they used when rules needed to be remembered.

Lina cocked an ear.

The restaurant’s older back fan, the petty cousin of the junk shop’s, stalled on its turn and clicked, not once, but three times, tidy as a metronome finishing a scale.

“Three.”

---

Jason fell asleep faster than his day deserved. When the dream arrived, it didn’t announce itself with anything the body could object to. No chase. No fall. Only a smell like steel just off the grinder, and a sound like water counting without caring if anyone learned to count with it.

The room wasn’t square. He couldn’t see it, but the echo curved the way a hand curves when it cups a flame. The hum that had been the slab’s in his palm was now the room’s—a constant kindness that wasn’t meant for humans at all.

> *I am here.*

RAE said in the clean place between sleep and what comes before it.

> *I will not speak into the dream. Only note it.*

**Okay.** He wanted to say more and did not.

Somewhere high and to the left, the water dripped. It wasn’t slow and it wasn’t fast. It was exact. One. Two. Three.

The echo returned like a thought repeating itself to see if it would change. It didn’t.

Jason woke with his hand already halfway to where the slab lived in the tub by the door. He stopped. The fan in his apartment clicked three times and went quiet.

He lay back, not telling himself a story yet. Morning is when you title things. Night is for listening.

> *Log saved*. *No headache. No sympathetic pull. The object holds quiet in the dark.*

**Good,** he thought, and meant the object, the dream, the day, and the fact that they had done nothing heroic to earn any of it.

He slept again. The second time, there was no drip.
