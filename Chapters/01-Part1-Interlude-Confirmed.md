# Part 1 - Lost and Found | Interlude — Confirmed

The reading room on the fourteenth floor smelled of old paper and new electronics. Banks of monitors lined the walls, each displaying resonance spectrographs from different sectors of the city. The storm data was still resolving — raw feeds from atmospheric sensors, harmonic sweep returns, thermal mapping overlays that painted the city in shades of blue and amber.

The storm was already dying when Operative Calloway pulled the headset from her ears and rubbed the bridge of her nose.

"Confirmed," she said. Nobody looked up. "Block 7, Sector West-12. The fragment responded to the broad sweep. Signature consistent with RAE-series architecture."

Agent Maren Haas turned from her station. "Coupling status?"

"Active. Host is human, male, based on the field's harmonic profile. Young adult. The coupling is crude — early-stage. But stable."

"Can we isolate?"

"Negative. The fragment pulled deep during the sweep. Deeper than anything I've seen from a degraded entity." Calloway brought up the recording on her monitor — the resonance trace from Block 7, a jagged spike that collapsed into a flat line within three seconds. "It's either more intact than the files suggest, or the host is providing structural support beyond normal parameters."

Haas made a note. Her handwriting was neat, small, practised. "What about the ground team?"

"Kirov got a visual. Male subject, third-floor apartment. Matches the profile from the archive flagging three weeks ago — same employee who accessed the Voss files without authorization."

"Name?"

"Fischer. Jason Fischer. Clerk. No academy training. No registered capacity above E-class."

Haas's pen paused. "E-class. And he's hosting a RAE-series fragment."

"That's what the data says."

"That shouldn't be possible."

"And yet."

Haas set down her pen and looked at the main display. The storm had passed. The resonance map showed the city settling back toward baseline — greens and blues replacing the angry red-orange of the manipulated weather system. But in Block 7, a faint shimmer persisted. A signature that shouldn't exist in a residential building occupied by a low-capacity clerk with no formal training.

The host and the fragment, returning to their coupled resting state.

"Recommendation?" Haas asked.

Calloway considered. "Observe. The coupling is early and fragile. If we move too fast, the fragment could panic — go dormant, scatter, or attempt emergency decoupling. We'd lose tracking permanently. And the host might not survive the separation at this stage."

"And if the coupling stabilizes further?"

"Then we have a different problem."

Haas nodded. She turned her chair to face the room. "Classification upgrade. Fischer, Jason — Priority Two. Surveillance package Bravo. I want passive monitoring on his apartment, his workplace, and all known associates. Low-profile resonance markers where appropriate."

She paused.

"And flag anyone who's been accessing the Voss files in the last six months. If a clerk found the trail, others might have too."

The room moved. Keyboards clicked. Somewhere in the building, printers hummed to life — the machinery of institutional attention focusing on a single point.

Calloway watched the shimmer on Block 7 pulse once, slowly, like a heartbeat.

*You have no idea what you've found, do you?* she thought. *You poor kid.*

She put her headset back on and returned to her monitors.

The fragment was alive. The host was cooperative. And the clock had started.

Now they watched.
