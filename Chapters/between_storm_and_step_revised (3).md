# Between Storm and Step

The storm was gone. The city went back to work—buses sighing, doors complaining, radiators clearing their throats on the hour. Jason listened without flinching. The sounds weren’t ominous; they were the city doing what it always did after being reminded it could break.

They kept the plan simple: no schedules, no drama. *Meet between steps.* Lina would text a photo of the day’s specials with the corner of the chalkboard in frame—code for *are you nearby?* Milo sent pins titled **benches**, which meant *public seating that won’t get us told off*. Jason checked his phone less when he wasn’t pretending not to.

> *Alignment’s inching up. About twenty percent. Translation on the low bands is cheaper now.*

**Good. Keep it cheap. My head likes its job.**

They made *be boring first* an actual rule. Lina wrote it on a receipt and slid it over with their bowls. “Eat first,” she said. “Talk after.” The room felt steadier when they obeyed her like it was a ritual instead of advice.

“How much Invest are you letting her take?” Lina asked later, when chairs were upside down and the lights were honest.

“As little as we can get away with,” Jason said. “She asks before she pulls. I say yes if my head doesn’t wince.”

> *Correction: I ask that we use slower encoding. Lower cost.*

**Teamwork.** The word held.

Milo treated waiting like a task you could do well. He printed maps, taped layers, cut little windows and slid routes until they lined up with his patience. Where a tile refused to load, he wrote *policy* and let it sit. The printouts made the wall look like the city had shed skins and kept them.

Jason handled the quiet by starting early at the archive. Paper was good—slow, human, uninstrumented. He “re‑inventoried” boxes that already knew where they were and practiced the face that said *I am harmless and slightly bored.*

> *Your nothing‑face is improving. Your shoulders have stopped pointing at the thing you don’t want noticed.*

He kept his hands still and said nothing out loud. The practice helped.

The pings, when they came, were ordinary and inconvenient. A stairwell complained about footsteps; a library vent whispered a note that didn’t belong; a construction fence hummed like it wanted attention. They didn’t chase. They marked. The notebook filled with times instead of conclusions.

> *Try different hours next time,* RAE suggested. *Same places, new pressure patterns. Easier to bracket the cavity.*

**We can do that. Day job permitting.**

Lina orbited—not hovering, just present. “Check in,” she’d text when their pins drifted toward places the map pretended not to know. “Tea after.” She had opinions about security theater, clipboards, and the slope of public benches. “If a man with authority hair asks, you’re helping me label pantry bins,” she told them. “He’ll respect labels.”

At night, the city thinned out. RAE liked it then. She didn’t talk about magic; she talked about behavior. “Old grid versus new grid,” she framed it, and—because words helped—gave the old break a name.

> *Sundering,* she said once.

**Official term or yours?**

> *Official, somewhere. The city’s resonance changed; people changed it more. I’m looking for someone who learned to live in the bend.*

**Elyra.**

> *Yes.*

They had normal problems. Milo’s boss wanted more hours than the day provided. Milo wrote a script to babysit a script and called it responsible. Lina’s dad’s delivery van refused damp mornings. Jason brought a jump pack and the borrowed confidence of a video tutorial. It worked. No one celebrated out loud.

> *Offer: I can help with the van,* RAE said once.

**Pass. Save your draw (Invest) for my brain.**

> *Saving.*

Sleep got easier. Headaches turned into nudges. When one arrived, RAE backed off without being told and kept her voice on the bands Jason tolerated.

> *Checklist: no heroics; slow moves; be furniture until furniture stops working,* she would remind, flat and precise.

He added, privately, **bring pastry tax to Lina**, and felt her amusement without needing the words.

One gray afternoon a tram took a corner too fast and the café window sang. Jason didn’t glance. Milo’s eyebrow did the work for him.

“We’re not hearing that, right?” Milo asked.

“Correct,” Jason said. “Not hearing in progress.”

> *Coincidence,*

**Mark it anyway.**

> *Marked.*

Back at the restaurant, Lina set tea in front of them and pointed at the receipt rule. “Report.”

“We’re collecting time windows,” Milo said. “Same corners, different hours. It makes her math possible.”

> *It does,* RAE said—out loud this time. The spoon in Jason’s cup gave a friendly tap, just enough to carry a voice. “Hello, Lina.”

“Hi, RAE,” Lina said, as if they’d been doing this for years. “Light draw.”

“Light,” RAE agreed. “Carriers only.”

Lina didn’t ask for numbers; she checked Jason’s face and waited for him to eat. “Then go be boring somewhere useful,” she said when the bowls were empty.

They split a plate that claimed to be for one. The plan wasn’t a Plan. It was a routine. Walk at normal speed. Check the same places at different times. Don’t be heroic. If something sings, don’t sing back.

On the way out, Jason paused by the door and texted *Later.* It had become code for *we’re doing the careful thing.* The glass showed a normal street, the kind of day that forgets names on purpose.

> *New ping, faint,* RAE murmured. *Riverside. Might be nothing. Might be neighbor to something.*

**Add it to the list.**

> *Added.*

Between storm and step, they didn’t get braver. They got practiced. Ordinary became something they could do on purpose.
