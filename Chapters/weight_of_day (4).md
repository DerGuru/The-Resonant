# Weight of the Day

Steam drifted in ribbons above the pass. The radio near the espresso machine mumbled a song that could have been any song; the clatter and hiss of the kitchen turned melody into texture. Lina slid plates along the pass like they were chess pieces, quick wrists, focused eyes, ponytail a metronome.

The bell over the door gave its sheepish jingle.

Milo froze in the entryway as if the floor were pressure-sensitive. Jacket zipped to the throat. Eyes on the corners first, exits second, people last.

“You going to stand there until closing?” Lina asked without looking up.

Milo blinked. “Is that an option?”

“Only if you’re decorative,” she said, pushing a menu across the counter. “Pick a table. Preferably not in the doorway.”

He navigated to a corner two-top with his usual part-spy, part-cat energy. The table gave him both exits and a reflection in the window. He sat and peeled off his backpack like it might explode.

“What can I get you?” Lina asked, pen poised over the pad though she could already guess.

“Coffee. Black.” He caught her look. “And… water.”

“You do eat, right?”

“When I forget not to.”

She poured, set the cup down with the soft, stern clink of someone delivering both caffeine and judgment. “He said he’d come?” she asked, still casual.

“Jason? Yeah. After work. Which may be a concept he’s only loosely familiar with.”

“Bold from the man whose job title is ‘internet raccoon.’”

“Freelance archivist,” Milo corrected. “Occasional librarian of other people’s secrets.”

“That’s worse.”

He cupped his coffee like a found fire. “Fine. I’m a raccoon. I wash my food.”

They let the kitchen noise fill the space. Lina wiped a clean spot that didn’t need wiping. Milo tried not to look like he was counting seconds.

“So,” she said finally, drawing the word out, “how bad is it?”

Milo kept his eyes on the coffee. “Define ‘it.’”

“Jason. The thing in his head. The… *RAE*.”

He glanced up at the ceiling as if the conversation might condense there. “You ever hear a fridge hum go a little too *true* for a second?”

“That’s specific.”

“It’s like that. He’s got… alignment. And something listening back. Not in a spooky, whisper-up-the-drains way. In a calibrated way. It wants to do things *clean*.”

“And *you* want to watch.”

“I want him not to get crushed between curiosity and policy.” Milo shrugged. “I know the contours of the machine that does the crushing.”

Lina nodded once, like she’d been bracing for worse. “He looks tired lately.”

“He downloads guilt in forty-eight-hour torrents,” Milo said. “Won’t admit it. Thinks the world will be better if he keeps his head down. Which, historically, ends with getting your head stepped on.”

“You like him,” Lina said, half accusation.

“I like that he’s careful,” Milo said. “And that he thinks the city should be kinder than it is.”

Lina’s mouth tilted. “He also orders the same thing three times a week and tips like it’s a math test. Don’t mistake that for a plan.”

Milo sipped. “You don’t trust plans.”

“I trust kitchens,” she said. “If you burn the garlic, you can’t unburn it. You start over.”

“That’s a plan.”

“It’s a *rule*.”

The bell over the door jingled again. Jason shook rain from his hair, coat carried like a surrender flag. He saw them and something in his shoulders unhooked.

“You two conspiring without me?” he said, breath fogging a little in the doorway.

“Always,” Milo deadpanned.

“Only for your benefit,” Lina added, already reaching for plates. “Sit. I’ll get you actual food, which is a thing your body needs to continue being a place where thoughts happen.”

Jason slid into the other chair at Milo’s table. “I ate,” he lied.

“He did not,” Milo said. “He sent me a photo of a vending machine.”

“That was *research*.” Jason rubbed the heel of his hand under his eye. “Work was—” He made a gesture that was half shrug, half apology. “—work.”

“Translation,” Milo said. “Management is inventing nouns again.”

Jason snorted. “He called it ‘risking compliance.’ In a meeting. With slides.”

Lina set down a plate: lemon rice, charred greens, a piece of fish with a crisp skin that looked like it knew secrets. “Ah yes,” she said. “The phrase that means ‘we noticed you noticing things.’”

Jason looked at the food like it had committed to him. “I’ll pay you in whatever currency I have left.”

“Eat it while it’s hot,” Lina said, then softer, “and drink your water.”

He obeyed. The first forkful slowed his face. The restaurant wasn’t quiet exactly, but the noise moved around them like it understood they needed the space.

“Okay,” Milo said after a minute, tapping the table once with his finger. “Since we’re all here, and since the espresso machine is whispering in a language I find unsettling, can we talk about the voice in your skull?”

Jason glanced at Lina.

She raised both hands. “As long as no one chants, I’m fine.”

Jason reached into his pocket and set a thin, clear ear clip on the table—a tiny curve of almost-nothing. The other one was already hooked behind his ear, tucked into hair and shadow.

“Low tide,” he said under his breath.

Lina blinked once, then hooked a twin clip behind her own ear, the motion practiced now. She touched it like testing a secret. “Low tide,” she echoed.

Milo lifted his backpack, rummaged, produced a matching clip with the pride of someone revealing contraband candy. “Low tide,” he said, and tapped the table twice.

The three looked like nothing more than friends leaning in. No wires. No glow.

“RAE,” Jason said softly. “Floor: Milo.”

A breath of latency, then a second voice arrived—not from the air, exactly, but along bone and skin, as if a thought had borrowed the architecture of a heartbeat. It wore Jason’s timbre the way light wears glass.

“Floor confirmed. Hello, Milo. Please avoid tapping in three-beat patterns; the espresso pump will try to follow.”

Milo’s eyebrows did a private jump. “Noted.”

“Floor: Lina,” Jason said.

“Hello Lina." RAE said with the same gentle patina. "Your father is about to over-salt the stock by three pinches."

Lina didn’t turn her head. “He can’t hear you.”

"He will taste and correct at two", RAE replied - faint amusement like a shimmer.

Lina pressed her lips together, faint, reluctant smile. “Alright. So how smart are you?”

“Unclear.” A fractional beat. "Smarter when we are careful. Less smart when we are hungry or proud."

Milo sat back, satisfied. “I like you.”

"Noted", RAE said again. "Floor back to Jason?"

“Hold it,” Lina said. “If anyone walks in, do we look like we’re… conspiracy-monologuing?”

“Half-duplex,” Milo said. “We’re literally doing radio etiquette. Tap for floor, keep bursts short. Two streams stable, the third makes Jason’s head file a complaint.”

“I’m fine,” Jason said.

“Which is what he says before he isn’t,” Lina said. “So. We keep it light.”

“Light is good,” RAE agreed. "The pump is quieter if we stay off its rhythm."

Lina flicked Jason a look that was half fond, half evaluation. “How are *you*, minus the voice?”

Jason stabbed a wedge of lemon, squeezed it, watched the oil haze shift. “My boss thinks the archive is safer if nobody cares about it. And the files I *do* care about keep vanishing. I am resisting the temptation to glue myself to the server rack like a barnacle.”

“Heroic,” Milo said. “Barnacles are underrated.”

“Also,” Jason added, almost sheepish, “I keep wanting to fix things I don’t own.”

“Like what?” Lina asked.

“Door hinges. Traffic lights. People’s bad timing.” He took another bite. “The city is… humming wrong in places. It’s hard not to hum back.”

“Restraint is a shape too,” RAE said, "and it leaves fewer fingerprints."

Milo tilted his head. “You hear that at night, L—”

“Floor,” Lina said quickly, stealing it with a fingertip tap. “I hear *people*. I hear him not sleeping.” She looked at Jason. “You’re allowed to need help, you know. It’s not a moral failure.”

Jason’s smile faltered at the edges. “I keep thinking it’ll stop asking me to be better at it.”

“**IT**,” RAE said, "is the world. It will never stop."

They let that sit, the way you let a pot sit when boiling slows on its own.

From the kitchen, her father barked, “Lina!”—then, softer—“Some help?”

“Coming,” she called. To the table: “He’ll pretend the stock salted itself.”

“Tell him the pump did it,” Milo said.

Lina rolled her eyes and slipped behind the pass. Jason watched her talk with her father in the language of cooks—pinch, tilt, taste, nod. The radio found a chorus and forgot it.

Milo leaned in. “You really okay?”

Jason glanced aside. “Define ‘okay.’”

“Eating. Talking. Not in a basement alone.”

Jason managed a ghost of a grin. “On a curve? Yeah.”

“Good. Because I need you conscious later. The thing in the stacks? I want another pass.”

“Tonight?” Jason asked.

“Tonight,” Milo said. “Quiet hours. We can look boring.”

Lina reappeared, set down a small bowl. “Taste that.”

Jason did. “Salt’s right.”

“Three pinches,” she said, smirk tucked away.

Jason and Milo both tried not to obviously stare at nothing.

*Two,* RAE said privately, pleased. *He stopped at two.*

## After-Hours Stacks

The archive at night sounded like the building checking its teeth. Air handlers breathed. Elevator cables whispered. The fluorescents hummed a little flat.

Milo set up at a table under a cone of light with the posture of someone who could fall asleep at attention. He arranged three snacks around his laptop like a Neolithic offering and promptly dozed.

Jason logged the returns, re-shelved a heavy map drawer by muscle memory. The day had left a silt line behind his eyes. He kept to the slow channel—water bottle within reach, movements clean. Every click of a latch, every soft slide, he let it be the only thing happening.

A heel of his hand pressed the edge of a drawer; the metal sang a thin line. He eased off.

**We can leave it alone,** he thought, not quite words.

> *Good,* RAE’s reply arrived inside the quiet, a small chord that didn’t unsettle the room. *Clean shapes or no shapes. Your boss?*

**He smiled at me like I’m a leak.** Jason stacked boxes in an order only he found obvious. **“Risking compliance.” He wants to rearrange the living to make shelving easier for the dead.**

> *We can help without getting caught being kind.*\
> *Standards: small, precise, hidden in the noise. The field likes it when you behave like you belong.*

Jason almost laughed. **Do we?**

> *We are trying.*\
> *Floor back to world. Rests are music too.*

The desk phone rang, soft and reprimanding. Jason stood a little straighter, glanced at the sleeping Milo, and picked up.

“Records,” he said.

“Jason,” said his manager’s voice—oily, slow. “You’re staying late.”

“Catching up.”

“Mm. Listen. I wanted to circle back to this morning.” A pause thick with slide decks. “There’s a perception that you’ve been… proactive.”

Jason watched a dust mote fail to land. “Perception.”

“In the sense that we prefer strict compliance. There are processes.”

“Like backing up files before they vanish?” Jason said, mild as milk.

A soft inhale over the line. “Don’t make me write you up for tone. We can’t afford messes. You understand.”

Jason pressed a thumb into the groove of the desk until the wood’s grain answered back. “I understand you’ll notice if I keep noticing things.”

Silence spread, bureaucratic and damp. “We value your… attention to detail,” the manager said finally. “Channel it. That’s all.”

“I’ll channel,” Jason said, and hung up before his mouth made it worse.

Milo snored, tiny and apologetic, then flinched awake. “I wasn’t sleeping,” he said.

“Good,” Jason said, “because we got a compliment.”

“What kind?”

“The kind that sounds like a warning.”

Milo scrubbed his face. “Then we should be very, very boring.” He jerked his chin toward the map room. “One more look at the light table?”

They moved together down the aisle. Jason laid a cloth, flipped the switch. The hum rose—thin, steady, a line that asked to be answered. A water‑stained sheet went translucent under his hands; pencil ghosts lifted like old, polite bones.

“Stay off the rhythm,” Milo murmured.

Jason didn’t touch the hum. He let eyes and breath do the work. Lines. Drift. A curve that wanted to be a circle and failed.

Milo leaned, yawned mid‑sentence, surrendered to it, and covered his mouth. “You keep looking like that, Fischer, and I’m going to film you and sell it as ASMR.”

“Don’t,” Jason said. “Internet raccoons have brands to maintain.”

Milo grinned despite himself, slid to a stool, and nodded off again, chin to chest.

Jason stood a little longer. The light held the paper like a patient promise. He took one photo on the old handheld they used for nothing else; the green LED blinked, obedient.

He switched off the table. The hum fell away. The room remembered how to breathe.

“Home,” he said gently, jostling Milo’s shoulder.

“Ugh,” Milo said, eloquent. “Fine. I was having a dream where Neumann apologized.”

“Impossible fiction,” Jason said, and they laughed in the small way that didn’t wake anything else.

## Tram in Sodium Light

The tram smelled faintly of raincoats and old brakes. Orange streetlamps smeared the windows with chemical dusk. Milo occupied two seats like someone defending a claim; his head tapped the glass at each curve and he accepted it.

“You going to tell Lina?” he asked without opening his eyes.

“About what?”

“Your boss. ‘Risking compliance.’ The joy.”

Jason watched a woman in a yellow coat count coins, fail to reconcile them, and shrug at reality’s math. “She’ll know without me saying it.”

“True,” Milo said. “She reads people like a recipe card written by a dead aunt.”

Jason smiled at the window. “That’s specific.”

“I contain multitudes. And grocery lists.” Milo adjusted his backpack into pillow status. “We’re still good?”

“With what.”

“Helping. But precise.” He cracked one eye. “Not chasing the next big thing. Not burning the garlic.”

Jason thought of the lines on the map, of drift that begged for hands. Of the stock that had taken salt and then stopped, like a conversation with a boundary.

**We hold the line,** he thought toward the quiet.

> *We do. We hold, then place. Quietly. And you drink water.*

**Bossy.**

> *Alive,* RAE said, and then went still again, like a hand off the rail.

They rode the rest of the way in the kind of silence that feels like being allowed to take off a backpack you didn’t know you’d been wearing.

## Closing Time Heat

They cut back past the restaurant to drop Milo three doors down; the bell gave its last brave jingle when Jason stepped in. Chairs were upturned on tables. The kitchen warmth held like weather that had decided to stay.

Lina’s father stood over a stock pot the size of a small planet, sleeves rolled, face folded into the kind of frown that meant satisfaction. He glanced up, grunted at Jason in the affectionate dialect of tired cooks, and handed him a wooden spoon without ceremony.

“Stir,” he said, deputizing him without asking.

Jason took the spoon. The broth lifted in slow spirals, steam fogging his glasses; he blinked and kept the circle clean. His shoulders found a vector they understood. He didn’t try to fix anything. He just kept it from catching.

Lina leaned in the doorway, arms crossed, watching. “Look at you,” she said. “A functional adult.”

“Don’t tell anyone,” Jason said.

“Too late,” Milo called from the hall, not appearing. “I’m tweeting it to exactly no one.”

“Go home,” Lina said over her shoulder.

Milo’s yawn gave a choked laugh. “On it.”

Jason set the rhythm just under the kitchen’s hum, off the pump’s beat, inside the fan’s small falter. The circle held.

**This is nothing,** he thought, almost embarrassed by the calm that came with it.

> *Not nothing,* RAE said—private, patient, using the spoon and the bone behind his ear at once. *Your pulse is up two. That is Invest without spectacle. The field prefers this.*

Jason didn’t answer. He watched the surface smooth—a small patch of order coaxed into being and then left to be itself.

He passed the spoon back. Lina’s father gave the minimal nod that meant approval and goodbye.

Lina stepped closer, pressed a wrapped container into his hands. “Leftovers,” she said. “Payment for services rendered. And because you’ll forget breakfast.”

Jason accepted both the food and the implication. “Thank you.”

“Don’t defend management to me tomorrow,” she said lightly.

“Wouldn’t dream of it.”

They stepped into the night. The street was the sort of empty that means a new day is already leaning in.

Jason looked at the container, then at the window where the pot’s slow swirl lingered like an afterimage.

**We’ll keep it small,** he told the quiet. **Clean.**

> *Clean is kind,* RAE said.
