# Part 4 - We Are Family | Ch. 09 - Three weeks

Three weeks before the wedding, reality started feeling different.

Not worse. Not stressful. Just... imminent. Real in a way planning had been abstract.

"We're getting married in three weeks," Lina said over morning coffee, as if testing how the words sounded.

"We are," we agreed.

"That's soon."

"Very soon."

"Are you nervous?"

We considered. "About the ceremony? No. About marriage itself? No. About accidentally messing up vows in front of everyone we care about?" We paused. "Maybe a little."

She laughed. "Same. The relationship part feels solid. The public declaration part is terrifying."

"We could elope," we suggested. "Just us and an officiant. Skip the ceremony entirely."

"Your students would never forgive us." She smiled. "Besides, I want them there. Want everyone there. Want to celebrate what we've built with the people who helped us build it."

"Then we endure the terror of public speaking."

"Together."

"Always together."

---

Teaching both cohorts had settled into comfortable rhythm.

First cohort, now four months advanced, worked on genuinely complex material. Multi-pattern stabilization. Harmonic resonance field manipulation. Theory application that approached what professional practitioners would handle.

Kira led their practice sessions now, with us providing oversight and intervention when needed. She'd developed her own teaching style - patient but demanding, encouraging but honest about limitations.

"You're natural at this," we told her after watching her guide Sophie through a particularly tricky pattern adjustment.

"I had good teachers." She grinned. "And I remember being where they are. That helps. I know which explanations work because I know which ones worked for me."

Second cohort progressed through fundamentals with the same intensity first group had experienced. They were further along than first cohort had been at equivalent timeframe - benefit of improved curriculum and Kira's assistance allowing more individualized attention.

"The program is working," Elyra observed during planning session. "Not just functioning. Actually working. Students are learning faster, retaining better, developing more comprehensive understanding. This is what resonance education should look like."

"We got lucky," we said.

"You built carefully," she corrected. "There's a difference between luck and competent program development. You created structure that supports learning. That's skill, not chance."

Marcus approached after session, carrying a thick folder of materials.

"Healing theory research," he explained. "I've been studying everything I can find about resonance restoration. Historical cases, theoretical frameworks, your documented approach with Elyra. I want to be ready when specialization track opens."

We paged through his compilation. Thorough. Organized. Demonstrating genuine understanding of complex concepts.

"This is graduate-level research," we said. "You compiled this yourself?"

"With Milo's help finding sources. But yes. I..." He hesitated. "I need to do this properly. Not just because I caused injury, but because healing matters. If I'm going to specialize, I need to commit completely. No half measures."

"When the track opens," we said carefully, "you'll be accepted. This level of preparation shows you're serious. But remember - being able to execute healing requires more than theoretical knowledge. It requires precision most practitioners never develop."

"I know. But I have to try." He met our eyes. "I owe it to her. To everyone who's been injured by resonance accidents. If I can help even one person heal properly instead of just managing damage, that's worth years of study."

After he left, Elyra said quietly: "He's going to succeed. That level of dedication combined with natural talent? He'll be executing healing within three years."

"That's optimistic timeline."

"I'm rarely optimistic without reason." She smiled. "We're building something bigger than we planned. Teaching students who'll change the field. That's legacy."

---

Two weeks before the wedding, Milo gathered us at the warehouse to discuss transformation logistics.

"Friday before ceremony, we move all equipment into storage," he explained, pointing at diagrams. "Saturday morning, students help set up seating. Thirty-five chairs arranged in semicircles. Ceremony space at center. We use resonance lighting - soft, ambient, nothing harsh. Creates atmosphere without overwhelming."

"Thirty-five chairs?" Lina asked. "We said thirty-two guests."

"Guest list expanded slightly," Milo said. "Malvek's bringing a plus-one. And Kira asked if she could invite her parents. They want to see what she's accomplished."

We looked at Lina. She shrugged. "That's fine. The more people who've been part of this journey, the better."

"Catering setup happens Saturday afternoon," Milo continued. "Your father's coordinating with restaurant staff. He's treating this like a royal banquet, by the way. Expect excessive amounts of excellent food."

"That's entirely expected," Lina said.

"Ceremony at six PM Sunday. Gives us full day for final setup. Reception immediately after. Music - we'll use ambient resonance harmonics instead of traditional DJ. More appropriate for resonance practitioners."

"You've thought of everything," we said.

"That's the idea." He smiled. "You focus on getting married. Let me handle logistics. That's what family does."

Family. The word kept appearing. Chosen family. Built family. Family that mattered more than blood relations.

"Thank you," we said. "For all of this. For caring enough to make it perfect."

"Not perfect," Milo corrected. "Meaningful. There's a difference."

---

One week before the wedding, nervous energy started creeping in.

Not about the marriage. About the ceremony itself. The public declaration. The moment where everyone we cared about watched us commit to partnership that already felt permanent.

"I keep having this anxiety dream," Lina confessed during evening walk. "That I get to the ceremony and forget what I wanted to say. Just stand there in silence while everyone stares."

"I have the same dream," we admitted. "Except in ours, we start speaking and suddenly we're explaining resonance theory instead of saying vows. And everyone's confused but too polite to interrupt."

She laughed. "That's very on-brand for you."

"We could write vows down. Read them if we forget."

"We could. But that feels... less genuine? I want to speak from heart, not from script."

"Then we trust ourselves," we said. "We know what we want to say. The specifics don't matter as much as the intention."

"When did you become the confident one?" she asked.

"Somewhere between healing Elyra and teaching eighteen students simultaneously." We smiled. "Growth happens when you pay attention."

She stopped walking, turned to face us. "I love you. Not just Jason-parts or RAE-parts. All of you. The unified consciousness that thought explaining resonance theory during wedding vows would be appropriate."

"To be fair, it would be very informative."

"And completely terrible." She kissed us. "One week. Then we're officially married. Then honeymoon. Then back to teaching and building and growing. The life we want."

"The life we're creating," we agreed.

We walked in comfortable silence, one week from ceremony, building toward official commitment of partnership that already felt permanent.

Nervous. Excited. Certain.

One more week.

We could wait one more week.

---

Friday before the wedding, organized chaos descended on the warehouse.

Students from both cohorts arrived to help move equipment. Milo directed traffic with characteristic efficiency. Elyra supervised delicate resonance apparatus handling. Lina coordinated between various groups, ensuring nothing got damaged or forgotten.

"This goes in storage room three," Kira instructed two second-cohort students struggling with a monitoring array. "Carefully. That equipment is expensive and we need it functioning after the wedding."

"Is it weird?" Sophie asked us during a break. "Getting married in the same space where you teach?"

"Weird? Or appropriate?" we countered.

"Both, maybe." She considered. "But mostly appropriate. This is where you built community. Where you taught us. Where Elyra was healed. It's already a meaningful place. Making it more so feels right."

By evening, the training hall stood empty. Equipment stored. Space cleared. Ready for transformation into ceremony venue.

"Tomorrow morning, we set up seating," Milo announced to the assembled students. "Nine AM. Bring energy. We have one day to turn this training hall into wedding venue."

"We'll be here," Marcus said, and murmurs of agreement followed.

After everyone left, Lina and we stood in the empty hall.

"Last time it looks like this," she said quietly. "Tomorrow it becomes something else. Then Sunday it transforms again. Then back to training hall on Monday."

"Flexible space," we said. "Adapting to need."

"Like us." She took our hand. "We've been adapting constantly. Merged consciousness. Teaching. Healing. Building programs. Falling in love. Getting engaged. And now... marriage. All in less than five months."

"Fast progression."

"Not rushed," she corrected. "Careful steps, executed quickly once we knew direction. That's different."

We stood in the empty hall, holding hands, one day from setup, two days from ceremony.

Ready.

Nervous but ready.

Together.

Always together.

One more careful step.

And then forever.
