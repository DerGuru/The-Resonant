# Part 4 - We Are Family | Ch. 06 - Expanding horizons

Six weeks after Elyra's complete healing, the teaching program had outgrown the warehouse.

Not physically - the space still accommodated our current eight students comfortably. But the waiting list had grown to twenty-three names. Applications arrived daily from practitioners across the city who'd heard about our approach. About students who'd been rejected by academies finding success here. About innovative teaching methods that actually worked.

"We need to make a decision," Elyra said during our weekly planning session. She moved with easy confidence now, no trace of limitation remaining. "Either we expand capacity or we start turning people away permanently."

"Expanding means more instructors," Milo pointed out. "You two can't teach twenty students with the same individual attention you give eight."

"We could train some of our current students to assist," Lina suggested. "Kira's advanced enough to help with basic concepts. Marcus too, despite his earlier struggles."

We considered the logistics. More students meant more responsibility. More complexity. But also more impact. More people learning ethical resonance practice instead of stumbling through self-teaching or accepting institutional rejection.

"What if we do cohorts?" we proposed. "Keep primary group size at eight to ten. But stagger start times. Current cohort continues advancing. New cohort begins in two months. By the time we're ready for a third cohort, first group is far enough along that advanced students can help with basics."

Elyra nodded slowly. "Sustainable growth. Not overwhelming ourselves trying to teach everyone at once. I like it."

"It means commitment though," Milo said. "Long-term planning. Infrastructure investment. This stops being a temporary program and becomes an actual institution."

"Is that what we want?" Lina asked, looking at us. "To build an institution? Or were we just trying to help a few people?"

Good question. Important question.

We thought about the past two months. The satisfaction of watching students progress. The way Kira had transformed from uncertain to confident. How Marcus had gone from frustrated to focused. Sophie's growing capability.

"We're good at this," we said finally. "Teaching. Not just surviving crises or accomplishing impossible tasks. Actually building skills in others. And there are people who need what we offer - practitioners rejected by traditional channels, self-taught individuals who need proper guidance, people with potential but no access to training."

"So that's a yes?" Elyra pressed.

"That's a yes. We expand. Carefully. Sustainably. But yes."

"Then we have work to do," Milo said, pulling up planning documents. "Formalize curriculum. Develop assistant instructor training. Set up proper application processes. This becomes real infrastructure instead of informal teaching."

The conversation stretched into evening, building framework for growth. By the time we finished, we had six months of planning outlined. Structured. Achievable.

We were building something that would outlast us. Something that mattered.

---

Two days later, during advanced training with our current cohort, Kira approached us with a question.

"Can I ask something personal?" she said after class.

"Of course."

"You and Lina. You're together, right? Not just working together but actually together?"

We blinked, caught off guard by the directness. "Yes. We are."

"I thought so. The way she looks at you. The way you move around each other - there's this comfortable synchronization that only happens with people who spend a lot of time together." Kira smiled. "It's nice. Seeing you two build something real while also building this program."

"That's... observant of you."

"I pay attention. It's part of why I'm good at resonance work - noticing patterns, understanding how things interact." She paused. "Are you going to marry her?"

Direct. Very direct.

"Eventually," we admitted. "We've talked about it. But there's no rush. We're both content with how things are developing."

"You should propose," Kira said with the certainty of youth. "Not tomorrow. But soon. Because anyone watching you two can see this is permanent. Might as well make it official."

"Is that advice or observation?"

"Both." She grinned. "You taught me to trust my pattern recognition. And the patterns between you and Lina? They're solid. Stable. Built to last."

After she left, we sat with that observation.

Solid. Stable. Built to last.

The same words could describe our teaching program. Our relationship with the team. Our merged consciousness.

We'd built something that worked. Something real.

Maybe Kira was right. Maybe it was time to make it official.

---

That evening, we took Lina to dinner. Not Morandi's - somewhere new. A small restaurant she'd mentioned wanting to try, nice but not intimidating.

"What's the occasion?" she asked over appetizers. "You don't usually suggest fancy dinners without reason."

"Can't we just want to spend nice evening with you?"

"You can. But you're nervous about something. I can tell." She studied us. "What's going on?"

Always perceptive. Always seeing through our attempts at subtlety.

"Kira asked if we were going to marry you," we said.

"Kira. Your student Kira." Lina's expression shifted to amusement. "And what did you tell her?"

"That we'd talked about it. That eventually, yes. But there was no rush."

"And she said?"

"That we should propose. Not tomorrow, but soon. That anyone watching could see this was permanent."

Lina was quiet for a moment, considering. "She's not wrong."

"About it being permanent? Or about proposing?"

"Both." She reached across the table, taking our hand. "I know we said eventually. When we're both ready. But..." She smiled. "I think I'm ready now. Have been for weeks. I've just been waiting for you to realize you are too."

Our breath caught. "You are?"

"I love you. All of you. The unified consciousness that's both Jason and RAE and something entirely new. I want to build a life with you - not just cohabitate or date or whatever we're calling this. I want partnership. Marriage. Commitment." She squeezed our hand. "So yes. I'm ready. Question is: are you?"

We thought about everything we'd built over the past three months. The teaching program. Elyra's healing. The relationship that had grown from awkward first date to comfortable partnership.

"Yes," we said. "We're ready too."

"Then propose," she said simply. "Officially. I'll say yes. But I want you to actually ask."

We hadn't planned this. Hadn't prepared anything. But looking at her across the table - at the woman who'd learned to love what we'd become instead of mourning who we'd been - we knew exactly what to say.

"Lina Morandi," we said, standing and moving to kneel beside her chair. Traditional. Jason would have been embarrassed. But we weren't just Jason anymore. "Will you marry us?"

She laughed - joy and surprise and certainty all mixed together. "Yes. Absolutely yes."

We didn't have a ring. Hadn't planned that far ahead. But it didn't matter. She pulled us up, kissed us, and the restaurant broke into spontaneous applause.

"We should probably get a ring," we said when she pulled back.

"Eventually," she agreed. "But this - this moment - this is what matters. Everything else is just details."

We sat back down, suddenly aware that we'd just gotten engaged. Actually engaged. Planning to marry.

"Your father is going to be insufferable," we said.

"Completely," she agreed cheerfully. "He's been asking when you'd propose for weeks. He'll probably claim credit for giving you the push you needed."

"Did he?"

"Little bit." She smiled. "But mostly you got there on your own. Just needed time to realize you were ready."

We finished dinner in comfortable conversation, planning nothing specific but knowing everything had changed. Not the relationship - that felt the same. But the commitment. The declaration that this was permanent, intentional, chosen.

Walking home afterward, hand in hand, Lina said quietly: "Thank you."

"For proposing?"

"For seeing me. For letting me learn to love who you've become instead of forcing me to accept it immediately. For giving us time to build something real before making it permanent." She looked up at us. "For being patient while I figured out how to love we instead of just Jason."

"Thank you for trying," we said. "For not giving up when it was hard. For choosing to see us as we are."

"Always," she said. "One careful step at a time."

"Together."

"Always together."

We walked in comfortable silence, engaged couple heading home to figure out what came next.

Tomorrow we'd tell everyone. Make plans. Deal with logistics.

But tonight we just existed in the knowledge that we'd found something rare. Someone who loved us completely. Someone we loved the same way.

One more impossibility achieved.

And somehow, we thought, that felt exactly right.

---

The next morning, we gathered everyone at the warehouse to share news.

Elyra, Milo, the current student cohort. Even Malvek joined remotely, curious about the "important announcement."

"We're engaged," we said simply. "Lina and us. Getting married."

Silence for about three seconds. Then chaos.

Kira squealed. Actually squealed. "I told you to propose! I knew you would!"

"You were right," we admitted. "About recognizing patterns."

Sophie hugged Lina enthusiastically. Marcus offered congratulations with genuine warmth. The other students joined in, excitement filling the space.

Milo approached more quietly. "Congratulations. Both of you. You've built something real."

"We have," Lina agreed.

Elyra's expression held satisfaction. "About time. You've been circling this for months. Good to see you finally committing."

"Harsh," we said.

"Accurate," she corrected. But she was smiling. "I'm happy for you. Both of you. You deserve this happiness."

Malvek's voice came through the comm system. "Congratulations. Though I should warn you - this makes the teaching program more complicated. Institutional policies around married instructors, conflict of interest considerations, that sort of thing."

"We'll figure it out," we said. "We've handled more complex challenges."

"True enough." We heard approval in his tone. "Then I wish you both happiness. And I look forward to seeing what you build together."

After the celebration settled, Milo pulled us aside.

"Have you thought about timeline?" he asked. "Wedding planning, ceremony, all that?"

"Not really. Just decided last night."

"Fair." He grinned. "But knowing you two, you'll want something simple. Meaningful. Not huge production."

"Accurate assessment."

"Then let me offer assistance. Venue coordination. Guest management. Technical support. Whatever you need. Consider it my gift."

"You don't have to—"

"I want to," he said firmly. "You're family. Both of you. This is what family does."

Family. Not blood relations. Not formal ties. But the people who'd stood beside us through transformation, crisis, healing, growth.

"Thank you," we said quietly. "That means more than you know."

"I know exactly what it means." He clapped our shoulder. "Now go plan a wedding. You've earned something happy and normal after everything else."

Happy and normal. Strange goals for people who'd achieved unprecedented merged consciousness, healed impossible damage, built revolutionary teaching methods.

But maybe that was exactly right. Maybe after all the impossible achievements, happy and normal was the most valuable impossibility of all.

We looked at Lina across the room, laughing with students, radiant with joy.

Yeah. We thought. This was exactly right.

One more careful step forward.

Together.

Always together.
