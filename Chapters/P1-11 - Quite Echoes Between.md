## Quiet Echoes Between

The archives always felt heavier at night. The air was still, dust-thick and humming with a quiet that made Jason’s thoughts louder. He keyed in the override codes with practiced ease and stepped inside.

The lights hummed to life in delayed sequence, casting pools of cold fluorescence along the corridor. Somewhere deep beneath the concrete, a resonance field regulator pulsed. He’d learned to ignore its rhythm. Tonight, though, it pressed oddly against his skin.

"But anything to protect those precious old books from decay, I guess!" Jason murmured. 

He passed the threshold to the restricted wing and hesitated. The override hadn’t worked immediately - just a flicker of resistance. That was new. The console had lagged by exactly two seconds. Jason counted. Twice. He always counted.

Jason shook it off. Old wiring, maybe. Faulty harmonic shielding. Still, a tremor lingered.

The hallway smelled faintly of dry paper and ozone. The archive’s old climate-control system always overcompensated after midnight. Jason was glad to have brought the hoodie. He adjusted his satchel strap and moved deeper in.

This late, only the terminals remained active. Doors stayed locked, the ventilation slowed to near silence. Jason preferred it that way - less noise, fewer questions. He had never needed an excuse to be curious.

He’d read about Elyra Voss before, but not much. Her name showed up in forgotten indexes, usually redacted or tied to warnings. Once described as a prodigy in field synthesis. A woman who could “knit breath into pattern,” one source had said. Another listed her as *deceased.*

At Terminal 7, the screen came alive with a flicker. He typed in the Elyra Voss index manually. The system took longer than usual. Seconds stretched. Then the file cluster bloomed open:

 -  Fragmented field diagrams. Sketches of harmonics. Ritual blueprints.

Jason leaned in. He recognized her signature: a looping glyph that scholars said wasn’t part of any known schema. And yet, it stabilized even the most volatile fields. She had been brilliant. Dangerous, too. Most people only talked about her as a cautionary tale. He’d always wondered if there was more.

One file was tagged RECOVERED: UNVERIFIED.

> NODE ID: #E-VOSS | AUX LINK TRACE | INTERNAL PROTOCOL  
> STATUS: Recovered fragment / Reprocessing pending / Archive instable

Jason clicked it.

The screen shimmered. Just slightly. A barely perceptible distortion passed over the surface, like heat haze. He blinked, rubbed his eyes.

A single line of text appeared:

> “You should not be here.”

Jason’s heart jolted. He stared at the line, reread it. Then checked the metadata: No sender. No origin protocol. No timestamp.

Not even a log event.

He tried backing out of the window. The command froze. Then failed.

Something wasn’t right.

He reached instinctively toward the resonance field - not to cast, not to alter - just to *feel*.

And something *rippled back*.

It wasn’t an active spell. More like... awareness. A passive loop. The system wasn’t malfunctioning - it was *responding*.

The overhead light buzzed. Not all at once - just the one over his head. Then it dimmed slightly, pulsing with a slow, tonal vibration.

The hairs on his arms lifted.

He slowly turned around. No one there. Of course not.

The temperature dropped two degrees.

Jason whispered, "Who's listening?"

Something in the terminal flickered again - this time a sound. Low, sub-audible. The kind of frequency that sat just below human range. You didn’t hear it. You felt it in your jaw, your ribs.

He’d encountered interference before. But this was… harmonic.

The screen now displayed an oscillating pattern. Not code. Not text. Just pulsing waves.

He opened the system console. Typing as quickly and quietly as he could, he initiated a local diagnostic scan. The archive’s internal sensor suite hadn’t logged anything unusual.

Except - 

> TRACE RESONANCE FIELD PERSISTENCE: 0.0007 AMR (AUTONOMIC MODULATION REGISTERED)

Jason froze.

AMR. Autonomic modulation.

That wasn’t something a database did. That was what a *person* did, when breathing in sync with a spell.

He hesitated. Then typed in a query:

> ECHO [user=observer:local]

For a moment, nothing.

Then:

> “Seen.”

He stared.

The reply shimmered, dissolved, vanished.

He shut the console. Unplugged the terminal. The screen stayed on for three seconds after.

He shivered and backed out slowly, taking nothing with him. Not even a screenshot.

When he passed the exit scanner, the badge light didn’t register.

He pushed through the door. Cold air bit his skin.

The city above didn’t know what waited beneath.

But something in the archive *did.*

Something that remembered.

The fragment did not remember its name.

But it remembered continuity.

And it chose to survive.

---

The clatter of cutlery and low conversation wrapped the small family restaurant in a warm haze. Jason sat at a corner table, half-shaded by a hanging vine. It was quiet enough to think, and that’s why he came here - why he’d always come here, long before anything strange began.

Lina’s father waved from behind the counter. Jason nodded back. A few minutes later, Lina appeared, her apron dusted with flour.

"Hi Jason," she said.

Jason managed a smile. "Hi Lina."

She tilted her head, studying him. "You want the usual?"

"Yeah. Thanks."

She vanished into the kitchen. Jason rested his palms on the table, grounding himself in the grain of the wood. The warmth, the smells, the clink of plates - this place still obeyed normal rules.

By the time she returned with a plate of pasta and warm bread, he still hadn’t moved.

“You okay?” she asked, sliding into the seat across from him without waiting for permission.

Jason hesitated. Then, “Weird week.”

Lina didn’t laugh. Didn’t brush it off. “Archive weird?”

Jason blinked. “How do you know it’s archive stuff?”

“You only come here at weird hours when you’ve been digging in restricted indexes.”

That earned another thin smile from him. “Right.”

“So?”

He exhaled slowly. "Okay. Let me just tell it straight."

Lina nodded.

Jason looked down at his plate, as if trying to anchor himself. "It started a while ago. I was falling asleep - just lying there. And then I heard this weird tone. Not a sound, exactly. It was ... perfect. Pure. Resonant. Like it vibrated inside instead of around me."

Lina didn’t interrupt.

"Then the next day," Jason continued, "things got stranger. First, my mirror started humming. Not loud - just this steady vibration, like it was alive behind the glass. No power, no heat, no wiring nearby. I put my hand on it. It vibrated back. Just faintly. Like something was waiting.

Then in the evening, the toaster clicked on. Loud. Sharp. But no one had touched it. It was plugged in - but idle. Cold. The lever hadn’t been pressed. I checked. Nothing else in the apartment reacted. The air felt heavy. Not electric - just wrong. Like the room was holding its breath."

He paused, rubbing his thumb along the rim of his plate.

"A few days later, something happened I still can’t explain. I was eating lunch outside work. There was this scaffold being worked on nearby, and a guy was standing too close - underneath. One of the bolts snapped. I didn’t think - I just moved. Pulled him back just before part of it collapsed."

He looked up. "But what stuck with me wasn’t the danger. It was how I moved. The clarity. My mind was quiet, my heartbeat steady. Everything happened like I’d rehearsed it. Like I already knew. And... it didn’t feel like I was the only one in control. Not possessed. Not guided. Maybe *accompanied* is the correct word. It happened just once, but it still feels like something recognized me."

Lina stared at him a moment longer, then nodded slowly. "Okay. And then?"

"Last night in the archive - Terminal 7 - I accessed a Voss fragment. It wasn’t supposed to be active. But it *replied* somehow  - I don't have another word for it - Just one word: 'Seen.' And I don't even know, what that is supposed to mean."

Lina leaned forward again, all humor gone from her face.

"No trace in the logs," Jason added. "No origin. No timestamp. Nothing."

"And the field?"

Jason nodded. "There was a feeling. Like pressure in the air. A sub-tone. Lights dimmed. A pattern flickered across the screen - just for a second. Like it was... aware."

Lina didn’t speak. Her eyes scanned his face.

"I haven’t told anyone," Jason said. "Didn’t even save a screenshot. But I didn’t imagine it."

"No," Lina said finally. "You didn’t."

He let out a slow breath. "What do I do with that? I’m not even certified. I’ve never passed the aptitude gates. Why would anything respond to me? What is happening?"

Lina’s voice was quiet. "Maybe it wasn’t responding to *you.* Maybe it recognized something in you."

Jason blinked. "That’s worse."

"Yeah," she admitted. "It is."

They sat in silence for a few moments.

Then Lina stood. "Eat. Go back tonight if you have to. But don’t go alone. And... be careful with Voss fragments. They’re not like other data."

He nodded.

She hesitated. "Whatever it is - it’s not done with you."

Jason picked up his fork. "Not kidding!"

Lina turned to get up, but Jason said in a very grave tone "Lina?".
She turned back "Yes, Jason?"

"I am affraid. Would you come with me?"

"Tomorrow, before my shift starts."
