# Chapter 1H – Familiar Static

Jason sat on the couch, phone in hand, thumb absently scrolling. The tea on the side table had gone cold an hour ago. The room was dark but for the glow of the screen and the soft blink of the router light beneath the shelf.

Outside, traffic murmured low and tired. The city didn’t sleep, but it softened.

He exhaled through his nose. The phone screen dimmed and locked. He didn’t unlock it again. Just dropped it onto the cushion beside him and let his head lean back.

He didn’t feel like getting up. Not for bed. Not for brushing his teeth. The couch was good enough.

He pulled the throw blanket over his legs, adjusted the pillow behind his head, and closed his eyes.

---

The tone came slowly.

Not a sound, not exactly. Not a ringing. More like the shape of a sound — a pressure that outlined something that should have been heard, but wasn’t. A phantom frequency.

Jason opened his eyes.

The room was still. The only light was the clock display on the microwave. No hum from the heater. No fridge compressor. No buzz from the street.

It was quiet.

But the shape of the sound still hung there, like a tension in the air.

He sat up. Rubbed his face. Walked over to the kitchen corner, leaned on the counter, listened.

Nothing.

He pressed his ear against the wall. Held his breath. Even placed a palm flat against the tiles.

Still nothing.

Jason frowned. "Probably just the plumbing."

He waited another moment, then turned back to the couch. The feeling had already started to fade.

He lay down again.

“Still weird, though,” he mumbled. “Weirdly... rhythmic.”

Sleep took longer than usual.

---

The next morning, Jason stood in his kitchen, chewing mechanically on a piece of toast. He stared at the weather widget on his phone, which insisted it would not rain, despite the heavy grey outside.

Behind him, the toaster clicked.

Jason turned. It was the toaster from the flea market — all chrome and red buttons, smug in its retro angularity. He hadn’t touched it since yesterday.

But it clicked again.

Not the spring. Not the button. Just a mechanical tick, like a relay resetting.

He narrowed his eyes. Walked over. Pressed his hand against the side.

It was warm.

Not burn-your-fingers hot, but definitely *not off*.

He unplugged it. The hum — was there a hum? — stopped.

Jason exhaled through his nose. “Of course it’s haunted.”

He gave it a few seconds, then plugged it back in.

Nothing happened.

“Ten bucks well spent.”

# Chapter 1I – Exceptions and Ghosts

The terminal interface blinked, as always. Grey text on dark background. Functional. Ugly. Unchanging.

Jason typed half a zoning code, tabbed twice, and opened the reference archive.

He didn’t expect anything out of the ordinary.

Which made the flagged entry stand out immediately.

> **Exception Report: Node Echo – #RAE.0001.x**
> Status: Incomplete / Access Level: Restricted
> Trace Origin: Unknown (non-repeating)

Jason blinked. That wasn’t a format he recognized.

He clicked. The file opened — slowly, like the system was thinking about it.

Inside: half-corrupted text. Strings of characters. A few half-rendered diagrams. One line stood out:

> *“Signal divergence recorded. Pattern echo non-reproducible. Node integrity unresolved.”*

And below that, in faded grey:

> *[Subject tag: ???]*

Jason squinted. He tried to copy the text — and the screen glitched.

The window closed itself.

He blinked. Looked around the interface. The tab was gone. The file no longer listed.

He tried the search function. Nothing.

He opened the cache.

Empty.

He stared at the screen.

“…Huh.”

He glanced around the room. No one nearby.

Then — on impulse — he hit the screenshot keys. The desktop flashed.

He saved it to a hidden folder, not because he thought it meant anything. Not yet.

Just in case.

He sat there for a moment longer.

Then, as always, he got back to work.
