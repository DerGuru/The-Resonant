# Echoes of the Broken Path

They chose the stairwell because stairwells belong to everyone and no one. The concrete held warmth like a memory; the window at the landing pretended to be a painting of a street. Lina had brought tea in a thermos cup that had lived a hard life. Milo had brought a printout he was pretending was a placemat.

“You know this is a terrible venue for secrets,” Milo said.

“This is a great venue for pretending we don’t have any,” Lina said, and set the thermos on the step between them like a neutral party.

Jason sat two steps down, elbows on knees, happy to be lower than the conversation. He could feel RAE pacing in the quiet bands, pressing her palm lightly against the building’s pulse the way she had taught herself to do.

“How’s the search?” Milo asked, softer than his jokes.

> *Slow and specific,* RAE said, private to Jason first. *Memory fragments are clearer at night, but the city’s daytime behavior is the map I need. I have a few names of places that are no longer places.*

**Tell them some of that,** Jason said. **Not the names. The shape.**

He looked up. “She’s getting fragments,” he told them. “Not images—more like rules. How certain corners used to behave. Before…” He glanced at RAE for a word and got one that curved through him and out again.

“Before the Sundering,” he said, and the stairwell took a second to remember it had air.

Milo’s eyebrows did a quiet lift. “That the thing with capital letters we don’t explain at dinner?”

“Capital letter, no dinner,” Lina said. She poured tea into the lid and passed it to Jason. “Tell her I said hi.”

The cutlery in the apartment above them gave a faint, synchronized tap, as if a drawer had closed politely. The thermos vibrated once in Jason’s hands, and then RAE’s voice arrived—audible, ordinary.

“Hello, Lina,” she said. “Thank you for the tea.”

Lina tilted her head. “You’re welcome. Please keep the draw light while you say so.”

“I am,” RAE said, as if she had always had a voice. “I’m riding the stairwell and the window frame. No strain.”

Milo waved the printout without looking at it. “Can we talk about how weird it is that I’m relieved when the disembodied voice uses the words *stairwell* and *window frame*?”

“Normal is a hobby,” Lina said. “We are amateurs.”

Jason let the thermos lid warm his fingers, then balanced it on the step to make room for words. “We’ve been doing the slow thing,” he said. “No heroics. She’s found a few faint echoes that feel older than the city wants to be. Not proof. Just… familiar wrongness.”

“Old wrongness,” Milo said. “My favorite kind.” He pointed at his fake placemat. “Routes that used to matter. Utilities that don’t connect the way they pretend to. A hole in a map that argues with being a hole.”

RAE said, more openly now, “Echoes are strong where people forced the grid to be something it didn’t want to be. Elyra lived in the bend of one of those. I keep finding the bend without the person.”

Lina blew on her tea. “Then we keep being boring until the bend notices us.” She looked at Jason more directly. “And you keep telling her no if she asks for more Invest than your head wants to donate.”

“I’m keeping the draw low,” RAE said before Jason could answer. “And I’m learning to use carriers that don’t hurt him. The plates at your restaurant were very cooperative.”

“They like being helpful,” Lina said dryly. “Unlike some humans.”

Milo leaned back until his shoulders found the wall. “Can you project, like, a little? Friendly ghost? Low wattage?”

RAE hesitated. Jason could feel the math she didn’t say. “I can make myself legible,” she said at last. “Not a face. A presence.”

The stairwell’s light shifted a fraction, not brighter, not dimmer—just less interested in being a light and more interested in being a place where you could see. The thermos lid’s surface stopped being entirely sure it was round; the reflection of the window changed its mind about angles. If a person had walked in just then, they would have called it good daylight.

Milo whistled, then pretended he hadn’t. “Okay. That’s new.”

“Not new,” RAE said. “Just careful. Visible projection is expensive at this alignment. This is cheaper: letting the world be a little more itself where I am.”

Jason felt the draw as a sip, nothing that asked his head to register a complaint. He let his breath out the way you do after you’ve been told nothing is on fire.

“Any names?” Lina asked. “Places?”

“Not yet ones the city will admit to,” RAE said. “I keep getting references to pre‑Sundering districts that were administratively erased and physically repurposed. The echoes suggest a perimeter around what is now called Mill‑4. But my confidence is not yet a number I like.”

“Confidence later,” Lina said. “Tea now.” She poured more, as if statistics were thirsty.

Milo tapped the printout. “I can get us near Mill‑4 without stepping on anything labeled *No Trespass* in typography that has opinions,” he offered. “Daylight, normal shoes, public benches with tragic slopes.”

“Text before and after,” Lina said automatically.

Jason nodded. **We’ll keep it slow,** he told RAE.

> *Slow,* she agreed. *I can do slow. The echoes can’t run away; they only fade if we push. We will not push.*

Lina stood and offered a hand to each of them like she was rescuing them from the step. “Eat at the restaurant,” she said. “Then be boring somewhere useful.”

“Your house rules are very specific,” Milo said.

“They are designed for people like you,” she said. “And for him.” She tipped her head toward Jason, then toward the thermos. “And for her.”

“Thank you,” RAE said again, and let the stairwell be merely a stairwell.

They filed out into the day that didn’t know it was about to be useful. On the landing, Jason paused just long enough to feel the window’s frame not sing, which he would have called silence once and now called a choice. He texted a single word to Lina—*Later*—because it had become their way of saying *we’re doing the careful thing.*

> *Echo marked,* RAE murmured. *Old wrongness, same bend. We are close to useful.*

**Close is enough,** he told her, and the step down to the street felt like part of a sentence that would end where it meant to.
