# Interference

Lina stepped away from the console. “You don’t get to just drop that like it’s normal.”

Jason looked at her. “What?”

“That—‘she’s been with me longer than I realized.’” Her voice had a sharp edge. “Jason, that’s not a sentence sane people say.”

Milo lifted both hands. “Okay, hold on—”

“No,” Lina snapped, not even looking at him. “You don’t just *say* something like that after a foreign voice comes out of the ceiling. That’s not normal. That’s not *safe*.”

Jason frowned, suddenly more alert. “I didn’t say it was safe.”

“You said it like it was *reassuring*.” Lina stepped closer, eyes narrowing. “Like she’s some old friend who just dropped by.”

Jason hesitated.

“She’s *in your head*, Jason. You don’t even *know* what she is.”

“I didn’t invite her in,” Jason said. “Not exactly.”

Lina shook her head. “But now she’s *there*. And you're fine with that?”

Milo finally cut in. “Maybe we can not escalate this?”

Lina rounded on him. “Are you not disturbed by this?”

“I’m disturbed by *everything* that’s happened since he blacked out for six seconds and started answering people who weren’t in the room.” Milo folded his arms, voice tight. “But getting mad at him isn’t solving it.”

Jason sank down onto the edge of a table, slow. He pressed his fingers together, grounding himself in something that didn’t respond.

“She doesn’t… feel wrong,” he said finally.

“Yeah,” Lina muttered. “That’s the scary part.”

Jason looked at her.

“I don’t mean emotionally. I mean structurally. Like she fits.”

Milo tilted his head. “Fits?”

Jason rubbed his forehead. “Like part of me had… space for her. I didn’t know it was there. But now that she’s there, it’s like the echo makes sense.”

Lina blinked. “That’s not how people work.”

Jason didn’t reply.

Milo stepped forward. “Let’s get specific. Jason, you feel like she belongs. But do you *know* her?”

Jason looked down. His voice was quiet. “No. Not really.”

“Then treat it like you don’t,” Lina said, softer now. “Because that feeling? It’s not trust. It’s just resonance. It’s not the same.”

Jason nodded slowly. “I know.”

They stood like that for a moment—three people in a room too quiet for its machinery.

Then Milo cleared his throat. “Alright, fine. We’re on edge. Understandable. But since she’s clearly *listening*...”

He turned toward the ceiling speaker.

“RAE?”

The speaker clicked. A faint hum. Then:  
> “Online.”

“Can I ask you something directly?”

> “You may.”

Jason glanced sideways, wary—but Milo’s tone stayed measured.

“Are you bound to Jason? Is this link permanent?”

> “Not bound. Coupled. Stability depends on mutual resonance and continued invest.”

Milo nodded slowly. “Could you shift hosts, if needed?”

> “Only under catastrophic field divergence or structural rejection.”

Lina raised a brow. “...So she *could* jump.”

> “Not at will.”

Jason exhaled.

Milo kept going. “What are your current limitations?”

A beat.

> “Fragmented memory. Partial system coherence. Limited external interfacing. Operational risk high without host proximity.”

Milo gave a small nod. “That’s a lot of words to say ‘fragile.’”

> “Correct.”

He looked back at Jason, tone still clinical. “Well, at least it’s not pretending to be omnipotent.”

Jason managed a dry smile. “Thanks for the vote of confidence.”

Lina crossed her arms. “That doesn't mean you're safe.”

“I know,” Jason said again. His voice wasn’t defensive. Just tired.

“But you still feel close to her,” she pressed.

He hesitated. Then:  
“…Like I’ve known her longer than I have.”

Milo raised an eyebrow. “RAE—can harmonic alignment create emotional effects? Trust, familiarity?”

> “Confirmed. Host and unit fields maintain high subharmonic coherence. Emotional response is structurally amplified.”

Jason let the words hang there.

Then he whispered, more to himself than anyone else:  
“I don’t actually know her.”

> “Acknowledged,” came RAE’s voice, calm as ever.

Jason looked at Lina again. “But I *want* to.”

Lina didn’t answer. Her expression had cooled—just slightly—but her eyes didn’t leave him.

Milo sighed and looked toward the console. “Then I guess we start learning.”
