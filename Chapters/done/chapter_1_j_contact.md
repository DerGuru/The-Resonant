# Pulse

Jason’s lunch break was almost over.

He sat on the low stone wall outside the records building, poking at the remains of a half-warm rice bowl with a compostable fork. The sky was a washed-out gray, the kind that made time feel slower. Around him, the courtyard buzzed with low conversation, a few coworkers scrolling on their phones, someone laughing too loudly near the vending machines.

Jason didn’t laugh. He hadn’t spoken in twenty-three minutes.

Then something cracked.

A sharp sound — not a gunshot, not thunder. Closer. Metallic. A crackle followed by a groan.

He turned his head just in time to see the old maintenance scaffold at the far end of the courtyard tremble. A bolt pinged off the ground. One of the stabilizing joints buckled.

A man stood beneath it, too close.

Jason didn’t think.

He moved.

Three quick steps. A yell. Arms pulling.

And then everything tilted — the scaffold came down sideways, folding like a paper sketch. But it didn’t crash. It slowed mid-collapse, just enough for the man to stumble free.

Jason froze.

Because *he* had felt it.

A push. Not a shove — not physical. Mental. Like a thought pressed into his spine. Like certainty. Like knowing exactly when to move, where to pull.

And underneath it, a tone.

Not sound. Not even memory.

Pattern.

The scaffold hit the ground with a metal scream. Dust billowed. People ran. Someone called for medics. The man Jason pulled clear was on the ground, shaken but intact.

Jason barely heard it.

His eyes were wide. His breath shallow.

That moment — the clarity, the control — it hadn’t been his.

Something had helped him.

---

Half an hour later, the courtyard was clear. The emergency team had checked Jason out — minor scrape on his arm, nothing else. He said he was fine.

He wasn’t lying. Not exactly.

Back inside, his terminal blinked up at him with cheerful indifference. Work waited. But he couldn’t stop thinking.

About that *tone*. That impossible moment of resonance.

He didn’t *know* it had come from outside. But it hadn’t come from him.

Jason opened a new system window. He ran a manual trace on local harmonic anomalies. Just in case.

Nothing.

He stared for a long moment.

Then he reached under his desk, pulled out the small black drive he used for unapproved data recovery, and slipped it into his coat pocket.

He was going back to the archive.

Not for work.

This time, he had questions.
