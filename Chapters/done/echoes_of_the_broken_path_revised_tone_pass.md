# Echoes of the Broken Path

They picked the stairwell because stairwells are public in a way that doesn’t invite questions. Concrete, paint that had opinions, a window trying to be a postcard. The building’s pulse came up through the rails and got lost in the landing like weather does.

Lina arrived with a thermos that had lost most of its original shine. Milo arrived with a stack of printouts pretending to be placemats. Jason arrived with the kind of tired that doesn’t ask for attention.

“You realize this is a terrible venue for secrets,” Milo said, sitting on the step like he owned gravity.

“This is a great venue for pretending we don’t have any,” Lina said. She set the thermos on the middle step as if convening a meeting. “Tea.”

Jason took the lid and felt some part of his shoulders try to come down. The stairwell soundscape was familiar—door closers, distant pipes, a neighbor’s soft catastrophe with keys.

> *I can use the stairwell return and the window frame as carriers if you want me audible.* *Low draw, slow encoding.*

**Keep it low. If we make you very obvious, we’ll end up with an audience.**

> *Low,* she agreed, shifting into the part of his attention that made room without being loud.

“How’s everyone’s favorite invisible collaborator?” Milo asked, as if they’d invited RAE to lunch and she’d texted she was running three minutes late.

Jason tipped the lid in a little salute. “Listening. Behaving. Learning to be cheap.”

> *Not cheap—efficient,* RAE corrected, dry. *Alignment is stable. Twenty‑one to twenty‑three percent, drifting with load.*

Lina held out her hand for the lid. “Hi, RAE,” she said to the thermos, because manners cost nothing. “Please keep the draw light.”

The lid shivered once in Jason’s palm. The teaspoons in the upstairs flat gave a polite, single tap as if a drawer closed and agreed. “Hello, Lina,” RAE said, the voice riding the metal and settling like steam. “Light draw. Carriers only.”

Milo wiggled a printout loose. “Friendly ghost that speaks in stairwells. Great. I can work with that.”

He flattened a map page across two steps. The lines were the city doing its best to be helpful: utilities, drains, the antique ribbon of a long‑abandoned spur. On top, in Milo’s blunt pencil, were small rectangles and circles that meant *maybe* and *don’t do that yet*.

“Report,” Lina said.

“Slow pings, steady,” Jason said. “We’re revisiting the same corners at different hours. Her math likes it.”

> *It does,* RAE said. *Pressure cycles change the cavity responses. With time offsets I can bracket more accurately without increasing draw.*

“English translation,” Lina said, passing the lid back.

"We walk by places when they’re tired and when they’re caffeinated", Jason said. "She learns the difference and stops needing my head to pay for the lesson."

“Approved,” Lina said. “What about the hole?”

Milo turned another sheet. The map changed scale; the mask became obvious in the way absence is obvious once you see it. An oval of nothing where lines should have been argumentative.

“Here,” Milo said, tapping the blank with his pencil. “Old Sector Mill‑4 perimeter. The training mirror gave us just enough to circle it with crayon.”

“Daylight, normal shoes,” Lina said automatically.

“Daylight, normal shoes,” Milo echoed, like a student who liked this class and the teacher.

Jason didn’t look at the hole for long. It wasn’t a superstition; it was policy. Look long enough at a thing and your face tells on you.&#x20;

"We’ll approach from streets with receipts", he mused. **Buy water we don’t need and ask about benches.**

> *I will bias sampling toward carriers you tolerate—stairwell returns and radiator harmonics—no window frames without warning*, RAE added.

“Good,” Lina said, answering the part Jason hadn’t said out loud. “After, you text. Before, you text. If a clipboard adopts you, buy something at the bakery and sit like you meant to be there all day.”

“Alibi calories,” Milo said. “My favorite kind.” He dug for another map. “Also: minor change to our ritual. I made a scale.” He produced a printed chart with columns labeled **Boring**, **Almost Interesting**, **We Leave Now**.

Jason leaned to look. “What’s the unit?”

“Benches,” Milo said solemnly. “One bench is very boring. Two benches is we talk quietly. Three benches and I gossip about how badly the city salts the corners.”

“Four benches?” Lina asked.

“Four benches is we go home and I pretend the printer jammed all night,” Milo said.

"Acceptable heuristic", RAE said. "I can map benches to pressure safe‑windows. A low‑resolution scale may outperform false precision."

“Look at that,” Jason said. “Our friendly ghost likes your scale.”

Milo grinned. “Finally, a critic I can please.”

They drank the tea in the holy order Lina insisted on: sip, breathe, speak. The stairwell behaved. Jason felt the quiet in the way his back muscles decided not to rehearse disasters.

“Tell me the word again,” Lina said after a minute, the one RAE had tried out in the small hours. “The old break.”

"Sundering", RAE said, audible this time because the thermos allowed it. "The city’s resonance spine shifted—some by disaster, more by decisions. I’m looking for someone who learned to live in the bend."

“Elyra,” Jason said.

"Yes. I keep catching her afterimage in places where the grid was made to behave. The echo is stronger where people forced it to be something it didn’t want."

Lina watched Jason’s face instead of the empty oval on the map. “And your head?”

"Quieter." He let that be true. "She’s keeping to the bands that don’t make the world brighter than it is."

“Good.” Lina screwed the lid back on the thermos with the efficiency of someone who had closed too many restaurants. “Then here’s the rest: you don’t play hero. You don’t buy trouble. You don’t educate men with clipboards. If asked, you were helping me label pantry bins and arguing about bench slope.”

“Bench slope is a menace,” Milo said.

“It really is,” Lina agreed.

A neighbor ghosted past the landing, gave them the polite oh‑it’s‑you nod, and continued up. The stairwell kept its secrets by being too boring to notice.

> *I can project a touch more without cost,* RAE said, as if offering a napkin. *Not a voice—legibility. So if someone steps in, they see ordinary light doing its job.*

**Do it small.**

The landing didn’t get brighter, exactly; it decided to be easier to look at. Shadows picked clear edges. The window stopped trying to be a glossy rectangle and quietly became a view.

Milo let out a low note of appreciation and pretended it had been the building. “Okay,” he said. “That feels like cheating, but the good kind.”

"It is not cheating", RAE said. "It is encouraging the room to remember itself."

Lina tapped a knuckle against the thermos. “Approved. Just don’t spend Invest on it if your numbers go wobbly.”

"Logged. Draw remains light."

They sketched a path that went nowhere dangerous and everywhere useful: two blocks past Mill‑4 on the legal side of the fence; three exits identified in case a clipboard learned words like *perimeter*; a bakery that pretended to need their opinion on muffins.

“Check‑ins,” Lina reminded. “Before and after.”

“Before and after,” Jason repeated.

Milo gathered his placemats into a stack and made paper‑tapping noises like a small, satisfied printer. “We could call someone,” he said, and watched both of them not blink. “Kidding. Kidding. I like my day job and my lack of court dates.”

"External calls would wake systems we don’t need awake", RAE said. "Slow is faster here."

“Slow is faster,” Lina echoed. She stood and offered each of them a hand, because she liked the ritual of getting people off steps. “Eat at the restaurant. Then be boring somewhere useful.”

Jason took the hand and tried not to think about how much better his head felt when he did what he was told.

They filed out. On the landing Jason paused, purely to practice not looking like a person who pauses, and let his fingertips rest against the window frame the way a man does when he’s checking for a draft. The frame gave him nothing helpful to work with. That was helpful.

He texted **Starting loop. Normal shoes.** and got **Be boring. Pastry tax applies.** in reply before the dots had time to tease.

> *Echo present, faint*. *Same bend as before. Don’t answer it; let it talk to the glass.*

**Copy.**&#x20;

He fell into step with Milo, who pointed at a bench with tragic slope and said, “One and a half benches,” like that meant anything to anyone, which now it did.

They turned toward the river, walking like men whose day contained only medium‑sized errands. Behind them, the stairwell remembered how to be just a stairwell, and the city kept its pulse in its pockets.
