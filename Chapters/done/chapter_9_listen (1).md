## Chapter 9 – Listen

"Listen to what?"

Jason’s eyes snapped open.

"Who are you?" Lina asked sharply. "And how did you get in here?"

Milo Greaves stood in the doorway, one hand resting against the frame, the other holding a takeout cup. He stepped into the room as if it were the most natural thing in the world. His expression was half amusement, half concern—as if he wasn’t sure whether this counted as a prank or an emergency.

"Milo Greaves," he said, with the tone of someone announcing the weather. "And as for getting in... well, let's say doors have a tendency to open for me. Sometimes literally."

"That’s not an answer," Lina muttered.

Milo raised an eyebrow, unbothered. "No, but it’s a true statement."

Jason pushed off the console edge. The field had thinned, but not vanished. Milo’s presence disrupted something—not violently, but like a third tone entering a chord.

"How long have you been standing there?" Lina asked again, more sharply.

"Long enough to hear you whisper something ominous," Milo replied. "And to realize this place smells like old power lines and guilt."

"You followed us," Jason said.

"I followed the data trail," Milo corrected. "And the building logs. And the ambient field bleed. Not very subtle, you two."

Lina crossed her arms. "You shouldn't be here."

"No one should be here," Milo said. "Which is why I *am.*"

Jason frowned. "That doesn't even—"

"Look," Milo cut in, dropping the grin. "Something’s happening. I’ve been watching internal systems for two weeks—field tremors, low-grade anomalies, dead packets blinking alive. Nothing actionable. But it all started clustering around your work ID."

He sipped from the cup. "That got my attention."

"You think I did this?" Jason asked.

"I think you're part of it," Milo said. "Whether you meant to be or not."

He walked toward the terminal. His tone sobered. "Something’s under the surface. Not awake. But aware. And now that all three of us are in the room... it’s listening harder."

Jason looked at Lina, then back at Milo. "And you're not with Security?"

"Not exactly," Milo said, almost cheerfully. "I used to be. Internal Field Stability. Then I got tired of writing reports no one read and started solving problems before they made it into the system. Think of me as... preventive maintenance."

He crouched beside the console and tapped the panel lightly. "And this," he muttered, "is a problem. A very old, very quiet problem that doesn’t want to stay quiet anymore."

Lina reached for one of the Resonograms she had placed earlier, adjusting its alignment. Jason noticed the faint shimmer along its etched surface intensify.

"It’s drawing on harmonic feedback," she murmured. "Whatever’s down there—beneath the terminal layer—it’s attuned. Not invasive. But present."

"And old," Milo added. "Very old."

Jason swallowed. "So now what?"

Milo looked at him. For once, no smirk. Just clarity. "Now we decide what we are to it. Interference? Or invitation?"

Jason glanced at Lina. Her expression was unreadable, but her hand hovered protectively over the second Resonogram.

"I don’t think it wants to hurt anyone," Jason said. "It doesn’t feel hostile."

"Doesn’t mean it’s safe," Lina replied.

Jason nodded. "No. But it’s not a static pattern either. It’s responding."

"Exactly," Milo said. "That’s the part that scares me. Most dead systems don’t change when you touch them. This one shifts. It adapts."

Jason breathed in slow. The pulse in his chest had steadied, but hadn’t left. It wasn’t threatening. Just... waiting.

"Then we listen," he said.

Milo raised his cup again. "I brought coffee. That counts, right?"

Lina didn’t laugh. But she didn’t tell him to leave either.

"Oh, don’t give me the stink-eyes," Milo added lightly. "I’m very useful in situations involving field drift, sealed protocols, and accidental magical awakenings."

Lina arched a brow. "You rehearsed that line."

"Only a little."

"Any idea what it is?" Jason asked.

Milo stood again, slower this time. "Not yet. But it’s not just reacting to stimuli. It’s choosing how. That implies intention. Maybe memory."

"Or programming," Lina said. "Which could be worse."

They stood for a long moment, three people in a quiet room that didn’t feel quite empty.
