# Ch. 11 - "I'm not interested in being observed"

That night, the team gathered at the warehouse.

The industrial yard after dark looked like a place where the future had died quietly.

Rusted equipment. Cracked pavement. Security lights that flickered when the wind hit them right. And in the center: the anomaly Milo had been tracking for weeks.

Jason, Lina, and Milo stood at the fence line, cheap ponchos keeping off the drizzle, handheld meters borrowed from Elyra held carefully.

"Readings are off," Milo said, checking his device. "Resonance signature refuses known classification. It's not following harmonic theory."

"Could be interference," Lina suggested.

"Or it could be something new." Milo adjusted settings. "Jason, can you sense it?"

Jason reached out with his perception. The yard's resonance was... wrong. Not hostile. Just fundamentally misaligned with everything he'd learned about how harmonic fields should behave.

*This pattern doesn't fit standard categories,* RAE said. *It's either a natural anomaly or something deliberately engineered.*

**Which?**

*Insufficient data. But the periodicity suggests intention. Look—*

Lightning flashed. Far in the distance, but the timing was precise. Too precise.

"Did anyone else notice that?" Jason asked.

"Notice what?" Lina said.

"The lightning. It's synchronized with the resonance pulses."

Another flash. Exactly nine seconds after the first.

Then another. Nine seconds again.

"That's not natural," Milo said, his voice tight. "Weather doesn't follow mathematical patterns."

They observed for twenty minutes. Documented intervals. Photographed the yard. Recorded readings that made no sense according to established theory.

And through it all, the lightning continued. Regular. Deliberate. Wrong.

"We need to go," Lina said finally. "This feels like a trap. Or bait."

"Agreed," Jason said. His instincts were screaming caution.

They packed quickly, efficiently. Made their way back to the tram stop in silence.

Only when they were blocks away did anyone speak.

"Someone's using weather as a carrier," Milo said. "That shouldn't be possible. The energy requirements alone—"

"Unless they're not generating it," Lina interrupted. "Unless they're just... steering it. Redirecting natural processes."

"That would take precision we don't have," Jason said.

"We don't have it. But someone does." Lina looked back toward the yard. "And they're experimenting. Testing capabilities. In an abandoned industrial zone where nobody's watching."

*Or where everyone assumes nobody's watching,* RAE added. *But we were. Which means we're now a variable in whatever equation they're solving.*

The thought sat heavy.

"We tell Elyra," Jason said. "Show her the data. See if she recognizes the pattern."

They rode the tram in silence, each lost in their own thoughts.

Jason kept replaying the lightning strikes. Nine seconds. Nine seconds. Nine seconds.

There was meaning there. A message, maybe. Or a warning.

Either way, they'd stumbled onto something bigger than they'd expected.

And that, more than anything, made him want to run very far away.

But he didn't.

Because running meant not knowing. And not knowing was its own kind of danger.

Better to understand the threat. Even if understanding made it more frightening.

*You're learning wisdom,* RAE observed.

**Or I'm just getting better at being scared productively.**

*Those are often the same thing.*

Jason smiled despite himself.

Tomorrow, they'd analyze the data. Compare it to known patterns. Try to understand what they'd witnessed.

But tonight, he needed to process the simple fact:

Someone out there had capability that exceeded anything he'd seen.

And they weren't hiding it carefully enough.

Which meant either they didn't care who knew...

Or they wanted to be found.

Neither option was comforting.
