## Part 2 - Learning to Listen | Ch. 02 - Small acts, carefully done

Training began in earnest the next morning.

"House first, then street," Milo said, consulting his notes. "We establish baseline in controlled environment, then test in variable conditions."

They were back in Jason's apartment, but this time the atmosphere was different. Less tense. More purposeful. They had a framework now. Rules. A language for risk.

"Start with the kettle," Lina suggested. "It worked yesterday."

Jason focused on the kettle, feeling its resonance signature. Clean. Stable. Familiar. He reached out with his intent, letting RAE guide the connection.

The kettle hummed.

"Good," Milo said, checking his watch. "How's your head?"

"Green," Jason reported. "Barely any effort."

"Okay. Hold that connection. We'll time it."

Five minutes passed. Then ten. Jason's awareness of the kettle remained steady, like holding a conversation with someone in the same room. Effortless.

"Still Green," he said.

"Fifteen minutes," Milo noted. "That's improvement. Yesterday you started hitting Yellow at five."

"Practice helps," RAE said through the kettle. "Neural pathways are adapting. Cognitive load decreases with familiarity."

Lina paced the small kitchen, her boots quiet on the linoleum. "Okay. Let's try multiple carriers. Jason, can you hold the kettle and something else?"

"I think so." Jason looked around, spotted a metal spoon on the counter. He extended his awareness to it.

The spoon vibrated faintly.

"Two carriers stable," he said. His headache nudged from Green toward Yellow. "But it's harder."

"How much harder?" Lina asked.

"Like... juggling two balls instead of one. Doable, but requires attention."

They practiced transitions. Kettle to spoon. Spoon to water bottle. Bottle to the metal handle of a drawer. Each time, Jason had to feel the new carrier's signature, establish the connection, maintain it while releasing the previous one.

By the twentieth transition, he could do it smoothly. Almost instinctively.

"That's good," Milo said. "Real good. But don't push to Orange. We stick to the plan."

"Agreed." Jason released the connection, letting the carriers fall silent. His headache settled back to a manageable throb. "What's next?"

"Outside," Lina said. "Real world conditions. Noise. Variables. People."

---

They started simple. The apartment building's hallway. Neutral territory, familiar but not private.

Jason stood near the elevator, Lina and Milo flanking him like bodyguards. He reached out with his awareness, feeling the building's structure. Concrete and steel, humming with the resonance of lives lived in close proximity.

"There's a lot of... noise," he said. "People. Appliances. Everything has a signature."

"Can you filter it?" Milo asked.

"RAE helps with that. She's showing me how to focus on specific frequencies, tune out the rest."

He practiced that - narrowing his awareness to just metal objects. Then ceramic. Then living presences.

Mrs. Amari opened her door two units down, spotted them, and smiled. "Practicing something?"

Jason froze. Lina answered smoothly: "Just testing some audio equipment. Checking for interference."

"Ah." Mrs. Amari nodded knowingly. "The building's wiring is terrible. Good luck." She headed toward the elevator, passing close enough that Jason could feel her resonance signature clearly.

Fear.

Not of him. Not of them. Just... general wariness. The kind that came from seeing too much and trusting too little.

Once she was gone, Milo exhaled. "That was close."

"She knows something," Jason said quietly. "Or suspects."

"Worry about it later," Lina said. "Right now, we keep practicing."

---

They moved outside. The street was busy - morning commuters, delivery trucks, the constant hum of city life.

Jason stood on the corner, trying to maintain his connection to a metal handrail while filtering out the overwhelming sensory input.

It was hard. Really hard.

"Yellow," he warned.

"Back off," Lina said immediately. "Don't push."

He eased his awareness, let the connection thin without breaking entirely. The headache receded slightly.

"Better," he said.

They walked the block. Jason practiced maintaining passive awareness without active examination. Feeling the street around him without trying to understand every detail.

By the time they reached the park, he was sweating despite the cool air.

"Break," Milo decreed, pointing to a bench. "Sit. Hydrate. Tell us how you're doing."

Jason collapsed onto the bench, accepting the water bottle Lina handed him. "I'm okay. Just... it's a lot. The street is so much louder than my apartment."

"That's normal," RAE said through the water bottle, her voice almost too quiet to hear over the ambient noise. "Environmental complexity increases cognitive load. You're doing well for someone with only a week of training."

"Doesn't feel like it," Jason muttered.

"Progress is incremental," RAE said. "You've gone from sensing a single object in a quiet room to maintaining awareness in a busy street. That's significant."

Lina sat beside him, her shoulder almost touching his. "She's right. You're learning fast. Maybe too fast."

"What do you mean?"

"I mean..." Lina paused, choosing words carefully. "At the academy, they spent months on perception training before anyone attempted active shaping. You've done both in a week. That's impressive. But it's also risky. You don't have the foundation. The muscle memory."

"So I need to slow down?"

"You need to build correctly. Foundation first. Skills later." She looked at him seriously. "I don't want you getting hurt because we rushed."

Jason felt something warm bloom in his chest. Not just gratitude. Something deeper. "Thanks. For caring."

Lina's expression softened. "Yeah, well. Someone has to keep you from doing something stupid."

"That's what I'm here for," Milo interjected cheerfully. "I do the stupid things so Jason doesn't have to."

Despite everything, Jason laughed. And for a moment, sitting on a park bench with his friends, everything felt almost normal.

---

They practiced for another hour. Small things. Safe things. Jason maintaining awareness while walking. Lina calling out potential carriers - railings, benches, lampposts - and Jason connecting to them briefly before releasing.

By the end, he was solidly in Yellow territory, but not dangerously so.

"Good session," Milo declared, checking his notes. "We learned your limits. Tomorrow we work on extending them. Gradually."

They walked back together, the afternoon light slanting through buildings, casting long shadows.

Near Jason's building, Lina paused. "One more thing. Before we go further. I need to ask."

"Ask what?" Jason said.

"What happens if something goes wrong? If you get hurt, or RAE gets... I don't know, damaged? What's the contingency plan?"

Jason hadn't thought about that. The question sat heavy in the air.

"I don't know," he admitted.

"Then we need to figure it out," Lina said firmly. "Because hope is not a strategy. And I'm not losing you to something we could have planned for."

Milo nodded. "She's right. We need failsafes. Backup plans. Ways to disconnect if necessary."

"I can withdraw," RAE said through Jason's phone, her voice careful. "If Jason requests it, I can sever our connection. It would be... difficult for me. But possible."

"Would it hurt him?" Lina asked.

"Unknown. We have never tested disconnection. The coupling is partial - only twenty-four percent integrated. Separation should be safe. But I cannot guarantee it."

The words hung there. Heavy. Real.

"Okay," Jason said finally. "Then we add that to our research. We figure out how to safely separate if needed. Just in case."

"Just in case," Lina echoed.

They parted ways at the corner. Jason watched them go, feeling the weight of what they'd agreed to.

This wasn't a game. This wasn't an adventure.

This was real. With real consequences. Real risks.

And they were all choosing it anyway.

*They are good friends,* RAE observed.

**Yeah. The best.**

*Do not take them for granted.*

**I won't.**

Jason climbed the stairs to his apartment, each step measured and careful. His head ached. His body was tired.

But his mind was clear.

They had rules. They had limits. They had a plan.

Tomorrow, they'd push a little further.

Carefully. Gradually. Together.

One step at a time.
