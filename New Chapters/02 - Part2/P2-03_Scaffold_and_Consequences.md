## Part 2 - Learning to Listen | Ch. 03 - You saved them, but you almost died

The scaffold buckled without warning.

Jason was at work, halfway through digitizing a box of land deeds from the 1970s, when his phone buzzed. Lina's name on the screen.

He stepped into the hallway before answering. "Hey."

"Where are you?" Her voice was tight. Controlled. Scared.

"Work. What's wrong?"

"How fast can you get to Porter Street? Near the old theater?"

"Ten minutes if I run. Why?"

"Milo's here. There's... Jason, something's happening. Structural collapse. People trapped. We can't wait for emergency services."

Jason's blood went cold. "What do you mean you can't wait?"

"I mean the scaffold is falling *now*. And there are kids in the courtyard below."

He was already moving, grabbing his coat, his badge, heading for the exit. "I'm coming. Don't do anything stupid."

"Too late. Get here fast."

The line went dead.

Jason ran.

---

Porter Street was chaos.

A construction site had failed - scaffolding twisted and sagging, held by cables that screamed with strain. Below, in a small courtyard used as a daycare play area, children huddled with two adults who were trying to shield them with their own bodies.

The scaffold wouldn't hold much longer. Minutes, maybe. And emergency services were still blocks away, sirens distant but not close enough.

Lina spotted him, waved him over. She and Milo stood at the edge of the courtyard, faces pale.

"Tell me you can do something," Lina said.

Jason's mind raced. The scaffold. The weight. The resonance of stressed metal and failing structure.

"I can try. But I've never done anything this big."

"Then we improvise," Milo said. "What do you need?"

"Time. And quiet. And..." Jason looked at RAE's presence in his awareness. "A lot of Invest."

*This is dangerous,* RAE said, her voice urgent. *The structure is failing in multiple places. Stabilizing it will require more capacity than you have safely developed.*

**If we don't try, those kids die.**

*If we try and fail, you could be permanently damaged.*

**Then we don't fail.**

Jason moved closer, feeling the scaffold's resonance. It was wrong. Discordant. Fractures propagating through connections that should have been solid.

"Lina," he said, not taking his eyes off the structure. "I need you to tell me if I start showing signs of overload. Nosebleed, loss of consciousness, anything. You call Red immediately."

"I will."

"Milo, keep people back. And if this goes bad... get everyone away from me."

"Jason - "

"Promise me."

"I promise."

Jason took a breath. Closed his eyes. Reached out.

The scaffold fought him. Not intentionally - it was just physics, weight and strain and material failure cascading through steel that couldn't hold anymore.

*I'm going to help,* RAE said. *But this will hurt. I'm sorry.*

**Do it.**

She did.

The world sharpened. Jason's awareness expanded, encompassing the entire structure at once. He could feel every stress point, every hairline fracture, every bolt threatening to fail.

And then, carefully, precisely, he began to stabilize.

Not by adding strength. Not by forcing the metal to hold. But by redistributing the load. Finding the points where small adjustments would ease pressure elsewhere. Harmonizing the dissonance into something that could, briefly, hold together.

It was like conducting an orchestra where every instrument was screaming and he had to make them all play the same note.

His head exploded with pain.

Blood ran from his nose. He tasted copper.

"Jason - " Lina's voice, distant.

"Not yet," he gritted out. "Almost - "

One more adjustment. One more redistribution of force.

The scaffold steadied.

Below, the adults were already moving, shepherding children away from the courtyard with urgent efficiency. Thirty seconds. That's all they needed.

Thirty seconds that felt like thirty years.

Jason held the resonance, feeling his body protest, feeling something in his cognitive patterns strain and stretch and threaten to *snap* - 

The last child cleared the courtyard.

"Now!" Milo shouted. "Let it go!"

Jason released the connection.

The scaffold collapsed with a sound like thunder, metal hitting concrete in a cascade of destruction that would have killed everyone below if they'd still been there.

But they weren't.

They were safe.

And Jason was falling.

---

He woke up in Milo's apartment, lying on the couch with a wet cloth on his forehead.

"Welcome back," Milo said quietly.

Jason tried to sit up. The world spun. His head felt like someone had taken a hammer to it from the inside.

"Don't move," Lina said, her hand on his shoulder. "You've been out for twenty minutes."

"The kids - "

"Safe. All of them. You did it." Her voice cracked slightly. "You absolute *idiot*, you did it."

Jason managed a weak smile. "You're welcome?"

"Don't you ever - " She stopped, took a breath. "That was too close, Jason. Your nose wouldn't stop bleeding. Your eyes rolled back. We thought - "

She didn't finish. Didn't need to.

"I'm sorry," Jason said quietly. "But I couldn't let them die."

"I know." Lina wiped at her eyes, angry at herself for showing emotion. "I know. But next time, we find another way."

"Agreed," RAE said through Milo's laptop speaker. Her voice sounded... tired. Strained. "Jason, I need to tell you something."

"What?"

"What we did today pushed both of us to our limits. Beyond them, actually. I had to draw on reserves I shouldn't access. And you... your neural pathways are inflamed. Not permanently damaged, but stressed. You need to rest. Complete rest. No resonance work for at least three days."

"Three days?" Jason protested weakly.

"Minimum. Possibly a week. Your body needs time to heal." RAE paused. "I'm sorry. I should have stopped you sooner."

"You tried. I insisted." Jason closed his eyes, exhaustion pulling at him. "We saved them, RAE. That matters."

"Yes. But next time, we find a way that doesn't nearly kill you."

"Next time," Jason agreed.

---

He slept for sixteen hours straight.

When he woke, the headache had receded to a dull throb. Manageable. Almost normal.

Lina was asleep in Milo's recliner, her jacket draped over her like a blanket. Milo was at his computer, typing quietly.

"Hey," Jason said.

Milo turned, relief flooding his face. "Hey yourself. How do you feel?"

"Like I got hit by a truck. But alive."

"That's more than we could say twelve hours ago." Milo came over, sat on the coffee table facing Jason. "Man, you scared us. Really scared us."

"I scared myself," Jason admitted. "That was... I didn't know it would be that hard."

"It shouldn't have been possible at all," Milo said. "I did some research while you were out. What you did - stabilizing a structure that size, redistributing forces in real-time - that's master-level work. Probably beyond master-level."

"RAE helped."

"Even with her help, it's impressive. And terrifying." Milo's expression grew serious. "Jason, we need to talk about limits. Real limits. Because if you'd died today - "

"I didn't."

"But you almost did. And we need to have a plan for situations where the heroic option is also the stupid option."

Jason nodded slowly. "You're right. We got lucky today. Next time we might not."

They talked for another hour. Establishing new protocols. Better planning. Ways to assess risk before jumping in.

When Lina woke up, they went through it all again. She had even more rules to add, born from her academy training and her instinct to protect.

By the end, they had something approaching a real framework. Not perfect. But better.

"One more thing," Lina said, looking at Jason seriously. "What you did today? That was brave. Stupid, but brave. And I'm glad those kids are safe."

"But?"

"But you need to understand something. You can't save everyone. And trying to will kill you. So from now on, we make smart choices, not heroic ones."

"Smart choices," Jason echoed. "I can do that."

"Can you?" Lina pressed. "Because when the moment comes, when people are in danger, you're going to want to help. That's who you are. But you need to remember: you're more use to the world alive than dead."

The words hit hard. Jason nodded. "I'll remember."

"Good." Lina stood, stretched. "Now, I need food. And you need to eat something that isn't hospital vending machine coffee."

They ordered takeout. Sat around Milo's small table. Ate in comfortable silence, the weight of what had happened settling into memory.

Jason's hands shook slightly when he lifted his fork. Aftershock. His body remembering.

But he was alive. And the kids were alive.

And that, for now, was enough.

*You did well today,* RAE said quietly, just for him.

**We did well.**

*Yes. But Lina is right. Next time, we plan better.*

**Agreed. No more improvised heroics.**

*Until the next emergency,* RAE said, and there was something like humor in her voice.

**Yeah. Until then.**

Jason smiled despite himself. Ate his food. Felt his body slowly remember how to be normal.

Tomorrow, they'd rest. The day after, they'd resume training.

But for now, this moment - safe, fed, surrounded by friends - was exactly what he needed.

Even if his head still felt like it had been used as a drum.

Small price to pay for a successful first crisis.

He just hoped the second one would be easier.

Somehow, he doubted it would be.
