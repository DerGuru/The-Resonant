## Part 4 - We Are Family | Ch. 11 - That requires more than good intentions

Return to the warehouse felt like stepping back into a life that had happened to someone else.

Everything looked the same - the training equipment, the resonance diagrams on the walls, the smell of Milo's coffee perpetually brewing in the corner. But we felt fundamentally changed.

Kira greeted us at the door, her expression cycling rapidly between relief and worry. "You're back. You're alive. Thank god. We heard reports but they were all classified and Milo couldn't tell us anything and - "

"We're fine," we said, though the word 'fine' felt like a lie. "Mostly. It's complicated."

Milo appeared from the planning room, his usual grin tempered by obvious concern. "Define complicated."

"Later," Lina said firmly. "First: How are things here?"

"Running," Milo said. "Not perfectly, but running. We're up to eight students now. Kira's been helping with beginner lessons. Elyra's been doing theory classes. Everyone's safe. No incidents."

"You did good," we told them both. "Really good."

But as we toured the facility, as Kira showed us the progress her students had made, as Elyra ran through the curriculum they'd developed, we felt something crystallizing in our mind.

This wasn't enough anymore.

We gathered for a team meeting that evening. Us, Lina, Milo, Elyra, Kira. The core group.

"Japan changed something," we began. "Not just in us, but in how we see what we're doing here. We're training people to use dangerous capabilities. And if we're going to do that responsibly - if we're going to prevent more things like Kage-Kōzō - we need to be more than just enthusiastic amateurs."

"What are you suggesting?" Elyra asked.

"Structure. Real structure. Not just ad-hoc training in a converted warehouse. Official facilities. Legal protection. Medical support. A sustainable model that can outlive any of us."

Milo cleared his throat. "Funny you should mention that. Malvek made an offer while you were gone."

He pulled up documents on his tablet - official proposals, legal frameworks, facility schematics. "Joint venture. He provides funding, facilities, legal protection, and regulatory oversight. You provide training methods, ethical frameworks, and operational independence."

"What's the catch?" Lina asked immediately.

"Transparency. Quarterly reviews. Coordination with other regulatory authorities. And..." Milo hesitated. "He wants veto power on certain decisions. Particularly anything involving hostile entities."

The room fell silent.

"That's a lot of control to give up," Kira said quietly.

"It is," Elyra agreed. "But it's also protection. Structure. The ability to actually scale what we're doing without running afoul of a dozen different laws."

We debated for hours. Independence versus security. Freedom versus resources. The value of staying small and autonomous versus the potential of building something that could actually change the field.

Finally, we spoke: "We accept. But with conditions."

We counted on our fingers: "One: We maintain full control over curriculum and teaching methods. Two: We get final say on student selection. Three: Malvek's veto power is limited to confirmed hostile entities - not suspected, not possible, but confirmed. Four: We can walk away if the partnership compromises our ethics."

"Think he'll agree to that?" Lina asked.

"He'd better," we said. "Or we stay in this warehouse and do it ourselves. Slower, smaller, but on our terms."

Milo grinned. "I'll send him the counter-proposal."

---

Two weeks later, we stood in the new facility.

Former industrial building in a cleaner part of town. Twenty thousand square feet. Proper training rooms with built-in safety systems. Dormitories for live-in students. Medical facilities with resonance-capable scanners. Conference rooms. Offices. Even a small cafeteria.

It was perfect. And terrifying.

"This is real now," Kira said, staring at the main training hall. "We're not just teaching a few people. We're building an institution."

"Yeah," we agreed. "We're building legacy now. And Elyra's right - that requires more than good intentions. It requires structure. Support. Systems that work even when we're not here."

Lina's hand found ours. "Scared?"

"Terrified," we admitted. "But also... ready. We've proven we can handle the hard stuff. Now we prove we can handle the boring stuff too. Paperwork. Regulations. Sustainable operations."

Milo laughed. "Never thought I'd hear you get excited about paperwork."

"We're not," we said. "But we are excited about what it enables. Twenty students by next month. Fifty by next year. Eventually? Hundreds. All trained with consent-based ethics. All understanding responsibility. All learning that power isn't about domination."

Elyra joined us, her cane tapping softly against the polished floor. Her integration had climbed to 28% now - enough that the constant pain had faded to manageable discomfort. Enough that she could teach full days without exhaustion.

"First official class starts Monday," she said. "Twelve students. Ages sixteen to thirty-five. Six different countries. This is happening."

We felt warmth in our consciousness - pride and anticipation mixing within ourselves.

*We're really doing this,* we thought.

*Yeah. We really are.*

And for the first time since Tokyo, the weight we'd been carrying felt a little lighter.

This was what the hard choices were for. This was why we'd fought. Why we'd destroyed Kage-Kōzō despite the cost.

To build something better. Something sustainable. Something that would help prevent the next hostile entity from ever being created.

Legacy indeed.
